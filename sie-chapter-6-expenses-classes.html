Chapter 6.4: Fund Expenses and Share Classes

Home/ SIE Study Materials/ Chapter 6.4

Expenses matter. A 1% difference in annual fees might seem trivial, but compounded over 30 years, it can mean the difference between retiring comfortably and working an extra decade. The fund industry has created multiple share classes offering different fee structures. Same fund, different costs.

12b-1 Fees: Paying to Advertise to Strangers

Here's something that would never fly in any other industry: you, the customer, pay for the company's advertising budget. Not indirectly through higher prices. Directly. As a line item. Every single year.

That's a 12b-1 fee.

When you own a mutual fund with a 12b-1 fee, a percentage of your investment funds TV commercials you'll never see, brochures you'll never read, and commissions to salespeople who sold the fund to people you'll never meet. You're essentially paying to recruit your own replacements.

How Did This Become Legal?

In 1980, the mutual fund industry had a problem: assets were shrinking. Their pitch to the SEC went something like this:

"Let us charge existing shareholders to market the fund. More investors means more assets, which means lower per-share expenses for everyone. It's a win-win."

The SEC agreed and created Rule 12b-1, expecting it to be a temporary measure to help struggling funds.

Forty-five years later, the "temporary" rule generates over $12 billion annually for the industry. The struggling fund rationale is long gone—12b-1 fees now appear on funds of all sizes, including the largest and most successful.

The Marketing Professional's Nightmare

If you've ever worked in advertising, this arrangement might make you twitch:

  • The "client" (shareholder) has zero input on creative direction, media strategy, or budget allocation
  • The "agency" (fund company) decides everything and bills automatically
  • The results benefit the fund company and distributors, not necessarily the shareholders funding the campaign
  • The contract renews forever unless the client actively opts out by selling their shares

Imagine pitching that deal to any other client. "You'll fund our marketing indefinitely, have no approval rights, and the primary beneficiary will be our sales team." You'd be laughed out of the room. Yet millions of investors agree to this daily without realizing it.

The Trail Commission Angle

Here's where 12b-1 fees get interesting for the exam—and for understanding conflicts of interest.

A portion of the 12b-1 fee often goes to your broker as a "trail commission" (also called a continuing commission). Your broker gets paid every year, automatically, just for having sold you the fund. Years ago. Once.

Think about the incentives this creates:

  • Broker recommends Fund A (0.25% 12b-1) vs. Fund B (0.75% 12b-1)
  • Fund B pays the broker 3x more annually for as long as you hold it
  • Both funds are "suitable" for your situation
  • Which one do you think gets recommended more often?

This isn't hypothetical. It's why regulators focus heavily on 12b-1 fee disclosure and suitability.

The Rules You Need to Know

RequirementLimit
Maximum 12b-1 fee1% of average annual net assets
Maximum for "no-load" funds0.25% (25 basis points)
Approval required fromShareholders, board, AND independent board members

The 0.25% threshold matters: any fund charging MORE than 0.25% in 12b-1 fees cannot call itself "no-load." This is the SEC's way of saying "if you're paying this much for distribution, you're effectively paying a load—just spread out over time."

The Compound Betrayal

A 0.50% annual 12b-1 fee on $100,000 invested for 20 years at 7% growth costs you roughly $25,000 in foregone returns. That's a new car. A year of college tuition. A very nice vacation every year for five years.

And where did that money go? Into advertising campaigns, sales commissions, and trail payments. You funded a marketing department that worked to bring in more investors—diluting your ownership while taking a cut of your returns.

Fees compound just like returns. Except in the wrong direction. And for someone else's benefit.

Exam Alert: The SIE loves testing the 1% maximum and the 0.25% no-load threshold. Also know that 12b-1 fees require approval from three parties: shareholders, the board of directors, AND the independent board members specifically. All three. Not just one.

Share Classes: A, B, and C

CLASS A (Front-End Load)

  • Higher up-front sales charge (max 8.5%)
  • Lower or no 12b-1 fees
  • Eligible for breakpoints
  • Best for: Long-term investors with significant assets

CLASS B (Back-End Load)

  • No up-front charge
  • Contingent Deferred Sales Charge (CDSC) if you sell early
  • Higher 12b-1 fees
  • Often converts to Class A after CDSC period (typically 5-7 years)
  • NOT eligible for breakpoints
  • Best for: Intermediate-term investors

CLASS C (Level Load)

  • No up-front charge (or minimal)
  • Small or no CDSC (typically 1% if sold within first year)
  • Higher 12b-1 fees that don't decline
  • No conversion to Class A
  • NOT eligible for breakpoints
  • Best for: Short-term investors (1-3 years)

Test Tip: The exam often presents scenarios asking which share class is appropriate. Key factors: investment time horizon and investment amount. Large, long-term investments = Class A.

Fund Expenses and Expense Ratios

Management Fee

  • Largest expense of most funds
  • Paid to the Investment Adviser
  • Based on assets under management—NOT performance

Redemption Fee

  • Charged when you sell shares
  • Different from CDSC — goes back to fund, not distributor
  • Payment must be received within 7 calendar days

Expense Ratio

Expense Ratio = Total Expenses / Total Net Assets
Fund TypeTypical Expense Ratio
Index funds0.03% - 0.20%
Actively managed0.50% - 1.50%
Specialty/sector1.00% - 2.00%+
Why Expense Ratios Matter

All performance is reported after expenses. A fund with a 1% expense ratio needs to beat its benchmark by 1% just to match it.

Key Points to Remember

  • 12b-1 fees: Max 1% for load funds, max 0.25% for no-load funds
  • Class A: Front-end load, lower 12b-1, breakpoints available
  • Class B: Back-end CDSC, higher 12b-1, converts to A
  • Class C: Level load, higher ongoing 12b-1, no conversion
  • Redemption payment: Within 7 calendar days
