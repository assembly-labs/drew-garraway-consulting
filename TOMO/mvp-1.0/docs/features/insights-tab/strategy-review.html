Insights Tab Strategy

FEAT-002 · Created March 30, 2026 · Status: Exploring

The Core Question

We have a full insights engine ready to deploy. The question isn't what to build -- it's how should insights show up in the user's experience?

  1. Where do insights live? Own tab vs. woven into existing screens
  2. What's the first impression? What does a user see on day one, day seven, day thirty?
  3. How do insights feel? Data dashboard vs. editorial narrative vs. conversational

UX Research Grounding

User states matter here

From the UX audit and persona research, TOMO users exist in two modes:

  • Exhausted (post-training): 90-second window, reduced cognition, voice-first logging. Insights are irrelevant in this state.
  • Relaxed (browsing): Couch, commute, rest day. Curious about progress. Open to depth. This is when insights deliver value.
Insights are a "relaxed state" feature. The user who opens TOMO on a rest day to see what the app noticed about their week -- that's the target moment.

What the personas want

Alex
White belt, 0-6mo
Needs validation that they're making progress. "You trained 3 times this week -- that's more than 80% of white belts at your stage" matters more than technique analysis. Risk: if insights are too technical or sparse, Alex feels like the app doesn't get them.
Jake
White belt, 6-18mo
Approaching blue belt. Wants to know their game is developing. "You've drilled half guard sweeps in 4 of your last 6 sessions -- that's becoming your system" is the insight that makes Jake feel seen. Most likely to churn if TOMO feels like a dumb log.
Marcus
Blue belt, plateau
Knows he's plateauing. Wants someone to see the patterns he can't. "Your submission rate dropped 20%, but your positional control improved -- that usually means you're working new positions. That's how games evolve." Shallow insights feel patronizing. Marcus needs analytical depth.
Sofia
Purple belt, grinder
Already tracks mentally. Looking for the blind spot. "You haven't touched your back game in 6 weeks -- every session has been guard-focused" is useful because Sofia was doing it unconsciously. Will stop checking if insights just confirm what she already knows.

Cold start is real

A new user has zero sessions. After one week they might have 2-4 sessions. The insights engine has minimum thresholds (1 session for weekly, 5 for monthly, 2 monthly reviews for quarterly). The first-time experience must handle this gracefully without making the tab feel empty or pointless.

The "show your training partner" moment

The strongest signal that insights are working: a user screenshots their weekly debrief and texts it to a training partner. This means the insight was specific enough to be interesting, personal enough to feel earned, and visual enough to be shareable. Every approach should be evaluated against this test.
Approach A

"The Debrief" -- Narrative-first, own tab

Insights live in a dedicated tab. The latest insight plays with a typewriter animation -- text reveals character by character, gold glow trailing behind, like TOMO is composing the observation in real time. Older insights collapse below. Chat follow-up slides in after the text finishes.

What the user sees

Day 1 (0 sessions)

A warm empty state. "Log your first session. After a week of training, your first debrief will appear here." No progress bars -- just a clear promise of what's coming and when.

Day 7 (3-4 sessions)

First weekly insight auto-plays with typewriter. The user watches TOMO compose: "Three sessions this week, all gi classes. You drilled closed guard sweeps twice -- that repetition is building your foundation..." Gold cursor pulsing. When it finishes, a chat input fades in.

Day 30 (12+ sessions)

Monthly review appears above the weekly feed. Tapping opens a full-page review with typewriter on first view. Names the user's developing game, evaluates consistency, flags concerns. Weekly insights reference the monthly context.

Day 90 (30+ sessions)

Quarterly assessment at the top. Feels like sitting down with a black belt. "Q1 2026: You went from a white belt who survived to one who hunts. Your half guard system is the foundation of a real game..." Weighty, strategic.

Strengths

  • Strongest "wow" moment. The typewriter animation creates anticipation. Users watch their insight being composed, which feels alive and personal.
  • Matches TOMO's voice-first identity. Users speak sessions into existence; TOMO speaks observations back.
  • The prototype already proved it. The web prototype validated this design language -- typewriter, glow, chat input.
  • Shareability is high. Gold glow on dark background is visually striking enough to screenshot.
  • Chat follow-up is natural. "Ask about this" feels like continuing a conversation.

Risks

  • Typewriter can feel slow. 200 words at 20ms/char = ~25 seconds. Need prominent skip and viewed-state tracking.
  • Tab may feel empty early on. Until the first weekly insight generates, it's an empty promise.
  • Engineering complexity. React Native glow effect needs adaptation. ~1,130 lines new/changed code.
  • Over-invests in presentation. If content quality is mediocre, fancy animation makes it worse -- like a beautifully animated horoscope.

Implementation: Medium-High   Estimated 1-2 sessions

Approach B

"Weekly Check-In" -- Focused, own tab, no animation

Ship the Insights tab with weekly insights only. No typewriter, no chat, no monthly/quarterly. Clean cards on a dark background. Most recent week at the top, older weeks below. Simple, fast, focused on proving that TOMO can say something useful.

What the user sees

Day 1 (0 sessions)

Same warm empty state as Approach A.

Day 7 (3-4 sessions)

A card appears with 2-3 observations. Each has a type badge (technique, consistency, sparring) and 1-2 sentences. Below: a "Focus Next Week" recommendation. No animation -- the content is just there.

Day 14 (7+ sessions)

Two weeks of cards. Newer week expanded, older collapsed. Users can compare weeks -- "Last week said focus on guard retention, this week says I drilled it 3 times. Progress."

Day 30+

Monthly and quarterly tiers are NOT shown. Added in a future update once weekly quality is validated.

Strengths

  • Fastest to ship. Use existing InsightsScreen as-is -- just wire the tab, deploy one edge function, add trigger. Could ship in a single session.
  • Lowest risk. One edge function (Haiku, cheapest model), minimal new code. Small blast radius if quality is poor.
  • Validates the core question first. Do users actually read weekly insights? Do they come back? Is the content useful? Real data beats design speculation.
  • Weekly cadence matches training rhythm. 2-4x/week training, weekly summary is the natural reflection point.

Risks

  • No "wow" factor. Static cards must stand on content alone. If Haiku output is generic, users check once and never return.
  • Underuses what's built. Monthly, quarterly, chat, typewriter, glow -- all sitting unused.
  • No chat means no depth. Users can't follow up on interesting observations.
  • Harder to communicate the vision. "We have a weekly summary" vs. "tiered coaching system with conversational follow-up."

Implementation: Low   Estimated ~0.5 sessions

Approach C

"The Feed" -- Insights woven into Journal

Don't add a separate tab. Instead, insights appear as special cards in the Journal feed alongside session entries. A weekly debrief card appears at the end of each week. Monthly reviews at month boundaries. The Journal becomes a combined timeline of training and reflection.

What the user sees

Day 1 (0 sessions)

Normal Journal empty state. No mention of insights -- the user just starts logging.

Day 7 (3-4 sessions)

Scrolling through the Journal, a new card type appears: "Weekly Debrief - Mar 24-30". Gold left border, sparkle icon, different visual weight than session cards. Tapping opens the full debrief.

Day 30 (12+ sessions)

The Journal now has a rhythm: sessions interspersed with weekly debriefs, a "Monthly Check-In" card at month boundaries. The feed tells the full story.

Day 90 (30+ sessions)

A "Quarterly Review" card appears -- the most visually prominent card type. The Journal has become a living document of training and growth.

Strengths

  • Zero navigation change. No new tab needed. Insights appear where users already look.
  • Natural context. Weekly debrief sits right next to the sessions that informed it.
  • Solves the "empty tab" problem. No empty state to manage. Insights scale naturally.
  • Engagement through existing habit. Users check Journal after logging. Insights catch them there.

Risks

  • Insights get buried. 5 sessions in a week = debrief is 5 items down. Users who don't scroll will miss it.
  • No dedicated home. Finding a monthly review from 2 months ago means scrolling through dozens of entries.
  • Feed gets cluttered. Mixing session logs, debriefs, monthly reviews, and quarterlies creates visual noise.
  • Chat follow-up is awkward. Where does the conversation live? Inline? Modal? No clean answer.
  • Harder to extend later. Moving to a dedicated tab later breaks the pattern users learned.
  • Screenshot test fails. Insights surrounded by session entries aren't shareable.

Implementation: Medium   Estimated 1-2 sessions (touches more existing code)

Recommendation

Start with B, graduate to A

Ship the Weekly Check-In first. Validate that the content resonates. Then layer on the typewriter animation, monthly/quarterly tiers, and chat as the earned graduation.

Why not C (The Feed)

It solves discovery but creates bigger problems -- buried insights, cluttered feed, no home for chat, harder to extend. It also means decomposing the existing InsightsScreen into embeddable components, which is more engineering work than just wiring what's built. And the screenshot test fails.

Why not A (The Debrief) first

The typewriter animation is impressive, but it's presentation. If the underlying Haiku output is generic or unhelpful, the animation makes it worse. We'd be polishing a feature we haven't validated. The React Native port also adds real complexity that delays the first user touchpoint.

Why B first

B answers the most important question: does TOMO's weekly insight content actually resonate with users?

The existing InsightsScreen already renders weekly cards with type badges, titles, body text, and focus recommendations. It handles empty states, pull-to-refresh, and mark-as-viewed. Wiring it to the tab bar, deploying the weekly edge function, and adding a trigger is roughly half a session of work.

Once it ships, we watch:

  • Do users open the Insights tab more than once?
  • Do they open it on non-training days (the "rest day check-in" behavior)?
  • Which insight types (technique, consistency, sparring, milestone) get the most views?
  • Do users screenshot/share?
  • What do testers say about content quality?

If weekly insights land well, Approach A becomes the graduation. If they don't, we iterate on content quality before investing in animation and chat.
