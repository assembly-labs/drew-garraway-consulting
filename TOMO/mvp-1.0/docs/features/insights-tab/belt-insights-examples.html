Insights by Belt Level

Content examples showing how the same training data produces different insights for white belts vs. blue belts

The core rule: White belts need to hear that showing up IS progress. Blue belts need to hear that their techniques are starting to connect. Same data, completely different framing.

Every example below uses realistic session data. Read each scenario's context line to understand what the user logged that week, then see how the insight adapts.

Blue Belt

Blue Belt Blues. 50-60% quit before purple. Name what's developing. Connect techniques to each other. Treat them like a peer.

Week 4 at Blue · Game Forming
4 sessions, 2 gi / 2 nogi. Drilled SLX, heel hook entries, k-guard. 12 sparring rounds, 3 heel hooks given, 2 guillotines received. 190 sessions total.
Insights
Based on 190 sessions
Apr 7 - 13
TechniqueNEW
Leg Lock Chain Is Forming
SLX into heel hook entries, with k-guard as the backup when the finish isn't there. Three heel hooks in sparring says the reps are translating. These techniques are starting to talk to each other.
Sparring
Guillotine Problem
Caught by guillotines twice this week. Your head position entering on guard pulls or shots might be the common thread -- worth paying attention to where your head is right before you get caught.
Consistency
Even Split
2 gi, 2 nogi. The nogi sessions are where your leg lock game gets reps. The gi sessions are forcing you to solve different problems. Both matter.
Focus Next Week
Spend 5 minutes each open mat drilling guillotine defense from the positions where you keep getting caught. You already know which ones.

Same Data, Different Belt -- Side by Side

Both users trained 3 times this week, drilled guard techniques, sparred 4 rounds, and got submitted twice. Here's how the insight differs:

White Belt (Session #18)

Technique Insight
"Closed guard and hip escapes again this week. The repetition is the point -- your body is memorizing these movements so they'll be there when you need them in sparring."
"Your guard system is developing with a clear closed guard emphasis."
Sparring Insight
"Got caught by an armbar and a collar choke. Those are two of the most common submissions at every level. Now you know what they feel like -- that's step one of learning to defend them."
"Submission ratio is 0:2 this week, down from last week."
Focus Next
"When you feel that armbar grip tighten, try pulling your elbow toward your hip before they extend. You'll get it wrong at first -- that's fine."
"Work on your armbar defense positioning and submission prevention strategy."

Blue Belt (Session #195)

Technique Insight
"Third straight week of guard work. Your open guard and closed guard are getting equal reps, which is unusual -- most blue belts lean one way. That versatility is worth keeping."
"Great job drilling guard techniques this week! Keep it up!"
Sparring Insight
"Armbar and collar choke caught you this week. Both are top-position finishes -- your guard retention might be the leak. Were you getting passed first, or were they finishing from inside your guard?"
"Don't worry about getting submitted! It happens to everyone!"
Focus Next
"Pay attention to the 5 seconds before each submission catch. If you're getting passed first, that's a guard retention issue. If they're finishing from inside your guard, your frame management needs work."
"Keep working hard and you'll see improvement soon!"
Key Differences

White belt: Names the technique, normalizes getting caught, gives a physical cue they can try tomorrow. Never quantifies the submission ratio. Never uses "system" or "game."

Blue belt: Looks at the same data analytically. Asks diagnostic questions. Assumes they can identify the position where things went wrong. Challenges them to investigate, doesn't just reassure.

The red strikethrough text shows what the wrong tone looks like for each belt. White belts don't need "system" language. Blue belts don't need cheerleading.
