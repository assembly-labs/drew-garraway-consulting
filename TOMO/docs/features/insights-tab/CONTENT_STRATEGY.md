# FEAT-002: Insights Content Strategy -- White Belt & Blue Belt

**Created:** 2026-03-30
**Focus:** What insights say, not how they look. Belt-specific content rules grounded in dropout research and BJJ psychology.

---

## Why Content Matters More Than Presentation

The Approach B decision (static cards, no animation) means content is the entire experience. There's no typewriter to create anticipation, no glow to make text feel alive. The words on the card have to earn the user's attention on their own.

If the insight says "You trained 3 times this week. Good consistency." -- the user checks once and never returns. If it says "Three sessions, all guard work. The hip escapes are starting to show up in your sparring -- you recovered guard twice on Saturday after getting passed. That doesn't happen by accident." -- they screenshot it.

---

## The Two Systems

### System 1: White Belt Insights ("The Encouraging Training Partner")

**Psychological reality:** 70-90% of white belts quit before blue belt. Peak dropout is months 3-6. These users are in survival mode -- physically exhausted, ego-bruised, confused about positions, and asking themselves "am I even getting better?" every week.

**The insight's job:** Make invisible progress visible. Normalize struggle. Celebrate consistency. Never overwhelm with technical analysis they can't contextualize.

**Voice:** A slightly more experienced training partner who started a year or two before them. Warm but not patronizing. Says things like "that's how it works" and "everyone goes through this" -- not "great job!" or "you're crushing it!"

**What white belts need to hear:**

| Need | Why | Example insight |
|------|-----|-----------------|
| **Progress is happening** | They can't feel it. Getting tapped every round makes them think nothing is changing. | "You drilled hip escapes three times this week. Last month you weren't logging any specific techniques -- the fact that you're recognizing what you're working on is itself progress." |
| **Consistency matters most** | They think skill matters. At white belt, showing up IS the skill. | "Three weeks in a row at 3 sessions each. At this point, you're training more consistently than most people who've been at it twice as long." |
| **Getting tapped is learning** | Every submission received feels like failure. It's actually data. | "Caught by armbars twice this week. That's your body learning what positions are dangerous -- next time you'll feel the danger a half-second earlier." |
| **The basics are the game** | They want to learn the flashy stuff. The fundamentals are what save them. | "Hip escapes, guard recovery, posture in closed guard -- that's not boring beginner stuff. Every black belt drills these. You're building the floor everything else stands on." |
| **Rest days count** | Many overtrain early because they think more = better. | "Four sessions this week, up from three. That's solid -- but make sure you're giving your body at least one full rest day. Longevity beats intensity." |

**What white belts should NOT hear:**
- "Your system is developing" -- they don't have a system. They have survival instincts.
- "Your game identity is..." -- they don't have a game. They have techniques they've seen once.
- "Submission ratio trends" -- they're getting submitted 10:1. Don't quantify it.
- "Strategic gaps" -- everything is a gap. Naming them is overwhelming.
- "Competition readiness" -- they're not competing. They're trying not to quit.

#### White Belt Sub-Tier: 3-4 Stripes (Approaching Blue)

These users are special. They survived the filter that kills 70-90% of practitioners. They've been training 12-18 months, have 80-150+ sessions, and are starting to experience moments of competence. They deserve acknowledgment that something has shifted.

**What's different about them:**
- They recognize positions now -- they know when they're in half guard vs. side control
- They've had their first successful sweeps and maybe submissions in live rolling
- They're starting to have "go-to" techniques (even if they can't name them)
- They're less lost, but also more frustrated because they can now see how much they don't know
- Pre-promotion anxiety: "Am I good enough for blue?" or "Will I ever get promoted?"

**Insight tone shift:** Still warm, still encouraging, but now acknowledge emerging competence. Start naming patterns without calling them "systems." Use language like "starting to see" and "becoming familiar" rather than "your game" or "your system."

**Example insights for 3-4 stripe white belt:**

"You've drilled half guard underhook in 4 of your last 6 sessions. Whether you chose it or ended up there, half guard is becoming a place you know. That's not nothing -- most people at your stage are still in survival mode."

"First sweep in live rolling this week. That's a real moment -- your body did something your brain has been trying to teach it for months. It won't be consistent yet, but the pathway is there now."

"120 sessions total. You've outlasted 80% of the people who started when you did. The fact that you're still showing up -- and still logging -- says something about where this is going."

### System 2: Blue Belt Insights ("The Honest Training Partner")

**Psychological reality:** 50-60% of blue belts quit before purple. The "Blue Belt Blues" is the most documented psychological crisis in BJJ. They just achieved their first major goal, but nothing changed overnight. White belts target them. Upper belts stop going easy. The 2-4 year road to purple feels endless. Accumulated injuries start limiting training.

**The insight's job:** Name what's developing. Connect techniques to each other. Acknowledge the plateau honestly. Flag blind spots they can't see. Treat them like a peer, not a student.

**Voice:** A training partner at the same level or slightly above. Speaks like a peer -- direct, observant, occasionally blunt. Can name patterns and connections. Doesn't sugarcoat but doesn't pile on either.

**What blue belts need to hear:**

| Need | Why | Example insight |
|------|-----|-----------------|
| **Your techniques connect** | They drill in isolation. Insights should draw lines between techniques. | "Single leg X into heel hook entry, then back to K-guard when the finish isn't there. That's three moves that chain together. You're not just collecting techniques -- they're starting to talk to each other." |
| **The plateau is real and normal** | They think they're uniquely stuck. The plateau is universal at blue. | "Same submission count as last month, similar technique spread. This is the blue belt plateau -- the learning curve flattens, but you're still improving. You just can't feel it week to week." |
| **Your vulnerabilities are data** | Getting caught repeatedly in the same submission feels like failure. It's a diagnostic. | "Guillotined twice this week, third time this month. Your head position on guard pulls might be the common thread. Worth paying attention to where your head is when you enter." |
| **Game identity is forming** | They have preferences but haven't named them. Name it for them. | "Three weeks of heavy guard work, mostly gi. You're spending more time on bottom than top -- that's not a weakness, it's a preference forming. Guard players build from here." |
| **Injuries need attention** | Blue belts push through injuries because they don't want to lose momentum. | "Second week mentioning that knee. Training through joint pain is how 6-week injuries become 6-month injuries. Talk to your coach about modifications." |

**What blue belts should NOT hear:**
- "Great job showing up!" -- patronizing at this level. They've been showing up for 2+ years.
- "You're building a foundation" -- they're past foundation. They're building a house.
- Unqualified positivity about getting submitted -- at blue, getting caught by white belts stings differently. Acknowledge it without dismissing it.
- "Your system is..." -- only use "system" language for blue belts who are 2+ stripes with clear patterns. Early blue belts are still exploring.
- Comparisons to white belt progress -- they know they're past that. Don't regress the tone.

## Insight Type Calibration by Belt

Each weekly insight has a `type` field. The emphasis and frequency should shift by belt:

| Insight Type | White Belt Frequency | White Belt Frame | Blue Belt Frequency | Blue Belt Frame |
|-------------|---------------------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| **technique** | Medium | "You drilled X" -- name it, normalize it, connect it to one other thing | High | "X connects to Y" -- draw lines between techniques, name what's developing |
| **consistency** | High | "You showed up X times" -- this IS the progress | Medium | "Your pattern is..." -- frame as data, not cheerleading |
| **sparring** | Low | Only if they logged sparring. Frame submissions received as learning. Never quantify the ratio. | High | Submissions given AND received. Patterns in what catches them. Head-to-head trends. |
| **risk** | Medium | Injuries only. Simple language. Always defer to coach. | Medium | Injuries + overtraining + plateau signals. More analytical. |
| **milestone** | High | Celebrate early and often (10, 25, 50 sessions). Normalize the journey. | Low | Only major milestones (100, 200, 1-year at belt). Don't patronize. |

## Focus Next Calibration

The `focusNext` field is the single most actionable line in the insight. It should differ dramatically by belt:

**White Belt Focus Next Rules:**
- Always concrete and physical -- a position, a movement, a situation to notice
- Never a "strategy" or "game plan"
- Preferably something they can do in their next session without asking anyone
- Examples:
  - "Next time you're in bottom side control, focus on getting to your side before anything else. That's the first escape."
  - "When you spar this week, count how many times you get back to guard after being passed. That number is your real progress metric."
  - "Try drilling that hip escape 5 extra times after class. The reps compound faster than you think."

**Blue Belt Focus Next Rules:**
- Can reference a technique by name and assume they know what it means
- Can suggest a training lens ("this week, notice when...")
- Can connect to a weakness pattern from the insight
- Examples:
  - "Spend 5 minutes on guillotine defense from the positions where you keep getting caught. You already know which ones."
  - "Next open mat, start every roll from half guard bottom. Repetition in that position will tell you what your game wants to be."
  - "Your guard passing hasn't shown up in two weeks. If that's intentional, fine -- but if not, throw in some passing rounds this week."

## Cold Start Content (First 4 Weeks)

The first weekly insight a user ever receives sets the tone for whether they ever check the tab again. Here's how it should read by belt:

### White Belt, Week 1 (1-3 sessions)

The user has just started. They may not have logged techniques or sparring details. The insight has to work with minimal data.

**Good:**
"First week in the books. [2/3] sessions logged. The hardest part of this whole thing is walking through that door -- and you did it [multiple times / twice / once]. Everything else builds from here."

**Bad:**
"You trained 2 times this week. Your target is 3. Try to hit your goal next week." (Feels like a fitness app scolding them.)

### Blue Belt, Week 1 (2-4 sessions)

The user has been training for years but just started using TOMO. They have expertise but no data in the app. The insight should respect their experience while being honest about what TOMO can see so far.

**Good:**
"First week tracked. [4] sessions, [12] sparring rounds, mostly guard work. TOMO can't see your whole game yet, but the patterns will get sharper as the data builds. For now: you're logging at [purple belt frequency] -- whatever you've been doing is working."

**Bad:**
"Welcome to TOMO! Great job logging your first sessions! Keep it up!" (Patronizing for someone with 200+ sessions in their body.)

## Quality Checks (Pre-Generation Filters)

Before any insight is shown to the user, validate:

1. **Does it say something specific?** If you could swap any user's name into the insight and it would still read the same, it's too generic. Reject it.
2. **Does the belt tone match?** White belt insight shouldn't use "system," "game identity," "strategic." Blue belt insight shouldn't use "great job showing up" or "you're building a foundation."
3. **Does Focus Next pass the "can they do it tomorrow" test?** If the recommendation requires context the user doesn't have, it's useless.
4. **Is there a submission ratio for white belts?** Remove it. Never quantify white belt submission ratios.
5. **Does it mention injury without deferring?** Every injury mention must end with "talk to your coach" or equivalent.
