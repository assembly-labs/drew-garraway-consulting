# Doing the Wrong Thing and What Happens Next: Ethics and Remedies

## Podcast Script: Two Hosts Break Down Chapters 3 and 8

**Coverage:** Ethical Practices and Obligations (25%) + Remedies and Administrative Provisions (11%)
**Combined exam weight:** 36%
**Target length:** 25 to 30 minutes of audio (roughly 4,500 to 5,000 words spoken)
**Tone:** Conversational, precise. Two people working through dense material without making it boring.

---

**HOST A:** Last episode we walked through who registers and where. Three chapters, one question. This episode is the sequel. What are the rules of conduct you have to follow once you are registered, and what happens to the people who break them?

**HOST B:** Thirty-six percent of the exam. Domain 3 is Ethics, twenty-five percent, the single biggest chapter on the Series 63. Domain 8 is Remedies, eleven percent. Together they are a little over one out of every three questions.

**HOST A:** And there is one idea that ties both chapters together. It is the most quoted sentence in NASAA commentary, and it is the reason none of the registration exemptions we talked about in Episode One actually save you if you lie about the product.

**HOST B:** Section 101. Antifraud. Let's start there.

---

## SEGMENT 1: Section 101 and the Rule With No Exemptions

**HOST A:** Section 101 of the Uniform Securities Act is the state-law equivalent of SEC Rule 10b-5. It says it is unlawful for any person, in connection with the offer, sale, or purchase of any security, to employ any device, scheme, or artifice to defraud, to make a material misstatement, or to engage in any practice that operates as a fraud.

**HOST B:** The verbs matter. Offer, sale, or purchase. Not just sales. An offer alone can trigger Section 101. A purchase can trigger it. The rule attaches the moment you start talking to someone about a security.

**HOST A:** And here is the headline fact. The NASAA commentary says, and this is a direct quote, "There are no exemptions from Section 101."

**HOST B:** A security can be exempt from registration. A transaction can be exempt from registration. None of that matters for antifraud. A U.S. Treasury bond is exempt from registration under Section 402(a)(1). If you sell one with a material misstatement, you have committed fraud under Section 101, and the state Administrator can come after you.

**HOST A:** The memory hook every Series 63 student should have burned into their brain: "Exempt from registration. Not exempt from fraud."

**HOST B:** Section 102 is the investment adviser version of the same rule. Same idea. No exemptions. An adviser who is excluded from the IA definition entirely under Section 401(f) can still be sued for fraud under Section 102. A federal covered adviser registered with the SEC is still subject to Section 102. The Investment Advisers Act at 203A(b)(2) expressly preserves that state antifraud power.

**HOST A:** Registration authority is one track. Antifraud authority is a separate track. The states keep antifraud authority over everyone, no matter what.

**HOST B:** That is the one idea you need to carry from Chapter 8 into Chapter 3. Because everything in the ethics chapter is built on top of it.

---

## SEGMENT 2: The NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest Practices

**HOST A:** Chapter 3, Section 1. The NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest or Unethical Business Practices of Broker-Dealers and Agents. This is the big document. NASAA first adopted it on May 23, 1983, and the current version was amended on April 7, 2025.

**HOST B:** It lists twenty-five prohibited practices for broker-dealer firms, and six prohibited practices for agents. Most of the ethics questions on the Series 63 trace back to this one document.

**HOST A:** The opening standard is straightforward. "Each broker-dealer and agent shall observe high standards of commercial honor and just and equitable principles of trade in the conduct of their business."

**HOST B:** Let's walk through the ones that get tested most often. Churning, in Section 1.b, is defined as inducing trading in a customer's account that is excessive in size or frequency given the customer's financial situation. Notice the test. It is not a hard count. It is proportional to the customer. A retired widow with fifty thousand dollars in assets does not need forty trades a month.

**HOST A:** Suitability, Section 1.c, requires reasonable grounds for a recommendation based on the customer's objectives, situation, needs, and known information. And Section 1.d is the new best-interest obligation for retail customers, which cross-references Regulation Best Interest. We will come back to Reg BI in a few minutes.

---

## SEGMENT 3: The Brand New "Adviser" Title Rule

**HOST B:** Here is something new that a lot of older prep materials do not cover yet. Section 1.e was added on April 7, 2025. It prohibits a broker-dealer agent from using a title, a purported credential, or a professional designation containing any variant of "adviser" or "advisor" unless the agent is actually licensed as an investment adviser or an investment adviser representative.

**HOST A:** This rule exists because of a decade of business cards that read "Senior Financial Advisor" on people who were legally salespeople. Customers kept assuming the word meant something. NASAA decided it should.

**HOST B:** So if a Series 63 question describes a broker-dealer agent handing out a card that says "Senior Financial Adviser" and that agent is not separately IAR-licensed, the conduct is prohibited under Section 1.e. Brand new. Often missing from older prep material. Easy to miss.

---

## SEGMENT 4: Discretion, BD Versus IA

**HOST A:** Section 1.g is discretionary authority for broker-dealers. A BD cannot exercise discretion without first obtaining written discretionary authority from the customer, unless the discretion is limited to time or price for executing an order.

**HOST B:** And this is where a major distinction shows up. The BD rule requires written authority before the first discretionary trade. The investment adviser rule, under NASAA Model Rule 102(a)(4)-1, gives investment advisers a ten-business-day window. An IA can exercise oral discretion for up to ten business days, and must get written authority before that window closes.

**HOST A:** So if the fact pattern describes a broker-dealer agent placing a discretionary trade based on an oral instruction from that morning, the trade is unauthorized. If the fact pattern describes an investment adviser doing the same thing and getting written authority within ten business days, the trade is fine.

**HOST B:** The exam tests this constantly. Ten business days means investment adviser. Written before the trade means broker-dealer. Do not flip them.

---

## SEGMENT 5: The Six Agent-Specific Prohibitions

**HOST A:** The NASAA statement lists six specific agent prohibitions in Section 2. Section 2.a is borrowing or lending money or securities with customers. For broker-dealer agents, this is a flat prohibition. No exceptions.

**HOST B:** Compare that to the investment adviser rule in NASAA Model Rule 102(a)(4)-1, which has narrow carve-outs. An IA may borrow from a client if the client is a broker-dealer, an affiliate, or a financial institution in the lending business. Those carve-outs do not exist for broker-dealer agents.

**HOST A:** So the classic trap is a question that says, "A broker-dealer agent may borrow from a customer if the customer is a bank." That answer is wrong. That carve-out is the IA rule, not the BD rule. The BD rule has no bank exception.

**HOST B:** Section 2.b is selling away. Effecting transactions that are not recorded on the firm's books, unless the firm authorized them in writing before execution. Section 2.c is fictitious accounts. And Section 2.d is sharing in profits or losses in a customer account.

**HOST A:** The sharing rule requires written authorization from both the customer and the firm. Notice what the NASAA rule does not require. Proportionality. There is a separate FINRA rule, Rule 2150, that says the agent's share must be proportional to the agent's contribution. That is a FINRA rule. It is not the NASAA rule.

**HOST B:** If a question offers an answer choice that mentions "proportional to contribution" in the context of the NASAA rule, that is a trap. The NASAA rule just requires written consent from both parties.

---

## SEGMENT 6: The No-Load 0.25% Rule

**HOST A:** Let's shift to the NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest Practices in Connection with Investment Company Shares. Adopted in 1997. Shorter document. Contains the single most-tested specific number in the ethics domain.

**HOST B:** Section A.2. A fund cannot be described as "no load" or "no sales charge" if any of the following are present. A front-end load. A contingent deferred sales load. A Rule 12b-1 fee or service fee that in total exceeds zero point two five percent of average net fund assets per year.

**HOST A:** Twenty-five basis points. One quarter of one percent. Not half a percent. Not one percent. Twenty-five basis points and one basis point higher, and the fund cannot legally be called "no load."

**HOST B:** Memorize it. 0.25%. It will be on the exam.

---

## SEGMENT 7: The Vulnerable Adults Model Act

**HOST A:** Now one of the most heavily tested specific documents in all of Domain 3. The NASAA Model Act to Protect Vulnerable Adults from Financial Exploitation. Adopted January 22, 2016.

**HOST B:** The act was drafted after state regulators spent years watching the same pattern repeat. A caregiver, a relative, sometimes a legitimate-seeming "financial consultant," would gain control over a senior's brokerage account and drain it. By the time a regulator found out, the money was gone. The model act gave firms a legal framework to pause, report, and delay suspicious disbursements before the money left the account.

**HOST A:** Start with the definitions. An eligible adult is a person 65 years of age or older, or a person subject to the state's Adult Protective Services statute. The number is 65. Not 60. Not 70. Same number as Medicare eligibility.

**HOST B:** A qualified individual is any agent, IAR, or person in a supervisory, compliance, or legal capacity at a broker-dealer or investment adviser. Financial exploitation is the wrongful or unauthorized taking or use of money or property, including conduct using a power of attorney or guardianship obtained through deception or undue influence.

**HOST A:** Here is the core rule. Reporting is mandatory. Delay is permissive. If a qualified individual reasonably believes financial exploitation may have occurred, the individual shall promptly notify Adult Protective Services and the state securities commissioner. The statute uses "shall."

**HOST B:** Delay is different. A broker-dealer or investment adviser may delay a requested disbursement if the firm reasonably believes the disbursement may result in exploitation. The statute uses "may," not "shall." You have to report. You can delay.

**HOST A:** And now the four numbers to memorize. Two, seven, fifteen, twenty-five.

**HOST B:** Within two business days of starting a delay, the firm must give written notification to all authorized parties on the account, unless one of them is the suspect, and also notify APS and the commissioner. Within seven business days, the firm must report internal investigation results to the agencies. The maximum initial disbursement delay is fifteen business days. And with an agency-requested extension, the maximum total delay is twenty-five business days.

**HOST A:** Two, seven, fifteen, twenty-five. Every exam that covers this section tests at least one of those four numbers.

**HOST B:** One more piece. Good-faith reports and good-faith delays carry immunity from administrative and civil liability under Sections 4 and 8. If the firm reports in good faith and it turns out to be nothing, the firm is not liable for making the report.

---

## SEGMENT 8: Regulation Best Interest and the DOL Rule That Died

**HOST A:** Now we get to one of the bigger historical moments on the exam. Regulation Best Interest. Rule 15l-1. Adopted by the SEC in June 2019.

**HOST B:** The backstory matters. In April 2016, the Department of Labor issued a fiduciary rule that said anyone giving retirement investment advice had to act solely in the client's best interest. The rule would have applied a strict fiduciary standard to all brokers and advisers handling retirement accounts.

**HOST A:** The financial services industry sued almost immediately. The case worked its way through the federal courts for two years. In March 2018, the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals vacated the DOL rule in Chamber of Commerce versus U.S. Department of Labor. The fiduciary standard for brokers died in a Louisiana federal courtroom.

**HOST B:** The SEC stepped into the vacuum about a year later. In June 2019, the SEC adopted Regulation Best Interest. It is not a fiduciary standard. It is not the old suitability standard either. It is something in between, and it is what controls how a broker-dealer talks to a retail customer today.

**HOST A:** Reg BI applies only to retail customers. A retail customer is a natural person, or a legal representative of one, who receives a recommendation and uses it primarily for personal, family, or household purposes. For institutional customers, FINRA Rule 2111 still applies.

**HOST B:** The general obligation is that the broker-dealer must act in the best interest of the retail customer at the time the recommendation is made, without placing the financial or other interest of the broker ahead of the customer.

**HOST A:** And the rule has four component obligations. The memory hook is D-C-C-C. Disclosure, Care, Conflict, Compliance.

**HOST B:** Disclosure obligation. Before or at the time of the recommendation, disclose in writing all material facts about the scope of the relationship, the material fees, the services, and all material conflicts of interest.

**HOST A:** Care obligation. Exercise reasonable diligence and skill. Understand the risks, rewards, and costs. Have a reasonable basis to believe the recommendation is in the customer's best interest. Consider reasonable alternatives. And this is important, evaluated as of the time of the recommendation, not in hindsight.

**HOST B:** Conflict of interest obligation. Written policies and procedures to identify all conflicts, and at minimum disclose or eliminate them. Mitigate conflicts that incentivize associated persons to put their own interests ahead of the customer. And here is a key piece, identify and eliminate, not just disclose, sales contests, sales quotas, bonuses, and non-cash compensation based on the sale of specific securities or specific types of securities within a limited period.

**HOST A:** Compliance obligation. Written policies and procedures reasonably designed to achieve compliance with Reg BI as a whole.

**HOST B:** D-C-C-C. Disclosure, Care, Conflict, Compliance. Burn it in.

---

## SEGMENT 9: The 5% Policy Is a Guide, Not a Rule

**HOST A:** One more Chapter 3 item before we move on to remedies. FINRA Rule 2121. The 5% Policy.

**HOST B:** It is not a rule. It is a guide. Adopted by the NASD in 1943 as a guideline for evaluating markups on principal transactions. It has survived eighty years because nobody has come up with a better way to express "a reasonable markup." It has also survived eighty years of prep providers misreading it as a hard ceiling.

**HOST A:** The rule itself, if you read the FINRA supplementary material, explicitly says a markup pattern of 5% or even less may be unfair or unreasonable depending on the circumstances. So a 3% markup can still be excessive. And disclosure of the markup does not cure an otherwise unfair markup. Those two traps show up on the exam a lot.

**HOST B:** The policy lists seven factors for determining a fair markup. Type of security. Availability. Price. Amount of money involved. Disclosure, which as we just said does not cure an excessive markup. Pattern of markups. And nature of the member's business.

**HOST A:** If a Series 63 question treats 5% as a bright-line ceiling, that answer is wrong. It is a guide, not a rule.

---

## SEGMENT 10: The Uniform Prudent Investor Act and 164 Years of Precedent

**HOST B:** Let's close out Chapter 3 with the Uniform Prudent Investor Act. Drafted in 1994 by the Uniform Law Commission. It applies to trustees and fiduciaries, and it applies to investment advisers acting in a fiduciary capacity.

**HOST A:** The backstory here goes way back. For 164 years, American trustees operated under a rule from a Massachusetts case called Harvard College versus Amory, decided in 1830. The court told trustees to observe how "men of prudence, discretion, and intelligence manage their own affairs." At the time, that was a progressive statement. It gave trustees flexibility.

**HOST B:** Over the next century and a half, courts turned it into the opposite. Judges started reading the "prudent man" rule as a categorical prohibition on anything speculative. Speculative eventually came to mean anything that wasn't a government bond or a blue-chip stock. Trustees were locked into a very narrow set of investments.

**HOST A:** Meanwhile, Harry Markowitz won a Nobel Prize in Economics for showing that a diversified portfolio of so-called speculative assets was often less risky than a concentrated portfolio of so-called safe ones. Modern Portfolio Theory was born in 1952. But it did not make it into trust law for another forty-two years.

**HOST B:** That is what the Uniform Prudent Investor Act did in 1994. It formally adopted Modern Portfolio Theory into American trust law. Its core principles are portfolio-level evaluation, risk and return managed together, no categorical restrictions on any investment type, diversification required unless special circumstances say otherwise, delegation permitted with reasonable care, duty of loyalty solely to the beneficiaries, and ex ante measurement, meaning compliance is judged at the time of the decision and not in hindsight.

**HOST A:** UPIA shows up on the Series 63 because NASAA lists it as testable in the scope statement for Domain 3. You are not going to get a lot of UPIA questions, but know the core principles.

---

## SEGMENT 11: Administrator Powers and Limits

**HOST B:** OK, switch to Chapter 8. Domain 8 is the Administrator's toolbox. Eleven percent of the exam. Seven questions. Three layers of consequences. Administrative, civil, and criminal.

**HOST A:** Let's start with what the state Administrator can and cannot do. The Administrator has broad authority, but the authority has hard edges.

**HOST B:** What the Administrator can do. Deny, suspend, or revoke a registration under Section 204 or Section 306. Bar an individual from association. Cancel a registration for non-punitive reasons like death or dissolution. Issue cease and desist orders under Section 408. Make rules, forms, and orders. Conduct investigations, public or private, inside or outside the state, under Section 407. Subpoena witnesses and compel production of records. Apply to a court for an injunction. And refer criminal cases to the Attorney General under Section 409.

**HOST A:** That is a lot of power. Here is what the Administrator cannot do directly. The Administrator cannot directly levy fines. And the Administrator cannot imprison anyone. Both of those require a court.

**HOST B:** This is a favorite trap on the Series 63. Any question that says "the Administrator fined the firm fifty thousand dollars" is probably wrong unless the question also describes a court-ordered consequence. The Administrator can bar, suspend, revoke, cease and desist, and refer to prosecutors. The Administrator can do almost everything except levy fines and put people in jail, which are of course the two things you would most want the Administrator to do to someone who defrauded your grandmother.

**HOST A:** The memory hook is "administrative actions stay administrative. Money and jail require a judge."

---

## SEGMENT 12: Section 409 Criminal Penalties

**HOST B:** Section 409 is criminal penalties. Here is the verbatim language. "Any person who willfully violates any provision of this act except Section 404, or who willfully violates any rule or order under this act, shall upon conviction be fined not more than five thousand dollars or imprisoned not more than three years, or both."

**HOST A:** Five thousand dollars maximum fine. Three years maximum prison. Willful violation required. Negligence is not enough.

**HOST B:** And there is an interesting historical quirk here. The five thousand dollar cap has not changed since 1956. In 1956 dollars, five thousand was roughly fifty-eight thousand in 2026 purchasing power. It was a meaningful fine at the time. Seven decades of inflation later, it is not. Most states have since passed their own blue sky criminal statutes with modern penalty levels. But the Series 63 still tests the 1956 number.

**HOST A:** Five thousand, three years, willful. And remember, the Administrator does not prosecute. The Administrator refers evidence to the Attorney General or the District Attorney, who then brings the criminal case.

---

## SEGMENT 13: Section 410 Civil Liabilities

**HOST B:** Section 410 is civil liability. This is the private right of action. A purchaser who bought a security in violation of the registration provisions or the antifraud provisions can sue to recover.

**HOST A:** What they can recover is specific. The consideration paid for the security. Plus interest at six percent per year from the date of payment. Plus costs. Plus reasonable attorney's fees. Less any income received on the security. Upon tender of the security, or damages if the security is no longer owned.

**HOST B:** Six percent. Hardcoded in the statute. Not prime rate. Not market rate. Not a legal rate that varies by state. Flat statutory six percent, running from the date of payment.

**HOST A:** Section 410(b) is joint and several liability. Control persons, partners, officers, directors, employees who materially aid in the sale, and broker-dealers or agents who materially aid in the sale are all jointly and severally liable with the direct seller.

**HOST B:** And there is a defense. A non-seller defendant is liable unless the non-seller proves that he or she did not know and, in the exercise of reasonable care, could not have known of the facts making the liability apply. The burden is on the non-seller defendant. The plaintiff does not have to prove the defendant knew.

---

## SEGMENT 14: Section 410(e) and the Known Discrepancy

**HOST A:** Now we get to one of the strangest pieces of the Series 63. Section 410(e), the civil statute of limitations. This is the one section of Chapter 8 where the actual primary text of the 1956 Uniform Securities Act and the consensus of every commercial prep provider do not match.

**HOST B:** Let's start with the primary text. The 1956 USA, Section 410(e), says, and this is a direct quote, "No person may sue under this section more than two years after the contract of sale." Straight two years from the contract of sale. No discovery rule.

**HOST A:** Now here is what every commercial prep provider teaches. STC, Achievable, Career Employer, all of them. They all say, "Civil suits must be brought within three years from occurrence or two years from discovery, whichever is earlier." That phrase is everywhere.

**HOST B:** The problem is it does not match any version of the Uniform Securities Act. It does not match the 1956 version, which says two years. It does not match the 1985 Revised USA, which says one year from discovery or three years from the act. It does not match the 2002 USA, which says two years from discovery or five years from violation. It matches nothing.

**HOST A:** So what do you do on the exam? Strategy: look for "two years from the contract of sale" first. If it is offered, pick it. That is the Tier 1 verbatim answer from the 1956 text. If that exact phrase is not offered, then look for "three years from occurrence or two years from discovery, whichever earlier." That is the prep provider consensus. It is defensible even though it does not match any primary source.

**HOST B:** And do not pick "five years from violation," that is the 2002 USA, which is not tested. And do not pick "one year from discovery," that is the 1985 Revised USA, which is also not tested.

**HOST A:** It is the single weirdest piece of prep in this entire exam. Two years is the primary text answer. Three and two is the prep provider convention. Know both.

---

## SEGMENT 15: Rescission, Judicial Review, and Service of Process

**HOST B:** Three more pieces of Chapter 8 before we wrap. Section 410(f) is the right of rescission. It is the seller's get-out-of-jail card. A seller who realizes they sold in violation can offer to rescind the sale. The written rescission offer must refund the consideration paid plus six percent interest from the date of payment, less any income received.

**HOST A:** The buyer has thirty days from receipt of the offer to accept. If the buyer does not accept within thirty days, the right to sue under Section 410 is waived. Thirty days, same six percent interest as the civil recovery.

**HOST B:** Section 411 is judicial review. Any person aggrieved by a final order of the Administrator may obtain judicial review by filing a petition in the appropriate state court within sixty days after entry of the order.

**HOST A:** And here is the key point about Section 411. Filing the petition does NOT stay the order. The Administrator's order remains in effect during the appeal unless the reviewing court specifically orders a stay. So if the Administrator suspends an agent and the agent appeals within sixty days, the suspension is still in effect the whole time the appeal is pending. The agent cannot go back to work just because they filed a petition.

**HOST B:** Sixty days to appeal. Appeal does not stop the order.

**HOST A:** Section 414 handles jurisdiction and service of process. Section 414(e) has the famous two-thirds rule for newspapers. An offer is not considered made in a state when the publication is not published in the state, or is published in the state but had more than two-thirds of its circulation outside the state during the past twelve months.

**HOST B:** And for radio and television, the rule is different. An offer is not considered made in a state when the program originates outside the state. Newspapers is two-thirds circulation. Radio and TV is where it originates.

**HOST A:** Section 414(g) covers consent to service of process. When a person registers in a state, they must file a consent appointing the Administrator as the person's agent for receiving legal papers. Three key features. It is irrevocable. Once filed, it cannot be withdrawn, even if the registration is later withdrawn. It is filed only once. It does not have to be refiled for subsequent registrations in the same state. And it binds successors, personal representatives, and assigns.

**HOST B:** Irrevocable, once, forever.

---

## SEGMENT 16: SIPC Covers Broker Failure, Not Market Failure

**HOST A:** One last thing. The Securities Investor Protection Corporation. SIPC.

**HOST B:** SIPC covers customers of failed broker-dealers. Missing cash and securities when a broker-dealer fails or goes bankrupt, up to specific per-customer limits.

**HOST A:** What SIPC does not cover is the thing the exam tests. Market losses. If the broker-dealer is solvent and the customer's stock just went down, SIPC does nothing. If the broker recommended something unsuitable and the customer lost money, SIPC does nothing. If the customer bought fixed annuities, which are insurance products and not securities, SIPC does nothing.

**HOST B:** The memory hook is "SIPC covers broker failure, not market failure." If a question describes a market decline or bad advice, SIPC is not the answer. If a question describes a bankrupt broker-dealer with missing customer property, SIPC is the answer.

---

## SEGMENT 17: Putting It All Together

**HOST A:** Let's close by connecting the two chapters. Ethics and Remedies, back to back.

**HOST B:** Chapter 3 tells you what the conduct standards are. The NASAA Statement of Policy on Dishonest Practices gives you twenty-five prohibited broker-dealer practices and six prohibited agent practices. The NASAA Investment Company Shares rule gives you the point two five percent no-load ceiling. NASAA Model Rule 102(a)(4)-1 gives you the ten-business-day oral discretion rule for investment advisers. The Vulnerable Adults Model Act gives you the two, seven, fifteen, twenty-five ladder. Regulation Best Interest gives you D-C-C-C. FINRA Rule 2121 tells you the five percent policy is a guide, not a rule. And UPIA tells you that fiduciaries manage portfolios and risk, not individual stocks in isolation.

**HOST A:** Chapter 8 tells you what happens when the rules in Chapter 3 are broken. Section 101 antifraud has no exemptions, so even an exempt security cannot be sold with a material misstatement. Section 102 covers investment advisers the same way. The Administrator has broad powers under Sections 204, 306, 407, and 408, but cannot directly fine or imprison anyone. Section 409 gives you criminal penalties of five thousand dollars and three years, willful violation required. Section 410 gives you civil recovery with six percent interest, thirty days to accept rescission, and joint and several liability on non-sellers who materially aided the sale. Section 411 gives you sixty days to appeal, and the appeal does not stay the order. Section 414 gives you the two-thirds newspaper rule and irrevocable consent to service of process.

**HOST B:** And the single most quoted sentence from all of it, the one that ties everything together:

**HOST A:** Exempt from registration. Not exempt from fraud.

**HOST B:** Thirty-six percent of the exam. Go study.
