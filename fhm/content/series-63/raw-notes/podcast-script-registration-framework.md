# Who Registers Where: The Series 63 Registration Framework

## Podcast Script: Two Hosts Break Down Chapters 1, 2, and 7

**Coverage:** Broker-Dealers, Broker-Dealer Agents, and Investment Adviser Representatives
**Target length:** 18 to 22 minutes of audio (roughly 2,500 to 3,000 words spoken)
**Tone:** Conversational, precise, occasionally dry. Two smart people working through a problem out loud.

---

**HOST A:** If you had to boil the Series 63 down to one question, it would be this: who has to register, and where?

**HOST B:** That's really it. Three of the eight chapters are basically different answers to that same question. Chapter 1 is about broker-dealers, which are firms. Chapter 2 is about broker-dealer agents, which are the humans at those firms. And Chapter 7 is about investment adviser representatives, which are the humans at investment adviser firms.

**HOST A:** Firms, then humans at firms, then humans at a different kind of firm.

**HOST B:** And the rules are different for each. On purpose. So if you memorize one version of the registration rule and try to apply it everywhere, you're going to get about a third of these questions wrong.

**HOST A:** Let's start with Chapter 1. Broker-dealers. The firms.

---

## SEGMENT 1: Broker-Dealers and What Counts as "Doing Business" in a State

**HOST B:** So the definition comes from USA Section 401(c). A broker-dealer is any person in the business of effecting transactions in securities, either for customers or for the firm's own account.

**HOST A:** And "person" here means something broader than you'd think.

**HOST B:** Right. "Person" includes corporations, partnerships, associations, trusts, even government subdivisions. It's not just humans. In fact, the definition specifically excludes humans working for firms. Those humans are agents, not broker-dealers.

**HOST A:** So if a company does securities business, it's a broker-dealer. If a person does securities business on behalf of a company, that person is an agent. Two different registrations.

**HOST B:** And before any broker-dealer can do business in a state, it has to register. Section 201(a) is the statute: "It is unlawful for any person to transact business in this state as a broker-dealer or agent unless he is registered under this act."

**HOST A:** Which leads to the question the exam loves to ask: what counts as transacting business in the state?

**HOST B:** Four things. Maintaining an office there. Soliciting customers there. Accepting orders from customers there, even if the customer called you. And executing orders for customers there.

**HOST A:** So if you're a firm in Delaware and you make one phone call to a retail prospect in New Jersey, you're transacting business in New Jersey.

**HOST B:** Technically, yes. But here's where it gets interesting. There's a safe harbor.

---

## SEGMENT 2: The 15-Offer De Minimis Rule

**HOST A:** This is one of the most testable facts in Chapter 1. Section 401(c)(4)(B). The out-of-state safe harbor.

**HOST B:** So if a firm has no place of business in a state, it is not a broker-dealer in that state if either of two things is true. One, the firm's only customers in the state are institutional. Banks, insurance companies, investment companies, pension trusts, that kind of thing. Or two, during any 12 consecutive months, the firm has directed no more than 15 offers to non-institutional persons in the state.

**HOST A:** Fifteen offers. Not fifteen clients. Not fifteen sales. Offers.

**HOST B:** Right. And this is where the exam loves to trap you. They'll write a fact pattern where a firm placed some number of cold calls into a state. You need to ask yourself: how many offers? Not how many responses. Not how many people who bought something. Just how many offers went out.

**HOST A:** And 16 is over the line. If the firm places 16 offers in 12 months, the safe harbor is blown. They now have to register in that state.

**HOST B:** The 16th cold call is the one that breaks the safe harbor. There's something almost poetic about a statute where the consequence of a phone call depends on whether you've already made exactly fifteen other phone calls.

**HOST A:** And watch out for this one: the old "15 client" private adviser exemption. That was repealed by Dodd-Frank in 2010. If a question mentions "15 clients," that is either a broken reference to an old rule that no longer exists, or a misread of the actual rule, which is 15 offers.

**HOST B:** Fifteen offers in twelve months. Offers, not clients.

---

## SEGMENT 3: Noon of the 30th Day

**HOST A:** Let's say the firm does have to register. They file Form BD through the CRD, FINRA's Central Registration Depository. What happens next?

**HOST B:** Section 202(a) says the registration becomes effective at noon of the 30th day after the application is filed, assuming no denial order is in effect and no proceeding is pending under Section 204.

**HOST A:** Noon of the 30th day. Not midnight. Not 9 a.m. Not the 29th day. Not the 31st.

**HOST B:** And this part is important: the Administrator does not have to do anything for the registration to take effect. It's automatic. The Administrator has to take action to stop it.

**HOST A:** That's a reversal of what you might expect. You'd think the regulator has to approve you. Nope. You file, you wait 30 days, and unless the Administrator blocks it, you're registered at noon on day 30.

**HOST B:** The Administrator can accelerate. They can specify an earlier date by rule or order. So the 30-day period is a ceiling, not a floor.

**HOST A:** And even if an exam question tells you that the Administrator wrote a letter saying "we approve your application," that is not how registration works. Registration is effective by operation of law, not by affirmative approval.

---

## SEGMENT 4: Surety Bonds and the 10 / 25 / 2 Rule

**HOST B:** There's one more piece of Chapter 1 to get through before we move on to agents. Surety bonds. Section 202(e).

**HOST A:** So the Administrator can require a firm to post a surety bond as a condition of registration. This is not insurance for the firm. It's a pool of money that a customer can claim against if the customer wins a civil judgment against the firm under Section 410.

**HOST B:** Three numbers to memorize. Ten, twenty-five, two. The maximum bond amount is $10,000. The net capital exemption is $25,000, meaning if the firm has more than $25,000 in net capital, the Administrator cannot require a bond. And a customer has 2 years from the contract of sale to bring a claim against the bond.

**HOST A:** Ten is the cap, twenty-five is the exit, two is the time window.

**HOST B:** Memory tactic: "10 up, 25 out, 2 back." Ten thousand dollars up front. Twenty-five thousand in net capital and you're out of the bond requirement. Two years to sue on the bond.

---

## SEGMENT 5: The 11 Grounds for Denial, Suspension, or Revocation

**HOST A:** Before we leave broker-dealer firms, there's one more thing that gets tested almost every time the exam covers this domain. Section 204(a) lists eleven grounds for denial, suspension, or revocation of a registration. Labeled A through K.

**HOST B:** And we're not going to walk through all eleven right now, but there are two lookback periods that the exam tests relentlessly.

**HOST A:** Ground C: 10-year criminal lookback. The Administrator can deny or revoke if the applicant was convicted within the past 10 years of either a misdemeanor involving securities, or any felony, regardless of the crime.

**HOST B:** So if somebody was convicted of felony mail fraud 9 years ago, that's a ground for denial. Mail fraud isn't a securities offense, but it doesn't matter. Any felony counts, for 10 years.

**HOST A:** But a misdemeanor only counts if it's securities-related. Shoplifting is not a ground. Insider trading misdemeanor, yes.

**HOST B:** Ground F: 5-year regulatory order lookback. If the applicant is the subject of an order from another state's securities administrator, the SEC, or a federal banking agency, entered within the past 5 years, that's a ground.

**HOST A:** So the mnemonic is "ten for crimes, five for regulators." And those two numbers get swapped on the exam all the time. If a question gives you a 7-year-old felony, that's within the 10-year criminal lookback. If it gives you a 4-year-old sanction from the Texas securities administrator, that's within the 5-year regulatory lookback. Both are grounds for denial.

---

## SEGMENT 6: Chapter 2, Agents Are Always Individuals

**HOST B:** Okay, Chapter 2. Broker-dealer agents. The humans.

**HOST A:** The definition is in Section 401(b). "Agent means any individual other than a broker-dealer who represents a broker-dealer or issuer in effecting or attempting to effect purchases or sales of securities."

**HOST B:** Three things built into that sentence. First, an agent is always a natural person. Not a corporation. Not a partnership. A human being.

**HOST A:** Second, the agent represents either a broker-dealer or an issuer. Those two tracks are treated completely differently by the statute.

**HOST B:** And third, the activity is sales. Effecting or attempting to effect purchases or sales. Clerical staff, back-office people, receptionists, they're not agents. They're not involved in sales.

**HOST A:** So here's the headline rule, and it's the most important one in Chapter 2. If an individual represents a broker-dealer and is engaged in sales activity, that person is an agent and must register. Full stop. No exemptions.

**HOST B:** Let me stress that. A broker-dealer agent selling anything, and I mean anything, must be registered. If the security itself is exempt from registration, that does not help the agent. If the transaction is exempt, that does not help the agent. The agent still registers.

**HOST A:** So if the fact pattern describes a broker-dealer agent selling U.S. Treasury bonds, which are themselves exempt under Section 402(a)(1), the agent still needs to register. The security is exempt. The agent is not.

---

## SEGMENT 7: Issuer Agents and the Five Magic Subsections

**HOST B:** Now flip the track. An individual representing an issuer is only an agent if the individual is NOT covered by one of the issuer-agent exemptions. And there are three pathways.

**HOST A:** Pathway one: five specific exempt security categories. Section 402(a)(1), (2), (3), (10), and (11). Not all twelve. Just those five.

**HOST B:** So what are they? Federal and state government securities. Foreign government securities from countries with diplomatic relations, mostly Canada. Bank and savings institution securities. Commercial paper with a 9-month or shorter maturity. And investment contracts issued in connection with an employee benefit plan.

**HOST A:** Five categories. 1, 2, 3, 10, and 11.

**HOST B:** And here's the trap the exam loves. Section 402(a)(8), which covers listed securities, is NOT on that list. So if an individual is representing an issuer selling the issuer's own stock on the New York Stock Exchange, that individual is still an agent. Even though NYSE-listed securities are themselves exempt from registration.

**HOST A:** The listed-security exemption helps the security. It does not help the person.

**HOST B:** Pathway two: exempt transactions. Any of the twelve transactions listed in Section 402(b) creates an issuer-agent exemption. Private placements, institutional sales, isolated non-issuer transactions, and so on.

**HOST A:** And pathway three: the individual represents the issuer in sales to existing employees, partners, or directors of the issuer, with no commission paid. That's the "pitching to your own company" carve-out.

---

## SEGMENT 8: U4, U5, U6 and the "Firm, Firm, Regulator" Rule

**HOST B:** Let's talk about the registration mechanics. Three forms drive the agent lifecycle: U4, U5, and U6.

**HOST A:** Form U4 is the Uniform Application for Securities Industry Registration. It's the "hello I'd like to start working here" form. Filed by the employing firm on behalf of the individual.

**HOST B:** Form U5 is the Uniform Termination Notice. It's the "this person no longer works here" form. Also filed by the employing firm, within 30 days of termination. And the firm has to give the former employee a copy within 30 days.

**HOST A:** And Form U6 is the Uniform Disciplinary Action Reporting Form. Filed by the regulator, not the firm, when the regulator takes a final disciplinary action. That's what feeds into BrokerCheck.

**HOST B:** Firm files U4. Firm files U5. Regulator files U6. Firm, firm, regulator.

**HOST A:** And the exam loves to ask who files the disciplinary report on an agent. The trap answer is "the firm." The correct answer is the regulator.

---

## SEGMENT 9: FINRA Rule 3210, or Why You Can't Hide a Brokerage Account From Your Boss

**HOST B:** There are two FINRA rules specifically tested on Chapter 2. The first is Rule 3210. Outside brokerage accounts.

**HOST A:** This rule has a backstory. For decades, from the 1970s through the early 2000s, registered representatives were opening personal brokerage accounts at other firms in the names of spouses, parents, adult children. To avoid supervision. So they could trade on inside information, or front-run clients, or engage in market manipulation without leaving a trail at their own firm.

**HOST B:** And the firms kept not asking. So FINRA finally, in April 2017, replaced the old NASD rule with Rule 3210, which makes it very explicit.

**HOST A:** Three requirements. Prior written consent from the employing firm before opening any outside account. Written notice to the executing firm of the employment relationship, before the account opens. And the employing firm has the right to receive duplicate copies of confirmations and statements upon written request.

**HOST B:** The consent has to be prior. And written. Oral is not enough. After-the-fact is not enough.

**HOST A:** And this applies to any account where the associated person has a beneficial interest. So accounts in their own name, yes. Accounts held by their spouse, yes. Accounts held for a minor child where they have control, yes. Joint accounts, yes.

**HOST B:** For pre-existing accounts, meaning accounts the person already had before joining the firm, the rule still applies. The person has 30 days from the start of employment to get written consent.

**HOST A:** Some products are carved out. Mutual funds held directly with the fund company. 529 plans. Variable contracts. UITs. Those don't present the same conflict concerns, so they're not subject to Rule 3210.

---

## SEGMENT 10: FINRA Rule 2040 and Paying People Who Aren't Registered

**HOST B:** The second FINRA rule tested on Chapter 2 is Rule 2040. Payments to unregistered persons.

**HOST A:** The general rule: a member firm cannot pay compensation to a person who should be registered but isn't. If someone should be a broker-dealer under Section 15(a) of the 34 Act and isn't, you can't pay them for securities work.

**HOST B:** There are two exceptions. The first is the retiring representative exception, Rule 2040(b). A firm can pay continuing commissions, also called trail commissions, to a retiring registered rep on their old book of business.

**HOST A:** But there are strict conditions. There has to be a bona fide contract between the firm and the rep. That contract has to exist before retirement. And the contract has to prohibit the retiring rep from soliciting new business, opening new accounts, or servicing the accounts that generate the commissions.

**HOST B:** The whole point is that the retiring rep built the book while they were registered. They can collect on past work. They cannot do new work.

**HOST A:** The second exception is for non-registered foreign finders. Rule 2040(c). A firm can pay a finder's fee to a non-U.S. national who directs foreign customers to the firm, subject to seven conditions. The finder has to be foreign, the customers have to be foreign, the arrangement has to be disclosed in writing, and so on.

**HOST B:** And watch out for this trap: the retiring rep exception does NOT cover a former employee doing new sales work after leaving the firm. Even if they were a retired rep at some point. The exception is only about old commissions from old business. New work means the person needs to be registered.

---

## SEGMENT 11: Chapter 7 and the Split Between Firm and Rep

**HOST A:** Chapter 7. Investment adviser representatives. Only three questions on the exam, but these three questions hinge on a single distinction that the exam tests in about ten different ways.

**HOST B:** So let's start with who an IAR is. USA Section 401 defines it. An IAR is a partner, officer, director, or other individual at an investment advisory firm who does any of five things.

**HOST A:** One: makes recommendations or renders advice about securities. Two: manages client accounts or portfolios. Three: determines which recommendation or advice will be given. Four: solicits, offers, or negotiates for the sale of advisory services. Five: supervises anyone who does any of the above.

**HOST B:** So a research analyst who never meets a client? Still an IAR, because they fall under item three. They determine what the firm recommends.

**HOST A:** A solicitor who just refers clients in exchange for a fee and never gives advice themselves? Still an IAR, because of item four.

**HOST B:** A supervising principal? Still an IAR, because of item five.

**HOST A:** Clerical staff, receptionists, back-office employees. None of those are IARs. They're not in the chain of advice.

---

## SEGMENT 12: The Split That Matters Most

**HOST B:** Now here's the distinction the exam tests. Where does an IAR have to register? The answer depends on whether the investment advisory firm is state-registered or federal covered.

**HOST A:** Let's take state-registered first. The firm is state-registered because its assets under management are below the federal threshold. Under that scenario, the IAR has to register in a state if either one of two things is true.

**HOST B:** Either the IAR has a place of business in that state, or the IAR has more than 5 non-institutional clients in that state in any 12 consecutive months.

**HOST A:** Either one triggers registration. The IAR only has to clear one bar.

**HOST B:** Now flip it. The firm is a federal covered adviser. SEC-registered. Usually because AUM is above $110 million. Now the rule changes.

**HOST A:** For an IAR at a federal covered firm, the only trigger is place of business. Client count is irrelevant. It doesn't matter if the IAR has 50 clients in the state, 500 clients, 5,000 clients. If the IAR has no place of business in that state, the state cannot require registration.

**HOST B:** This is the rule that answers most Chapter 7 questions. "Place or more than 5 for state. Place only for federal." Seven words. Memorize it exactly.

**HOST A:** Here's a fact pattern the exam uses. An IAR works out of an office in Pennsylvania. The firm is SEC-registered. The IAR has 3 clients in Pennsylvania, 40 clients in New Jersey, and 8 clients in Delaware. The IAR has no place of business in New Jersey or Delaware.

**HOST B:** The answer is Pennsylvania only. The IAR registers in Pennsylvania because that's where the office is. The IAR does not register in New Jersey, even with 40 clients, because the firm is federal covered and the IAR has no place of business there. Same for Delaware.

**HOST A:** If the firm had been state-registered instead, the answer would be different. The IAR would register in New Jersey because of the 40 clients, and in Delaware because 8 is more than 5, and in Pennsylvania because of the office.

**HOST B:** Same facts. Different firm type. Different answer. That's the whole trap.

---

## SEGMENT 13: NSMIA and the Compromise

**HOST A:** Let's talk about why this split exists. Because it's not arbitrary. It's a direct result of the National Securities Markets Improvement Act of 1996. NSMIA.

**HOST B:** Before NSMIA, every investment adviser had to register with every state where it did business. One adviser with clients in 40 states could end up with 40 separate registrations. It was an administrative mess.

**HOST A:** Congress decided to split it up. Big advisers, meaning those with more than $110 million under management after Dodd-Frank, would register with the SEC. States would lose the ability to require these firms to register at the state level.

**HOST B:** That's the preemption half of NSMIA. Section 203A(b)(1) of the Investment Advisers Act.

**HOST A:** But states pushed back. They said, "fine, you can take the firms, but we're keeping the humans. The people sitting across the table from our residents."

**HOST B:** And that compromise is embedded in the same statute. States may not require federal covered advisers themselves to register, but states retain the right to require registration of investment adviser representatives who have a place of business in the state.

**HOST A:** Firm answers to the SEC. Rep answers to the state. That's the phrase to remember.

---

## SEGMENT 14: The 30-Day Promptly Rule

**HOST B:** There's one more piece of Chapter 7. NASAA Model Rule 202(a)-2. This is the rule that governs the mechanics of IAR registration.

**HOST A:** The initial application is Form U-4, filed through IARD. That's the Investment Adviser Registration Depository, which is FINRA's electronic system, running on behalf of NASAA and the SEC.

**HOST B:** Annual renewal is also filed through IARD.

**HOST A:** And here's the timing rule the exam tests. If any information on Form U-4 changes, the rep changes addresses, has a new disclosure event, whatever, the rep has to file an amendment promptly.

**HOST B:** The rule itself defines "promptly" as within 30 days of the triggering event. Not 10 days. Not 60 days. Not "as soon as possible."

**HOST A:** Thirty days means promptly. That's a direct quote from the rule.

**HOST B:** And be careful of a distractor answer here. If the exam offers "10 business days," that's the investment adviser oral discretion rule from Chapter 3. Different rule. Different context. Different number.

---

## SEGMENT 15: State Antifraud Authority Over Federal Covered Advisers

**HOST A:** One last point about NSMIA before we wrap up. Section 203A(b)(2) preserves state antifraud authority over SEC-registered advisers.

**HOST B:** So even though a state cannot require a federal covered firm to register, the state can still investigate that firm for fraud. State securities administrators can bring enforcement actions under Section 102 of the Uniform Securities Act against federal covered advisers.

**HOST A:** Registration and antifraud authority run on separate tracks. Preemption of registration does not preempt antifraud.

**HOST B:** And this is consistent with everything else on the exam. The USA Sections 101 and 102 antifraud provisions have no exemptions. Ever. A security exempt from registration is still subject to Section 101. An investment adviser exempt from registration is still subject to Section 102. The "exempt from registration, not exempt from fraud" rule applies everywhere.

---

## SEGMENT 16: Putting It All Together

**HOST A:** Let's close by connecting the three chapters. The whole registration framework, in one pass.

**HOST B:** A broker-dealer firm registers when it does business in a state, which means having an office there, soliciting customers there, accepting orders from customers there, or executing orders for customers there. The registration is effective at noon of the 30th day after filing, unless the Administrator blocks it.

**HOST A:** A broker-dealer agent, meaning an individual working for that firm, always has to register if they're involved in sales. There are no exemptions for broker-dealer agents based on the security or the transaction. The BD agent registers. Period.

**HOST B:** An issuer agent sometimes has to register and sometimes doesn't. The carve-outs are in Section 402. Five specific exempt security categories, twelve exempt transaction categories, and the existing-employee-no-commission sale.

**HOST A:** An investment adviser representative registers with a state if, at a state-registered firm, they have either a place of business there or more than 5 non-institutional clients there. If the firm is federal covered, only place of business triggers registration.

**HOST B:** Form U4 in, Form U5 out, Form U6 for the regulator. Firm, firm, regulator.

**HOST A:** Ten for crimes. Five for regulators. Those are the Section 204 lookback periods.

**HOST B:** Noon of the 30th day. That's when registration becomes effective.

**HOST A:** And "promptly" means 30 days in the IAR amendment context.

**HOST B:** That's the whole framework. Three chapters. One question: who registers, and where.

**HOST A:** The exam will ask it about fifteen different ways. The answers will come from what we just covered.

**HOST B:** Go study.
