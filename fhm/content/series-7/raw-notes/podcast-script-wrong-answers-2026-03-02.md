# Why Common Sense Fails the Series 7 — Practice Exam Review

## Podcast Script: Two Hosts Break Down 19 Wrong Answers

---

**HOST A:** Alright, so we just went through a practice exam, and let's be honest — some of these questions were humbling.

**HOST B:** Nineteen wrong answers. And here's the thing — most of them weren't because the material was impossibly hard. It's that the exam is testing whether you can resist the answer that *feels* right and pick the one that actually *is* right.

**HOST A:** That's the whole game. So let's walk through these, group by group, and figure out where the thinking went sideways. Starting with something that should've been a gimme.

---

## SEGMENT 1: Securities Laws — Know Your Acts

**HOST B:** Okay. "The SEC was created by the..." — and the answer chosen was the Securities Act of 1933.

**HOST A:** Which feels completely logical if you're thinking chronologically. The SEC exists, the '33 Act came first, so the '33 Act must've created it.

**HOST B:** But no. The Securities Act of 1933 was about requiring registration and disclosure for new securities. It set the rules. The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 came right after and said, "Cool, now we need someone to actually enforce all this." That's the SEC.

**HOST A:** And the timing is wild — the '33 Act was passed at the very end of 1933, and the '34 Act was passed at the very beginning of 1934. They were basically enacted back-to-back.

**HOST B:** So the way to remember it: 1933 = the rules. 1934 = the referee. The SEC is the referee.

**HOST A:** Next one — "All of the following are exempt from registration under the 1933 Act EXCEPT." The choices were investment companies, insurance companies, agency issues, and municipal issues.

**HOST B:** And the pick was insurance companies.

**HOST A:** Which are actually exempt. Here's the trick list you have to memorize: governments, agencies, municipals, insurance companies, banks — all exempt. The one that's NOT exempt? Investment companies. Mutual funds, ETFs — they have to register and sell with a prospectus.

**HOST B:** Think about it this way: investment companies are literally in the business of selling securities to the public. Of course they need a prospectus. The others are issuing debt or are regulated by other agencies already.

**HOST A:** That's the "why" behind the rule. The SEC doesn't need to regulate Treasury bonds — the U.S. government's got that covered. But a mutual fund packaging stocks together and selling shares? Yeah, you need a prospectus for that.

---

## SEGMENT 2: The Underwriting Spread — Twice Burned, Same Lesson

**HOST B:** This one came up twice on the exam. Twice! Different wording, same concept. "Which fee does the lead underwriter earn?" and "Which component of the spread is the smallest?"

**HOST A:** And both times, the answer was selling concession. Both times, the correct answer was management fee.

**HOST B:** So let's just lock this in. The spread is the total gross compensation earned by the underwriting syndicate. It breaks down into three pieces. Think of it like a layer cake.

**HOST A:** At the top — the management fee. Goes to the syndicate manager for running the whole show. It's the smallest slice.

**HOST B:** In the middle — the underwriting fee. Goes to the syndicate member who takes financial responsibility for those shares.

**HOST A:** At the bottom, the biggest piece — the selling concession. Goes to whoever actually sells the share to the investor, whether that's a syndicate member or a selling group member.

**HOST B:** And here's the kicker that the exam loves: if a syndicate member both takes financial responsibility AND sells the share themselves? They get both the underwriting fee and the selling concession. They get everything except the management fee.

**HOST A:** The lead underwriter gets the management fee on every single share sold. It's small per share, but they get it on everything. That's how you remember it — the manager takes their cut off the top, on every deal.

## SEGMENT 3: Retirement Plans — The Details That Trip You Up

**HOST B:** Three questions in this bucket, and they're all about knowing the specific numbers and rules that don't follow common sense.

**HOST A:** First — 403(b) plans. "Which person is least likely to contribute to a 403(b)?" And the answer picked was secretary at a foundation.

**HOST B:** But a secretary at a foundation is an employee of a non-profit. That's exactly who 403(b) plans are for. The correct answer was a student at a college.

**HOST A:** Students aren't employees. It doesn't matter that they're at a university — which is a non-profit institution. You have to be on the payroll.

**HOST B:** Professors, nurses at the hospital, secretaries at foundations — they're all employees of non-profits. Students just attend. No employment, no 403(b).

**HOST A:** Next — the RMD penalty. "What's the tax penalty for failing to take a required minimum distribution?" The pick was 10%.

**HOST B:** It's 25%. And RMDs kick in the year you turn 73. Miss one, and the IRS takes a quarter of whatever you were supposed to withdraw. That's not a slap on the wrist — that's a punch in the wallet.

**HOST A:** The 10% number is probably floating around in your head from early withdrawal penalties. That's the penalty for withdrawing from a retirement account before age 59 and a half. Different rule, different penalty.

**HOST B:** 10% for taking money out too early. 25% for not taking it out when you're supposed to. The IRS wants its tax revenue one way or another.

**HOST A:** And then IRA transfers versus rollovers. "Which is true about IRA transfers?" The pick was "a maximum of 1 per year."

**HOST B:** That's rollovers. Transfers are unlimited.

**HOST A:** Here's the distinction, and it matters: a transfer goes directly from trustee to trustee. The money never touches your hands. You can do this as many times as you want.

**HOST B:** A rollover is when the check is made out to you, the account holder. You have 60 days to get it into another IRA, and you only get one of those per year.

**HOST A:** The easy memory trick: Transfers = Trustee-to-Trustee = no Tally (no limit). Rollovers = you're Rolling the dice with a 60-day clock and only one shot per year.

## SEGMENT 4: Account Types, Documents & Broker-Dealer Rules

**HOST B:** Okay, this group is all about knowing which piece of paper does what and how different types of firms actually operate.

**HOST A:** First one — "What document permits a broker-dealer to borrow customer securities for short sales?" And the answer was hypothecation agreement.

**HOST B:** Which is so close to right that it's almost cruel. The hypothecation agreement IS a margin document — it's the one where the customer pledges their securities as collateral for the margin loan. That's mandatory for margin accounts.

**HOST A:** But the question asked about lending securities for short selling. That's the loan consent agreement. And here's the thing — it's customary but NOT mandatory. The customer can say no.

**HOST B:** So: hypothecation = "I pledge my securities as collateral." Loan consent = "I allow you to lend my shares to short sellers." Different permission, different document.

**HOST A:** Then there's the FINRA employee account question. An accounting supervisor at another FINRA firm wants to open an account. What's NOT required?

**HOST B:** The answer picked was that the employee gives written notice to the executing member about their association with the employer firm. But that IS required.

**HOST A:** What's actually NOT required is sending annual account summaries to the employer. The real requirements are: prior written consent from the employing firm, the executing member is notified in writing, and on written request, duplicate confirmations and statements get sent.

**HOST B:** And this rule isn't just for registered reps. It covers "associated persons" — officers, registered employees, unregistered employees. The only exception is pure clerical or ministerial staff. It even extends to accounts at banks and investment advisers, not just other member firms.

**HOST A:** And then introducing broker-dealers. "Which statement is FALSE?" The pick was "must sign a clearing agreement with a clearing broker-dealer."

**HOST B:** But that's true! That's literally what makes them an introducing firm — they introduce their accounts to a clearing firm.

**HOST A:** The false statement was "cannot accept trades from customers." They absolutely can accept trades. They just can't hold the money or the securities. The clearing firm handles that back-office stuff.

**HOST B:** Think of the introducing firm as the front desk and the clearing firm as the back office. The front desk takes your order, the back office processes it. And customer complaints? Those go to the introducing firm, not the clearing firm. You deal with the front desk.

---

## SEGMENT 5: Bonds — Basis Points, VRDOs, and Dollar Pricing

**HOST B:** Three bond questions, and each one tests a different calculation or concept that's easy to second-guess.

**HOST A:** Basis points first. "$10,000 municipal bond, one year to maturity, basis quote raised by 20 basis points. What's the price change?" The answer was $20 increase.

**HOST B:** Got the dollar amount right — it IS $20. But the direction is backwards. When a basis quote goes UP, that means interest rates went up. And when interest rates go up...

**HOST A:** Bond prices go down. The inverse relationship. This is fundamental, but under exam pressure, you see "raised" and your brain wants to pick "increase."

**HOST B:** Let's walk the math. 1 basis point = 0.01%. Twenty basis points = 0.20%. On $10,000 par, that's 0.002 times $10,000 = $20. Interest rates rose, so the bond's price drops by $20.

**HOST A:** And a critical note — this type of question can ONLY be asked for bonds with one year to maturity. Longer-term bonds would require discounted cash flow calculations, which aren't tested on the Series 7.

**HOST B:** Next — the VRDO question. "A security with a floating rate, stated maturity, and the option to deliver it back to the issuer for face value plus accrued interest." The pick was put option.

**HOST A:** And I get why — there's a "put" feature in a VRDO. You can put the bond back to the issuer. But a VRDO is a specific type of municipal security. It's not the same as a standalone put option contract.

**HOST B:** A variable-rate demand obligation has three defining features: floating interest rate that resets daily, weekly, or monthly; a long-term maturity date; and that put feature letting you sell it back at face value plus accrued interest. It's a bond with built-in flexibility.

**HOST A:** A put option, by contrast, is a derivatives contract giving you the right to sell an underlying security to an option writer at a strike price. Completely different instrument.

**HOST B:** And finally — municipal bond dollar pricing. "$5,000 of New York 3% G.O.'s at 90." The answer was $5,000.

**HOST A:** But "at 90" means 90% of par value. It's not $90 per bond or a $90 fee. It means the bonds are trading at a 10% discount to face value.

**HOST B:** So 90% of $5,000 = $4,500. That's what you actually pay, excluding commissions and accrued interest.

**HOST A:** And there's a vocabulary distinction here that the exam cares about. Municipal bonds quoted as a dollar price — like "at 90" — are called dollar bonds, and they're usually term issues, meaning they all mature on the same date.

**HOST B:** Serial bonds, on the other hand, are quoted on a yield-to-maturity basis. Different quoting convention, different type of bond structure.

## SEGMENT 6: Equities, Products & Suitability

**HOST B:** Last group — and it's a mix of corporate structure, investment products, and matching the right strategy to the right client.

**HOST A:** Outstanding shares. "100,000 authorized, 20,000 issued, 5,000 treasury. How many outstanding?" The pick was 120,000.

**HOST B:** Which would mean you somehow have more shares outstanding than were authorized. That's mathematically impossible. The formula is simple: Outstanding = Issued minus Treasury. 20,000 minus 5,000 = 15,000.

**HOST A:** Authorized is just the maximum number the company is allowed to issue per its corporate charter. It's a ceiling, not a factor in the outstanding calculation.

**HOST B:** Then the registrar question. "The registrar is responsible for?" And the answer was recording changes of ownership.

**HOST A:** That's the transfer agent. The registrar's job is to oversee and audit the transfer agent. The registrar is the quality control layer — making sure share counts are correct and that issued shares never exceed authorized shares.

**HOST B:** Transfer agent = does the work. Registrar = checks the work.

**HOST A:** Now suitability — the interval fund question. "Wealthy investor, large portfolio of public equities and fixed income, low liquidity needs, long time horizon — what's most suitable?"

**HOST B:** The pick was balanced mutual fund, which is just more of what they already have. The key phrase in the question is "wants to diversify." If they already own publicly traded stocks and bonds, buying a fund that holds publicly traded stocks and bonds doesn't diversify anything.

**HOST A:** Interval funds invest in illiquid stuff — commercial real estate, private securities, below-investment grade debt. They only let you sell shares at specific intervals, with a max 25% buyback. That's why they can invest in things regular funds can't.

**HOST B:** And because they're less correlated with public markets, they actually provide genuine diversification. The trade-off is low liquidity — but the question told us this investor has low liquidity needs. Perfect match.

**HOST A:** Last one, and it's probably the trickiest. "Wealthy, sophisticated investor with high risk tolerance turns extremely bullish. Best recommendation?"

**HOST B:** The pick was sell index puts. Which is bullish — but it's moderately bullish with limited profit potential. The question said "extremely bullish."

**HOST A:** The correct answer is sell leveraged inverse ETFs. Think about this: a 300% leveraged inverse ETF based on the S&P 500. If the index goes UP 15%, this ETF goes DOWN 3 times 15% — that's 45%. If you're short that ETF, you just made 45%.

**HOST B:** It's an aggressive strategy for a client who is convinced the market is going up and has the wealth, sophistication, and risk tolerance to handle it if they're wrong. Because if the market drops, they lose big time.

**HOST A:** And there was a trap answer in there — inverse floaters. Those are bonds whose interest rate moves opposite to market rates. They're not really tested on the Series 7 except as a wrong answer.

---

## SEGMENT 7: The Open Interest Question

**HOST A:** One more — options. "Which increases open interest?"

**HOST B:** The answer picked was an opening sale and a closing purchase. But that's one of each — one opens, one closes. They cancel out.

**HOST A:** Open interest increases when BOTH sides of the transaction are opening. An opening purchase AND an opening sale — that's a brand new contract being created. Both parties are entering fresh positions.

**HOST B:** And the reverse is true: closing purchase plus closing sale = contract disappearing, open interest goes down.

**HOST A:** If it's mixed — one opening and one closing — open interest stays flat. Think of it like a relay race. If a new runner starts and an old runner finishes at the same time, the number of runners on the track doesn't change.

**HOST B:** That's actually a great way to think about it. Opening transactions put new contracts on the field. Closing transactions take them off. Both need to open for the count to go up.

---

## WRAP-UP

**HOST A:** So what's the pattern across all 19 of these?

**HOST B:** A few things. First — direction matters. Interest rates go up, bond prices go down. The exam will test whether you can keep the inverse relationship straight under pressure.

**HOST A:** Second — know the specific numbers. 25% RMD penalty, not 10%. Unlimited transfers versus one rollover per year. 90% of par means $4,500 on a $5,000 bond. The exam is full of answers that are almost right.

**HOST B:** Third — read the question twice. "Which is FALSE?" "Which is the EXCEPT?" Half these mistakes came from the answer being true when the question asked for false, or picking something that IS required when the question asked what ISN'T.

**HOST A:** And finally — suitability questions are about what the client DOESN'T already have. If they own public stocks and bonds, don't recommend more public stocks and bonds. Match the product to the gap in their portfolio, not to what sounds safest.

**HOST B:** Nineteen questions. Nineteen lessons. Now they're locked in.

**HOST A:** Until next time.
