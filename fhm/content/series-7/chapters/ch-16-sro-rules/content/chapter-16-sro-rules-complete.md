# Chapter 16: Self-Regulatory Organizations -- Wall Street's Private Police Force

## The Complete Guide for Series 7 Candidates

---

## Opening: Why the Industry Polices Itself

After the 1929 crash wiped out millions of investors, Congress faced a question that still shapes your career today: who should watch the watchers? Creating the SEC in 1934 was step one, but the agency couldn't realistically monitor every broker, every trade, and every customer conversation. So in 1938, Congress passed the Maloney Act, which authorized something unusual in American regulation: letting the industry police itself, under government supervision.

The logic was practical. Securities professionals understand their own business better than any government bureaucrat. They know when a trade smells wrong, when a rep is churning accounts, when a firm's compliance is more theater than substance. So Congress said: organize yourselves, write your own rules, enforce them on your members, and we'll supervise from above. If you fail, we step in.

That framework has survived for nearly nine decades. Today, FINRA oversees roughly 3,400 broker-dealer firms and over 620,000 registered representatives. The MSRB writes the rules for municipal securities. The exchanges regulate their own trading floors. And the SEC sits above them all, with the power to approve, modify, or overrule any SRO rule.

For the Series 7 exam, SRO content appears across all four functions. You'll see it in questions about registration, communications, customer complaints, trading rules, and municipal securities. It's not concentrated in one section -- it's woven throughout. Understanding this chapter means understanding the operating system your entire career runs on.

## Part 1: The Regulatory Hierarchy

Before diving into specific SROs, you need to understand who reports to whom. This chain of authority gets tested directly, and mixing up which body does what is one of the most common mistakes on the exam.

### The Chain of Command

At the top sits **Congress**, which writes the foundational securities laws: the Securities Act of 1933, the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the Investment Company Act of 1940, and the Investment Advisers Act of 1940.

Below Congress is the **Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)**, created by the Exchange Act of 1934. The SEC is a federal government agency -- this matters because SROs are not. The SEC has ultimate authority over all SROs: it registers broker-dealers, investment advisers, transfer agents, and clearing agencies. It enforces federal securities laws. It can censure, fine, suspend, revoke registrations, or bar individuals. And critically, every SRO rule must be submitted to the SEC for review and approval before it takes effect.

Below the SEC sit the **Self-Regulatory Organizations**: FINRA for broker-dealer regulation, the exchanges (NYSE, Nasdaq, Cboe) for exchange regulation, the MSRB for municipal securities rulemaking, and clearing organizations like DTC, NSCC, and OCC.

Running parallel to this federal structure are **state regulators** -- sometimes called "Blue Sky" regulators because early securities fraud was so brazen that promoters were accused of selling "building lots in the blue sky." Each state has its own securities administrator, and state registration requirements apply in addition to federal requirements. The Uniform Securities Act governs state-level regulation.

### Entities That Are NOT SROs (But Get Tested)

**The Federal Reserve Board (FRB)** sets margin requirements through Regulation T and controls monetary policy. It is not an SRO. When the exam asks who sets initial margin requirements, the answer is the Fed -- not FINRA, not the SEC. FINRA sets maintenance margin requirements through Rule 4210, which can be stricter than Reg T but never less strict.

**The Treasury Department** issues government securities. Not an SRO.

**FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network)** handles AML oversight and SAR filings. Not an SRO.

**SIPC (Securities Investor Protection Corporation)** is not a regulator, not an SRO, and not an insurance company in the traditional sense. It provides account protection when a broker-dealer fails financially. We'll cover SIPC in detail later because it is heavily tested, and the exam loves to test whether you know what SIPC is NOT.

## Part 2: FINRA -- The Primary SRO

### History

The story of FINRA starts in 1938, when the Maloney Act amended the Exchange Act to authorize national securities associations. In 1939, the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASD) registered with the SEC as the first -- and for decades, the only -- such association. In 1971, the NASD created the Nasdaq Stock Market as an electronic quotation system, which eventually became its own independent exchange in 2006.

The big change came on July 30, 2007, when the NASD merged with the member regulation, enforcement, and arbitration functions of the NYSE to form FINRA -- the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority. So when you see old exam materials referencing "NASD rules," know that these are now FINRA rules.

### What FINRA Does

FINRA is a private, not-for-profit corporation. This is not a government agency. It operates under SEC authorization as an SRO under Section 15A of the Exchange Act. Here's what it does:

- Writes and enforces rules governing broker-dealer conduct
- Administers qualification exams (Series 7, SIE, Series 63, Series 66, and others)
- Operates the Central Registration Depository (CRD) system
- Operates BrokerCheck, the free public database for researching firms and representatives
- Runs FINRA Dispute Resolution (arbitration and mediation)
- Conducts examinations and investigations of member firms
- Issues sanctions for violations: fines, suspensions, bars, and firm expulsions

### What FINRA Cannot Do

This is where exam questions get tricky. FINRA can fine you, suspend you, bar you permanently, or expel your firm. But FINRA **cannot send you to jail**. Only the Department of Justice can bring criminal charges. FINRA also cannot issue injunctions -- only courts can do that. And FINRA cannot regulate non-member firms or non-associated persons.

When the exam gives you a scenario where someone commits fraud and asks about potential consequences, remember: FINRA handles the regulatory side (fines, bars, expulsion). Criminal prosecution goes through the DOJ. Civil enforcement actions go through the SEC or federal courts.

## Part 3: All the Other SROs

The exam doesn't just test FINRA. You need to know every SRO and what makes each one different.

### NYSE (New York Stock Exchange)

A registered national securities exchange and SRO under Section 6 of the Exchange Act. The NYSE sets listing standards for companies traded on its floor and maintains its own trading rules. Since 2007, member regulation functions were consolidated into FINRA, but the NYSE still handles market regulation -- surveillance, listing standards, and trading rules specific to its venue.

### Cboe (Chicago Board Options Exchange)

The largest U.S. options exchange. It's an SRO that writes rules for options trading. Key Cboe rules tested on the Series 7 include Rules 9.8, 9.9, and 9.15, which govern options communications and advertising.

### Nasdaq

A registered national securities exchange, originally created by the NASD in 1971 as an electronic quotation system. Nasdaq became a separate, independent exchange in 2006, before the FINRA merger. It sets its own listing standards and trading rules.

### OCC (Options Clearing Corporation)

The central counterparty and clearinghouse for all U.S. listed options. The OCC issues and guarantees all listed option contracts. It publishes the Options Disclosure Document (ODD) -- that document that must be delivered to customers before they're approved for options trading. The OCC is registered as a clearing agency with the SEC.

### DTC/NSCC (Depository Trust Company / National Securities Clearing Corporation)

Part of DTCC (Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation). The DTC is the central securities depository that holds securities in "street name." The NSCC clears and settles most equity and debt trades. Both are registered clearing agencies -- SROs under SEC oversight.

### MSRB (Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board)

The MSRB deserves its own section because of a critical distinction the exam loves to test.

Created by the Securities Acts Amendments of 1975, the MSRB writes rules for broker-dealers and banks that deal in municipal securities. But here's the twist: **the MSRB writes rules but does NOT enforce them.**

- FINRA enforces MSRB rules for broker-dealers
- Bank regulators (OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve) enforce MSRB rules for banks
- The SEC oversees the MSRB itself

The MSRB also cannot regulate municipal issuers directly. It can only regulate the professionals who work with municipalities. So when a city issues bonds with misleading disclosure, the MSRB can't go after the city -- but it can go after (through FINRA) the broker-dealer that underwrote the deal without adequate due diligence.

## Part 4: Registration -- Getting In and Staying In

### Who Needs to Register?

Not everyone at a broker-dealer needs to pass the Series 7. But more people than you might expect require some form of registration. The following individuals must register as **associated persons**:

- Individuals who solicit orders
- Individuals who train others to solicit orders
- Supervisors of these activities
- Anyone responsible for supervising financial services offered by registered representatives
- Anyone with signature or approval authority for customer accounts

The SIE exam alone does NOT qualify you to work in securities. You must also pass a representative-level exam (like the Series 7) while sponsored by a member firm.

### Form U4 and Form U5

**Form U4 (Uniform Application for Securities Industry Registration)** is filed by the member firm to register an individual. It requires a 10-year employment history, 5-year residential history, and disclosure questions covering criminal, regulatory, civil, and financial matters. The U4 must be filed before an individual can take a qualification exam. It creates the individual's record in the Central Registration Depository (CRD).

Amendment deadlines matter for the exam:
- **30 days** for most material changes
- **10 days** for statutory disqualification events (criminal convictions, regulatory orders, etc.)

**Form U5 (Uniform Termination Notice)** is filed by the member firm within **30 days** of termination. The firm must provide a copy to the terminated individual within 30 days. The reason for termination must be disclosed: voluntary, terminated, permitted to resign, or other. There's a continuing obligation to amend disclosure questions until final disposition.

Late filing penalty for U5: $100 for the first day late, then $25/day after, up to $1,575.

### Requalification and the 2-Year Clock

If you spend 2 or more years without association to a member firm, you must retake your qualification exams. FINRA prohibits firms from artificially extending this window by "parking" or "hanging" an individual's registration.

However, the **Maintaining Qualifications Program (MQP)** allows extending the period from 2 to 5 years by completing annual continuing education -- but only for those who've been registered for at least one year.

### Statutory Disqualification

A person is "statutorily disqualified" under Sections 3(a)(39) and 15(b)(4) of the Exchange Act if they have been:
- Convicted of certain felonies (within 10 years)
- Expelled or barred from an SRO
- Subject to an SEC order denying registration
- Convicted of a misdemeanor involving securities or money

A statutorily disqualified person cannot associate with a member firm unless FINRA grants a waiver through the Membership Continuance Application (MC-400) process.

### BrokerCheck

BrokerCheck is FINRA's free public database where investors can research the background of firms and representatives. It includes employment history, exam results, and any disciplinary actions. Every year, member firms must provide customers with:
- The FINRA BrokerCheck hotline number
- A statement about the availability of the BrokerCheck investor brochure
- The FINRA website address

## Part 5: Supervision and Compliance

### Office Structure

Every broker-dealer must have a **main office** with a supervisory principal. Beyond that, firms operate through branch offices where customer securities activities occur.

An **Office of Supervisory Jurisdiction (OSJ)** is where supervisory responsibilities are carried out. Not every branch is an OSJ, but every OSJ must have a designated OSJ manager who is a registered principal. Think of OSJs as the supervisory hubs in a firm's network.

### Written Supervisory Procedures (Rule 3110)

Every firm must establish, maintain, and enforce written supervisory procedures (WSPs) that are:
- In effect at all times
- Kept at each location (subject matter appropriate to that office)
- Updated annually
- Accessible to all employees

Firms must also designate a chief compliance officer (CCO) and hold an **annual compliance meeting** that every registered person must attend.

Firms must conduct background investigations on new hires, including review of Form U4 and U5 history, verification of prior employment for the previous 3 years, and search of public records.

### Continuing Education (Rule 1240)

Registration isn't "one and done." FINRA mandates ongoing education through two elements:

**Regulatory Element:** Computer-based training created by FINRA. Must be completed annually by December 31 for each registration held (this changed in 2023 -- the old rule was every 3 years). If not completed by December 31, the registration becomes **inactive**. While inactive, the person cannot perform any activities requiring registration. Content covers regulatory topics, sales practice standards, and new rules.

**Firm Element:** Designed and administered by the member firm. Must be tailored to the firm's size, structure, scope of business, and regulatory concerns. A written training plan must be maintained, updated at least annually, and delivered to all covered registered persons.

### Military Service Accommodations

FINRA provides special accommodations for registered persons entering active military service:
- Upon notification, the individual is placed on inactive status
- The firm may allow continued commission payments on customer accounts
- The person cannot perform regular duties
- Continuing education requirements are suspended
- The 2-year termination clock is **tolled (paused)** until 90 days after leaving military service

## Part 6: Know Your Customer, Suitability, and Regulation Best Interest

These three rules form the foundation of customer protection, and the exam tests them heavily. Understanding the progression from KYC to suitability to Reg BI is essential.

### Rule 2090 -- Know Your Customer (KYC)

The foundation. Members must use "reasonable diligence" to know the essential facts about every customer: identity, financial status, investment objectives, and authority to act. This information must be gathered at account opening and updated periodically.

### Rule 2111 -- Suitability

Three components, and you need to know all three:

1. **Reasonable-basis suitability:** The rep must understand the product or strategy well enough to evaluate its risks and rewards. This is product-level due diligence -- you can't recommend something you don't understand.

2. **Customer-specific suitability:** The recommendation must be suitable for the specific customer based on their investment profile: age, other investments, financial situation, tax status, investment objectives, experience, time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance.

3. **Quantitative suitability:** Even if individual trades are suitable, the overall level of activity must not be excessive. This is the anti-churning provision, and it's the one people forget on the exam.

Important: Rule 2111 does NOT apply to recommendations subject to Regulation Best Interest. Compliance with Reg BI satisfies Rule 2111.

### Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) -- SEC Rule 15l-1

Effective June 30, 2020, Reg BI applies to broker-dealers when making recommendations to **retail customers**. It's a higher standard than suitability -- the rep must act in the customer's "best interest."

Four core obligations:

1. **Disclosure Obligation:** Full and fair written disclosure of material facts about the relationship and conflicts of interest, provided before or at the time of recommendation.

2. **Care Obligation:** Exercise reasonable diligence, care, and skill. Consider reasonably available alternatives. No recommendation that places the broker-dealer's interest ahead of the customer's.

3. **Conflict of Interest Obligation:** Establish written policies to identify, disclose, and mitigate or eliminate conflicts. Must address sales contests, quotas, bonuses, and non-cash compensation tied to specific products.

4. **Compliance Obligation:** Establish and maintain written policies and procedures reasonably designed to achieve compliance with Reg BI.

**Form CRS (Customer Relationship Summary):** A required disclosure document, maximum 2 pages, delivered to retail investors at or before the earliest of: a recommendation, placing an order, or opening an account.

The exam distinction: if a question says "retail customer" and involves a recommendation, think Reg BI. If it says "institutional customer," think suitability (Rule 2111).

## Part 7: Communications with the Public (Rule 2210)

FINRA classifies all communications into three categories, each with different approval and filing requirements. The threshold number -- 25 retail investors in 30 days -- is one of the most tested details.

### Three Categories

**Correspondence:** Written communication to 25 or fewer retail investors within any 30-calendar-day period. Subject to supervision and review but does not require pre-use principal approval.

**Retail Communication:** Written communication distributed to more than 25 retail investors within any 30-calendar-day period. This includes advertising and sales literature. Must be approved by a registered principal **before first use**.

**Institutional Communication:** Written communication distributed only to institutional investors. Must have written supervisory procedures for review but does NOT require pre-use approval.

**Institutional investor** includes: registered investment companies, banks, insurance companies, registered investment advisers, government entities, employee benefit plans with at least 100 participants, and persons with total assets of at least $50 million.

### Filing Requirements

Filing requirements depend on how long the firm has been a FINRA member:

| Communication Type | New Member (First Year) | Established Member |
|---|---|---|
| Options | 10 business days before use | Within 10 business days of first use |
| Investment Companies | 10 business days before use | Within 10 business days of first use |
| CMOs / DPPs | 10 business days before use | Within 10 business days of first use |
| General Advertising | 10 business days before use | No filing requirement |

### Content Standards

Every communication must be fair, balanced, and not misleading. Must provide a sound basis for evaluating the product. Cannot omit material facts. Cannot predict or project performance (with very limited exceptions for hypothetical illustrations). Must disclose conflicts of interest and include firm name and contact information.

Communications must be retained for **3 years** -- this applies to correspondence, retail communications, and institutional communications alike.

## Part 8: Conduct Rules -- The Do's and Don'ts

### Gifts and Gratuities

The **$100 limit** is one of the most tested rules: registered representatives are prohibited from giving or receiving gifts worth more than $100 per person per year to or from employees of another firm.

Permissible exceptions:
- Promotional items with firm logo (pens, caps, apparel)
- Occasional gifts of minimal value if records are kept

Note: This $100 limit applies between broker-dealer employees. The SEC has separate rules for gifts to customers.

### Borrowing and Lending

General rule: representatives cannot lend money to or borrow money from customers. Exceptions require written approval from the firm and a written agreement between the parties:

| Exception | Requirements |
|---|---|
| Immediate family member | Permitted (parents, children, siblings, in-laws) |
| Customer is a bank or financial institution | Permitted (they're in the business of lending) |
| Both parties are registered persons at the same firm | Permitted with firm approval |

### Sharing in Customer Accounts

Representatives generally cannot share in the profits or losses of customer accounts. Sharing is permitted when:
- The customer provides prior written consent
- The firm provides prior written approval
- Sharing is **proportionate to capital contribution**
- Exception: Proportionality requirement waived for immediate family members

### Guarantees Against Loss

Representatives **cannot guarantee a customer against losses**. This prohibition applies to formal and informal agreements alike. Promising to "make a customer whole" if an investment loses money is a serious violation.

### Outside Business Activities (Rule 3270)

Registered persons must provide **prior written notice** to their firm before engaging in any outside business activity that involves compensation -- including board memberships, family businesses, or part-time work.

The firm must evaluate whether the activity could interfere with responsibilities, be viewed by the public as part of the firm's business, or create conflicts. The firm can approve, approve with conditions, or deny the activity.

Exceptions: Charitable work without compensation and passive investments (where you invest but play no active role, like being a limited partner in a DPP).

### Private Securities Transactions -- "Selling Away" (Rule 3280)

Associated persons must provide **prior written notice** to the firm before participating in any securities transaction outside the regular scope of employment.

The distinction between compensation and no compensation is heavily tested:

- **If NO selling compensation is involved:** The firm can simply acknowledge and allow it.
- **If selling compensation IS involved:** The firm MUST specifically approve the transaction AND record it on the firm's books and records AND supervise it as if it were the firm's own business.

Selling away without firm approval is one of the most serious violations -- it can result in a permanent bar from the industry.

### Non-Cash Compensation

Compensation for selling pooled products (variable annuities, mutual funds, DPPs) is strictly regulated. No associated person may accept compensation from anyone other than their employer unless the firm agrees, payments go through the firm, and records are maintained.

Permissible non-cash compensation:
- Gifts not exceeding $100 in value per year
- Occasional meals or entertainment that are not frequent or expensive
- Training or educational event reimbursements (no guests allowed)
- Compensation not based on sales of a specific brand or product

## Part 9: Anti-Money Laundering (Rule 3310)

Every member firm must develop and implement a written AML compliance program with four minimum requirements:

1. **Policies, procedures, and internal controls** reasonably designed to detect and cause reporting of suspicious transactions
2. **Designation of an AML Compliance Officer (AMLCO)** -- must be a registered principal
3. **Ongoing AML training** for appropriate personnel
4. **Independent testing** of the AML program (annually for most firms)

### Customer Identification Program (CIP)

Required under the USA PATRIOT Act (Section 326). Must verify the identity of each customer opening an account. Four required pieces of information:
- Name
- Date of birth
- Address
- Government-issued ID number (SSN for U.S. persons, passport or alien ID for non-U.S.)

### Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs)

Must file with FinCEN within **30 days** of detecting suspicious activity involving **$5,000 or more**. It is a **federal crime** to "tip off" a customer that a SAR has been filed. This is not optional -- the firm cannot decide to handle it internally instead.

### Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs)

Required for cash transactions exceeding **$10,000** in a single business day. **Structuring** -- breaking transactions into smaller amounts to avoid reporting -- is illegal.

### OFAC (Office of Foreign Assets Control)

Firms must screen customers against the Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) list. Transactions with persons on the SDN list are prohibited.

## Part 10: Customer Complaints, Disputes, and Resolution

### Customer Complaints

A customer complaint is any written statement from a customer alleging a grievance involving the firm's activities or representatives. Key rules:

- A **principal must handle all complaints**, regardless of how minor they seem
- Firms must report to FINRA within 30 days if aware of complaints involving allegations of theft, forgery, or misappropriation of funds, or claims for damages over $15,000 (against an associated person) or $25,000 (against the firm)
- Statistical and summary information must be filed quarterly
- Customer complaint records must be maintained at the OSJ for a minimum of **4 years** under FINRA rules
- The MSRB requires **6 years** for municipal complaints -- know both numbers

### Rule 4530 -- Reporting Requirements

Firms must report specified events to FINRA, including:
- Statistical and summary information regarding customer complaints (quarterly)
- Internal disciplinary actions involving suspension, termination, withholding compensation over $2,500, or fines over $2,500
- Any finding by a securities regulator that the firm or associated person violated securities laws
- Any criminal indictment, conviction, or guilty plea

### FINRA Arbitration (Rule 12000 Series)

FINRA operates the largest securities dispute resolution forum in the United States. Arbitration is required when there's a written agreement to arbitrate, OR when the dispute is between a customer and a member/associated person and the customer requests arbitration.

Key details that get tested:

- Claims must be filed within **6 years** of the event giving rise to the dispute (the eligibility rule)
- **Simplified arbitration:** Claims of **$50,000 or less** are decided by a single arbitrator based on written submissions (no hearing required unless requested)
- **Standard arbitration:** Claims over $50,000 but not exceeding $100,000 use one arbitrator with a hearing; claims over $100,000 use three arbitrators
- Arbitration awards are **final, binding, and subject to very limited court review**
- Industry disputes (between two firms or between a firm and a registered person) are handled under a separate code (Rule 13000 series)

### Mediation

A voluntary, non-binding process facilitated by a neutral mediator. Either side can withdraw at any time. It's less formal, typically faster and cheaper than arbitration. If mediation fails, the parties can still proceed to arbitration.

### FINRA Disciplinary Process (Code of Procedure)

The Code of Procedure handles complaints alleging FINRA rule violations -- these are disciplinary matters, not customer money disputes. The process:

1. Investigation (Market Regulation or Enforcement)
2. Wells Notice (gives the respondent an opportunity to respond before formal charges)
3. Complaint filed
4. Answer filed by respondent (within 25 days)
5. Hearing before a FINRA hearing panel
6. Decision issued

**Appeal path:** National Adjudicatory Council (NAC) at FINRA headquarters, then SEC, then federal court of appeals.

### Sanctions FINRA Can Impose

1. **Censure:** Official reprimand on public record
2. **Fine:** Monetary penalty (no statutory maximum)
3. **Suspension:** Up to 2 years; anything longer should be a bar
4. **Bar:** Permanent prohibition from associating with any FINRA member firm
5. **Expulsion:** Permanent removal of a member firm
6. **Disgorgement:** Requiring return of ill-gotten gains
7. **Restitution:** Requiring compensation to harmed customers

### Settlement Options

**Minor Rule Violation (MRV) Letters:** For minor complaints, the respondent signs an MRV letter accepting a finding of violation, admits guilt, and pays a maximum **$2,500 fine**.

**Acceptance, Waiver, and Consent (AWC):** Allows the respondent to accept findings and waive appeal rights **without admitting guilt**.

## Part 11: MSRB Rules -- Municipal Securities Regulation

The Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board creates rules for broker-dealers, underwriters, investment advisers, and their representatives who deal in municipal securities. Remember the key distinction: the MSRB makes the rules but doesn't enforce them. FINRA and the SEC enforce for broker-dealers; bank regulators enforce for banks.

The MSRB also has no regulatory authority over municipal issuers themselves -- only over the professionals who work with municipalities.

### Key MSRB Rules Tested on Series 7

| Rule | Topic |
|---|---|
| G-11 | Primary offering practices and syndicate rules |
| G-17 | Conduct of municipal securities activities (fair dealing) |
| G-19 | Suitability of recommendations and transactions |
| G-21 | Advertising by municipal securities dealers |
| G-27 | Supervision |
| G-28 | Transactions with customers of other dealers |
| G-30 | Prices and commissions (fair and reasonable pricing) |
| G-32 | Disclosures in connection with primary offerings (delivery of official statement) |
| G-34 | CUSIP numbers, new issue requirements, and information dissemination |
| G-37 | Political contributions and prohibitions ("pay-to-play") |
| G-38 | Solicitation of municipal securities business (consultants) |

### The Pay-to-Play Rule (G-37)

This is one of the most commonly tested MSRB rules. A **Municipal Finance Professional (MFP)** is anyone who performs financial advisory work or underwriting for municipal issuers.

- An MFP can contribute up to **$250 per election** to a candidate they're entitled to vote for
- Contributions above $250 (or any amount to candidates they cannot vote for) trigger a **2-year ban** on municipal business with that issuer
- The ban applies to the entire firm, not just the individual who made the contribution
- Contributions must be disclosed quarterly to the MSRB

A registered representative who only deals with retail clients is not an MFP. But the moment they negotiate underwriting terms with a municipal issuer, they become one and subject to the rule.

### EMMA (Electronic Municipal Market Access)

EMMA is the MSRB's free website providing retail investors access to:
- Official statements for most municipal bonds, notes, and 529 plans
- Details on pre-refunded bonds
- Ongoing issuer disclosures and financial information
- Real-time prices and yields via RTRS (Real-Time Transaction Reporting System)
- Investor education resources

### MSRB Trading and Market Rules

- **Regular way settlement:** T+1 (cash transactions settle same day)
- **Good delivery:** Securities must be in good physical condition and properly endorsed
- **Published quotes:** Must generally be firm bids or offers
- **Nominal quotes (BW/OW):** Given for informational purposes only, not firm commitments
- **Transaction reporting:** All trades reported to MSRB's RTRS

### MSRB Record Retention (Different from FINRA)

| Record Type | MSRB Retention |
|---|---|
| Customer complaints | **6 years** |
| Advertising | **4 years** |
| 529/ABLE plan filings | 6 years |

Compare to FINRA's 3-year retention for general communications and 4-year retention for customer complaints.

### Comparing the Regulators

| | SEC | FINRA | MSRB |
|---|---|---|---|
| Type | Federal agency | SRO (private) | SRO (private) |
| Writes rules | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Enforces rules | Yes | Yes | **No** |
| Creates exams | No | Yes | Yes |
| Can impose jail time | No (refers to DOJ) | No | No |

## Part 12: SIPC -- What It Is and What It Isn't

SIPC is not an SRO and not a regulator, but it's heavily tested. Created by the Securities Investor Protection Act of 1970, SIPC is a nonprofit membership corporation funded by assessments on member broker-dealers.

### Coverage Limits

- **$500,000 per customer** (per separate account capacity)
- Of that, a maximum of **$250,000 for cash claims**
- Securities are covered up to the full $500,000

The cash limit is within the total, not on top of it. If a customer has $400,000 in securities and $300,000 in cash at a failed firm, SIPC covers the $400,000 in securities but only $100,000 of the cash (to stay within the $500,000 total), not $250,000. Many exam questions test this distinction.

### What SIPC Covers

SIPC protects customers if a FINRA-member broker-dealer fails financially (insolvency/liquidation). It replaces missing securities and cash from customer accounts.

### What SIPC Does NOT Cover

- Losses from market declines or bad investment advice
- Commodity futures contracts or currency
- Fixed annuity contracts
- Investment contracts (limited partnerships) not registered with the SEC

### Separate Capacities

Each legally distinct account type -- individual, joint, IRA, Roth IRA, trust -- is treated as a separate customer for SIPC purposes, each getting up to $500,000 coverage.

**SIPC is not FDIC.** FDIC insures bank deposits. SIPC protects brokerage account assets. They are completely separate programs.

## Part 13: New Issue Rules (Rules 5130 and 5131)

### Rule 5130 -- IPO Restrictions

Prohibits member firms from selling new issues (IPOs) to "restricted persons":

- FINRA member firms and their associated persons (and immediate family members)
- Finders or fiduciaries of the managing underwriter
- Portfolio managers (anyone with authority to buy/sell securities for institutional accounts)
- Owners of broker-dealers (10%+ ownership)

Exceptions include certain de minimis exemptions and investment companies registered under the Investment Company Act.

The purpose is to prevent "spinning" -- allocating hot IPO shares to insiders or people who can steer future business to the underwriter.

### Rule 5131 -- New Issue Allocations and Distributions

Addresses spinning and quid pro quo allocations specifically. Prohibits allocating IPO shares as a condition of receiving future business (investment banking, brokerage commissions, etc.).

## Part 14: Customer Account Information (Rule 4512)

Firms must maintain the following for each customer account:
- Customer's name and residence (or principal place of business)
- Whether customer is of legal age
- Name(s) of associated person(s) responsible for the account
- Signature of a principal denoting the account has been accepted
- For discretionary accounts: date discretionary authority was granted
- For margin accounts: signed margin agreement

For natural persons, firms must also make reasonable efforts to obtain:
- Tax ID/SSN
- Occupation
- Name and address of employer
- Whether the customer is an associated person of another member firm

## Part 15: Record Retention -- The Numbers That Matter

The exam loves to test specific retention periods. Here's the complete picture:

### FINRA Record Retention

| Record Type | Retention Period |
|---|---|
| General records / Correspondence / Communications | 3 years |
| Customer complaints | 4 years |
| Account records | Lifetime of account + 6 years |
| Customer identification records | Lifetime of account + 5 years |

## Part 16: Where SROs Appear on the Series 7 Exam

SRO content is not concentrated in one function -- it's woven throughout:

**Function 1 (7% -- 9 questions):** Communications standards (FINRA Rules 2210-2220), Cboe options communication rules, MSRB Rule G-21, IPO/new issue rules (5130, 5131).

**Function 2 (9% -- 11 questions):** Account opening, KYC (Rule 2090), suitability (Rule 2111), Reg BI, customer account information (Rule 4512), AML/CIP requirements.

**Function 3 (73% -- 91 questions):** While mostly product knowledge, this section weaves in suitability analysis, recommendations, margin rules, and regulatory requirements. Municipal securities questions will reference MSRB rules.

**Function 4 (11% -- 14 questions):** Trade processing, settlement, regulatory requirements for transactions, reporting obligations, customer complaints.

Conservatively, 15-25 questions will directly test regulatory knowledge. Many more will have regulatory concepts embedded in product-specific scenarios.

## Part 17: The 15 Exam Traps

These are the concepts candidates most commonly get wrong. If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these.

1. **FINRA is NOT a government agency.** It's a private, not-for-profit SRO authorized by the SEC. The SEC is the government agency.

2. **MSRB writes rules but does NOT enforce them.** FINRA enforces MSRB rules for broker-dealers. Bank regulators enforce for banks.

3. **SIPC is NOT an SRO and NOT a regulator.** It provides account protection only when a broker-dealer fails. It does NOT protect against market losses.

4. **SIPC cash limit is $250,000 WITHIN the $500,000 total.** It's not $500,000 + $250,000. Total maximum per customer is $500,000.

5. **Reg BI applies only to RETAIL customers.** Institutional customers are still governed by suitability rules.

6. **Rule 2111 has THREE types of suitability.** Reasonable-basis, customer-specific, AND quantitative. Quantitative suitability (the anti-churning provision) is the one people forget.

7. **The correspondence vs. retail communication threshold is 25 retail investors in 30 days.** 25 or fewer = correspondence. More than 25 = retail communication. Different approval requirements.

8. **Private securities transactions with compensation require firm approval AND supervision.** Without compensation, only acknowledgment is required.

9. **SAR threshold is $5,000 and you CANNOT tip off the customer.** CTR threshold is $10,000 for cash transactions.

10. **Form U5 must be filed within 30 days of termination.** Form U4 amendments for statutory disqualification events must be filed within 10 days.

11. **Arbitration eligibility has a 6-year time limit.** Claims filed more than 6 years after the event are ineligible.

12. **Simplified arbitration is for claims of $50,000 or less** -- decided on papers only unless a hearing is requested.

13. **The Federal Reserve Board sets initial margin requirements (Reg T), not FINRA.** FINRA sets maintenance margin requirements (Rule 4210).

14. **Continuing education Regulatory Element is now ANNUAL (since 2023).** Failure to complete by December 31 makes the registration INACTIVE.

15. **FINRA can bar an individual permanently but suspensions should not exceed 2 years.** If misconduct warrants more than 2 years, it should be a bar.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **SRO** | Self-Regulatory Organization; industry body that creates and (usually) enforces rules under SEC supervision |
| **FINRA** | Financial Industry Regulatory Authority; main SRO for the securities industry; private, not-for-profit |
| **MSRB** | Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board; creates rules for municipal securities but does not enforce them |
| **SEC** | Securities and Exchange Commission; federal government agency with ultimate authority over all SROs |
| **Form U4** | Registration form filed when joining a firm; creates CRD record |
| **Form U5** | Termination form; must be filed within 30 days of leaving a firm |
| **CRD** | Central Registration Depository; FINRA's system for tracking registrations |
| **BrokerCheck** | FINRA's free public database for researching firms and representatives |
| **OSJ** | Office of Supervisory Jurisdiction; supervisory hub requiring a registered principal |
| **WSP** | Written Supervisory Procedures; required at every firm and branch |
| **CCO** | Chief Compliance Officer; required designation at every firm |
| **Regulatory Element** | FINRA-created CE training; annual completion required |
| **Firm Element** | Annual training plan created by the member firm |
| **MQP** | Maintaining Qualifications Program; extends requalification period from 2 to 5 years |
| **Statutory Disqualification** | Legal prohibition from associating with a member firm based on criminal/regulatory history |
| **Reg BI** | Regulation Best Interest; SEC rule requiring broker-dealers to act in retail customers' best interest |
| **Form CRS** | Customer Relationship Summary; max 2-page disclosure to retail investors |
| **KYC** | Know Your Customer; Rule 2090 requiring reasonable diligence about customer facts |
| **CIP** | Customer Identification Program; USA PATRIOT Act requirement for account opening |
| **SAR** | Suspicious Activity Report; filed with FinCEN for suspicious transactions of $5,000+ |
| **CTR** | Currency Transaction Report; required for cash transactions exceeding $10,000 |
| **AMLCO** | Anti-Money Laundering Compliance Officer; must be a registered principal |
| **OFAC** | Office of Foreign Assets Control; maintains the SDN list |
| **SDN** | Specially Designated Nationals list; transactions with persons on this list are prohibited |
| **Customer complaint** | Written grievance alleging wrongdoing; a principal must handle it |
| **Code of Arbitration** | Process for resolving customer money disputes; binding decision |
| **Code of Procedure** | Process for FINRA rule violations; disciplinary in nature |
| **MRV** | Minor Rule Violation; max $2,500 fine; respondent admits guilt |
| **AWC** | Acceptance, Waiver, and Consent; accept findings without admitting guilt |
| **NAC** | National Adjudicatory Council; FINRA's appellate body |
| **Correspondence** | Communication to 25 or fewer retail investors in 30 days |
| **Retail communication** | Communication to more than 25 retail investors in 30 days |
| **Institutional communication** | Communication distributed only to institutional investors |
| **Selling away** | Private securities transactions outside the firm; Rule 3280 |
| **OBA** | Outside Business Activity; Rule 3270; prior written notice required |
| **EMMA** | Electronic Municipal Market Access; MSRB's free disclosure website |
| **RTRS** | Real-Time Transaction Reporting System; where municipal trades are reported |
| **MFP** | Municipal Finance Professional; subject to G-37 pay-to-play rules |
| **SIPC** | Securities Investor Protection Corporation; not an SRO; provides brokerage account protection |
| **OCC** | Options Clearing Corporation; issues and guarantees all listed options |
| **ODD** | Options Disclosure Document; published by OCC; must be delivered before options approval |
| **Spinning** | Allocating hot IPO shares to insiders; prohibited under Rule 5130 |
