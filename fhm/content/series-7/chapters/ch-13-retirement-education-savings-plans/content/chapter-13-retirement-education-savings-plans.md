# Chapter 13: Retirement and Education Savings Plans

_Series 7 Exam Weight: ~8-10 Questions (5-8%)_

## Section 1: Retirement Plans

In December 1963, the Studebaker automobile company shut down its South Bend, Indiana factory and terminated its pension plan. More than 4,000 workers lost most or all of their promised retirement benefits overnight. Some had worked at the plant for decades. The scandal wasn't that Studebaker went bankrupt—companies fail all the time. The scandal was that nothing in existing law required the company to actually fund the pensions it had promised.

Congress spent the next eleven years debating what to do about it. The result was the <span class="key-term">Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA)</span> of 1974—the single most important piece of retirement legislation in American history. Everything in this chapter flows from that moment: the rules governing IRAs, the structure of employer-sponsored plans, and the regulatory framework that protects workers from another Studebaker.

### Why This Section Matters

The Series 7 exam treats retirement plans as a product knowledge area. You need to know contribution limits, withdrawal penalties, tax treatment, and the critical distinctions between plan types. The questions aren't conceptually difficult, but they're detail-heavy—the kind where mixing up "59 1/2" with "73" costs you the point.

### Topic 1: Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)

#### Contributing to an IRA

Under IRS rules, individuals can set up <span class="key-term">individual retirement accounts (IRAs)</span> to save for retirement on a tax-deferred basis. Depending on income and the availability of a workplace retirement plan, specific individuals may be eligible to deduct their contributions. At withdrawal, all deductible (pre-tax) contributions and earnings are taxed at the individual's ordinary income tax rate. Nondeductible (after-tax) contributions can be withdrawn tax-free. To distinguish them from Roth IRAs, these accounts are commonly referred to as <span class="key-term">traditional IRAs</span>.

The government puts limits on IRA contributions. All contributions must be made only from <span class="key-term">earned income</span>, as opposed to passive income. Earned income typically means wages. <span class="key-term">Unearned income</span> includes dividends, capital gains, interest on securities, Social Security benefits, unemployment benefits, child support, rental income, and inherited funds. All new contributions to an IRA must be made in cash only. Existing assets from a pre-existing IRA may be transferred to another IRA.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> There is no such thing as a joint IRA. IRAs are for individuals and must be separate accounts.</p>
</div>

#### Contribution Limits

Traditional IRA contributions are limited to earned income, up to <span class="key-term">$7,500 per year</span>. Persons aged 50 or above may contribute an extra <span class="key-term">$1,000 annually</span> as a catch-up contribution. In addition, a nonworking spouse can set up a separate IRA, and their working spouse can contribute up to the annual maximum with their earned income (with a catch-up contribution if qualified). Excess contributions are taxed at <span class="key-term">6% per year</span> as long as they remain in the account.

If an individual is participating in another qualified retirement plan, such as a 401(k), they can open an IRA and make contributions up to the limit, but their contributions may not be tax-deductible. The deduction amount will depend on the individual's income level.

#### Suitable Investments for an IRA

Only cash or cash equivalents can be deposited into an IRA. However, once the funds are in the account, they can be used to buy securities. IRAs can even be used to purchase real estate.

Collectibles, such as works of art and stamps, and life insurance can never be purchased in an IRA. In addition, naked calls are also prohibited trades in an IRA. However, writing covered calls and buying protective puts are permitted. Although the purchase of municipal bonds is allowed, they are typically not suitable, given the tax-deferred nature of an IRA—you'd be wasting the muni's built-in tax advantage inside an account that's already tax-sheltered.

#### Withdrawing Funds from a Traditional IRA

Most individuals will make pre-tax contributions to their traditional IRA. Any after-tax contributions can be withdrawn tax-free, but the individual must show evidence that taxes were paid on those contributions. While individuals can withdraw funds at any time from their accounts, if they make withdrawals before the age of <span class="key-term">59 1/2</span>, they will be taxed on both their pre-tax contributions and their earnings and will face an additional <span class="key-term">10% tax penalty</span>. Individuals can avoid the 10% penalty if the withdrawal is for one of the following reasons:

- Qualified education expenses (for example, tuition, books, and supplies)
- Payment of medical expenses (if they are more than 7.5% of the individual's gross income)
- Permanent disability
- First-time home purchase ($10,000 lifetime maximum)
- Payment of health insurance premiums by an unemployed or self-employed person who has received state or federal aid for 3 consecutive months
- The withdrawals occur in substantially equal periodic payments (IRS Rule 72(t)) continuously for 5 years
- A person who has a child (including by adoption) can withdraw up to $5,000 from their 401(k) or IRA without penalty. This benefit must be used within 1 year of the date of birth or adoption, and the $5,000 limit applies separately to each parent.

#### Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

<div class="historical-note">
  <div class="historical-note__label">Why RMDs Exist</div>
  <p>Congress created Required Minimum Distributions because IRAs were meant to fund retirement, not serve as perpetual tax shelters to pass wealth to heirs. Without RMDs, a wealthy individual could park money in an IRA indefinitely, deferring taxes for decades while the account compounds. The government wants its tax revenue eventually.</p>
</div>

The first distribution from the plan must be taken no later than <span class="key-term">April 1 of the year following the year in which the participant turns 73</span>. All subsequent distributions must occur by December 31 each year. The amount of the withdrawal is calculated by the IRS. Failure to take an RMD on time results in a tax equal to <span class="key-term">25% of the undistributed RMD</span>.

<div class="critical-concept">
  <div class="critical-concept__label">MEMORIZE THIS</div>
  <p><strong>RMD penalty = 25%</strong> of the amount that should have been withdrawn but wasn't. This is one of the harshest penalties in the tax code—memorize the number.</p>
</div>

#### Retirement Plan Transfer vs. Rollover

A person can <span class="key-term">transfer</span> their IRA directly into another IRA at a different bank or broker-dealer. If the individual is leaving a company, retirement plan assets can also be directly transferred into a conduit IRA. When assets are transferred directly from one account to another, there is no tax liability. The number of transfers that can be made is unlimited. Transfers may also be referred to as <span class="key-term">direct rollovers</span>.

Another method of transferring retirement assets is a <span class="key-term">rollover</span>. In this case, a check is sent directly to the participant, and 20% of the rollover will be withheld. The money must be redeposited within <span class="key-term">60 days</span> of the distribution date, or the entire amount will be taxable and possibly subject to early withdrawal penalties. Rollovers can only occur <span class="key-term">once per year</span>.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Know the difference between a transfer (direct, unlimited, no withholding) and a rollover (indirect, 20% withheld, 60-day deadline, once per year). The exam loves to test this distinction.</p>
</div>

#### Traditional IRA: Death of IRA Owner

What happens if an IRA holder dies? As a general rule, the recipient of the IRA must deplete the assets over 10 years or less and pay taxes due, but no penalty tax applies if the recipient is under age 59 1/2. However, spouses have more tax-friendly options available to them.

**Spousal beneficiary:** The spouse has 3 choices based on age and need:

1. A younger spouse may roll over the account into their existing IRA. Therefore, if the spouse is 53 years old, RMDs are not required to begin until 20 years later.
2. An older spouse with sufficient income may open an <span class="key-term">inherited IRA</span>, which is depleted over a 10-year period. So, if the spouse is 80 years old with a 2-year life expectancy, this choice "stretches" the payout window by 8 years, reducing annual tax liability.
3. Wealthy persons may refuse to accept (disclaim) the IRA.

**Non-spousal beneficiary:** The non-spouse has 3 choices:

1. Transfer the IRA into an inherited IRA and deplete the assets within 10 years.
2. Cash out the IRA and pay taxes due.
3. Wealthy persons may refuse to accept (disclaim) the IRA.

#### Roth IRAs

Unlike traditional IRAs, Roth IRA contributions are <span class="key-term">never tax-deductible</span>. In other words, contributions to a Roth IRA are made with after-tax dollars. However, if the account has been held for 5 years or more and the individual is at least age 59 1/2, earnings may be withdrawn <span class="key-term">tax-free and penalty-free</span>. The following rules apply to Roth IRAs:

- Contributions are never tax-deductible
- Contributions are limited to earned income
- Contribution limits are the same as traditional IRAs
- Contributions can be withdrawn tax-free without penalty at any time
- Participants with income over certain levels (high-earners) are not eligible to contribute
- There are <span class="key-term">no required minimum distributions</span>

Individuals may contribute to both a traditional and a Roth IRA, but they cannot contribute more than the annual maximum combined across both accounts. Roth IRAs have the same annual contribution limits as traditional IRAs.

### Topic 2: Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plans

<div class="historical-note">
  <div class="historical-note__label">Historical Context</div>
  <p>Before ERISA, employers could promise pensions and simply never fund them. The law changed that by establishing minimum standards for retirement plans in private industry—covering participation, vesting, benefit accrual, and fiduciary responsibility. It also created the Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation (PBGC) to insure defined benefit plans. Essentially, Congress said: if you're going to promise workers a retirement, you have to actually put money aside for it.</p>
</div>

#### ERISA Requirements

<span class="key-term">ERISA (Employee Retirement Income Security Act)</span> establishes minimum standards for retirement plans in private industry. Not all plans are subject to ERISA—notably, <span class="key-term">457 plans</span> for state and local government employees are exempt. Plans subject to ERISA must meet requirements including fiduciary responsibilities and minimum vesting standards.

A person who is <span class="key-term">vested</span> has worked for the company long enough for the employer's contributions to become fully theirs. The employee's own contributions always vest immediately.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Remember: 457 plans are NOT subject to ERISA. This is a favorite exam question. The 401(k), 403(b), SEP, SIMPLE, ESOP, and profit-sharing plans ARE subject to ERISA.</p>
</div>

#### Defined Benefit Plans

In a <span class="key-term">defined benefit plan</span>, the employer promises a specific retirement benefit—typically a percentage of salary multiplied by years of service. The <span class="key-term">employer bears the investment risk</span> because they must deliver on the promised benefit regardless of how the underlying investments perform. These plans are increasingly rare, having largely been replaced by defined contribution plans. Think traditional pensions—the kind your grandparents might have had.

#### Defined Contribution Plans

In a <span class="key-term">defined contribution plan</span>, the employer and/or the employee contribute specific amounts to an individual account. The retirement benefit depends entirely on investment performance. The <span class="key-term">employee bears the investment risk</span>. Common types include:

- <span class="key-term">401(k)</span> plans — for employees of private companies (subject to ERISA)
- <span class="key-term">403(b)</span> plans — for employees of tax-exempt organizations such as schools, hospitals, and nonprofits; also known as <span class="key-term">Tax-Sheltered Annuity (TSA)</span> plans (subject to ERISA)
- <span class="key-term">457</span> plans — for state and local government employees (NOT subject to ERISA)

Qualified withdrawals from a defined contribution plan are taxed as <span class="key-term">ordinary income</span>. Early withdrawals before age 59 1/2 are generally subject to a 10% penalty in addition to regular taxes.

<div class="critical-concept">
  <div class="critical-concept__label">MEMORIZE THIS</div>
  <p><strong>Who bears the investment risk?</strong><br>
  Defined <em>benefit</em> plan = <strong>employer</strong> bears the risk (they promised a specific benefit)<br>
  Defined <em>contribution</em> plan = <strong>employee</strong> bears the risk (they own the account, for better or worse)</p>
</div>

#### Profit Sharing Plans

In a <span class="key-term">profit sharing plan</span>, employer contributions are discretionary and typically based on company profits. These plans are subject to ERISA.

#### Qualified Plans for Small Businesses and the Self-Employed

**SEP IRA (Simplified Employee Pension):** A <span class="key-term">SEP IRA</span> is designed for small businesses and self-employed individuals. Only the employer makes contributions—there are no employee contributions. Subject to ERISA.

**SIMPLE IRA (Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees):** A <span class="key-term">SIMPLE IRA</span> is designed for businesses with 100 or fewer employees. Both employer and employee can contribute. Subject to ERISA.

#### Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)

In an <span class="key-term">ESOP</span>, the company contributes its own stock to employee accounts. This gives employees an ownership stake in the company. ESOPs are subject to ERISA.

#### Nonqualified Retirement Plans

<span class="key-term">Nonqualified retirement plans</span> are NOT subject to ERISA. Employer contributions are not tax-deductible until the employee actually receives the benefit. <span class="key-term">Deferred compensation plans</span>—where an employer promises to pay an employee at a later date—are the most common type. These plans are typically used for highly compensated executives, which is probably why nobody rushed to regulate them.

## Section 2: Variable Annuities

In 1952, the Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association (TIAA) launched the College Retirement Equities Fund (CREF)—the world's first variable annuity. The idea was radical at the time: instead of promising retirees a fixed monthly check that inflation would slowly erode, why not tie the payments to the stock market? If equities grew over time (and historically they had), retirees could maintain their purchasing power. The insurance industry had just built a bridge to Wall Street—and regulators have been trying to figure out who's in charge ever since.

### Why This Section Matters

Variable annuities sit at the intersection of insurance and securities, which means they're regulated by both industries. The Series 7 exam will test you on the mechanics (accumulation units vs. annuity units, AIR, separate accounts), the tax treatment (LIFO for random withdrawals, ordinary income only), and the suitability rules. Expect 3-5 questions from this section alone.

---

### Topic 1: Annuity Basics

An <span class="key-term">annuity</span> is an investment contract between an individual and an insurance company. As part of the contract, the owner invests money with the insurance company, either as a lump sum or in periodic payments. When the owner reaches retirement age, the insurance company will begin distributing payments. Payments may be made monthly, in a lump sum, or on a schedule agreed upon by the contract owner. The size of the payments depends on the amount deposited and any accumulated growth.

There are two types of annuities: <span class="key-term">fixed annuities</span> and <span class="key-term">variable annuities</span>.

#### Fixed Annuity: Guaranteed Rate of Return

In a fixed annuity contract, the insurer guarantees a specific rate of return to the investor. No matter how good or bad the investments funding the annuity perform, the investor earns the same guaranteed rate of return.

The insurance company invests the customer's <span class="key-term">annuity premiums</span> into the company's <span class="key-term">general account</span>. The insurance company chooses the investments and assumes the investment risk, so a fixed annuity is <span class="key-term">not considered a security</span>—it is an insurance product regulated by insurance laws only.

<span class="key-term">Indexed annuities</span> are a type of fixed annuity that tracks an index and pays a rate of return based on the performance of that index. Since indexed annuities are also considered to be fixed annuities, they are not subject to securities laws. Many of these contracts offer a guaranteed minimum rate of growth, but the downside is that investors often only earn a percentage of the equity index return in a given year.

At retirement, the owner may choose to <span class="key-term">annuitize</span> the contract and begin receiving periodic payments. Because insurance companies tend to invest conservatively in fixed-income securities, fixed annuities usually have a lower rate of return. Moreover, the payments are typically fixed and are adjusted for inflation. This means that the annuitant is subject to <span class="key-term">purchasing power risk</span>, known as <span class="key-term">inflation risk</span>—the risk that future fixed payments will be worth less in real dollar terms.

#### Variable Annuity: No Guaranteed Rate of Return

In a variable annuity contract, there is no guarantee of a specific rate of return. Customers are given a menu of mutual fund-like investments to choose from. If the investments perform better than expected, the annuity value increases. If the investments funding the annuity perform worse than expected, the annuity value decreases. Hence the name variable annuity.

#### Variable Annuity Regulation

Since the investor selects the investment vehicles to be used, variable annuities are <span class="key-term">considered to be securities</span> that are regulated under securities laws. Unlike fixed annuities, variable annuities must be sold under a <span class="key-term">prospectus</span>. In addition, individuals who sell any type of annuity must be state registered to sell insurance products. Sellers of variable insurance products must also be registered to sell securities (i.e., Series 6 or Series 7).

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Fixed annuity = insurance product only (general account, NOT a security). Variable annuity = both insurance product AND security (separate account, requires prospectus). This distinction drives dozens of exam questions.</p>
</div>

### Topic 2: Variable Annuity Phases

Funds from variable annuities are kept in accounts that are separate from the insurance company's <span class="key-term">general account</span> and are often referred to as <span class="key-term">separate accounts</span>. Within a separate account, the investor chooses from several possible <span class="key-term">subaccounts</span> that operate similarly to mutual funds. For variable annuities, the investor, rather than the insurance company, bears the investment risk.

A variable annuity has two phases: the <span class="key-term">accumulation phase</span> and the <span class="key-term">distribution phase</span>.

#### Accumulation Phase

In the first phase, the customer contributes money to the annuity account and purchases a variable number of <span class="key-term">accumulation units</span>. As the customer contributes more, they will purchase more accumulation units. The units will grow (or shrink) in value depending on the performance of the separate subaccount investments chosen. The contract will grow tax-deferred, with taxes being paid on earnings upon distribution.

Clients often have the option to purchase "riders" on a contract. A <span class="key-term">rider</span> is an additional benefit such as a guaranteed minimum growth rate or insurance against a market downturn. These riders add to the overall cost of the contract. One popular rider is a <span class="key-term">guaranteed minimum income benefit (GMIB)</span>, which guarantees a minimum level of payments once a contract has been annuitized, regardless of market conditions.

#### Distribution Phase

Customers typically wait until they reach retirement age to begin receiving distributions from their annuity. These distributions may come in a lump sum or the form of random withdrawals. Customers may choose to <span class="key-term">annuitize</span> the contract.

If the customer chooses annuitization, they become an <span class="key-term">annuitant</span> and will be promised a series of payments over a specified period or for the remainder of their life. The accumulation units will be converted into a fixed number of <span class="key-term">annuity units</span> based on the life expectancy of the annuitant. The monthly payments will depend on the performance of the investments in the customer's separate account, the age and sex of the customer, and the customer's choice of payment.

Typically, customers make contributions to a variable annuity with after-tax dollars. Taxes on income earned in the annuity are deferred until the money is withdrawn. There is a <span class="key-term">10% penalty</span> on the earnings portion of withdrawals made before the age of 59 1/2. Once earnings are withdrawn, they are taxed at the customer's ordinary income tax rate.

Variable annuities are considered to be both an insurance product and a security and are subject to the regulations of both industries. On the security side, variable annuities are regulated by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA), as well as by each state.

#### General vs. Separate Accounts

<div class="critical-concept">
  <div class="critical-concept__label">MEMORIZE THIS</div>
  <p><strong>Fixed annuity → General account.</strong> The customer's investment is deposited into the insurance company's general account. The insurance company manages these funds in the same pool as its funds for other insurance products. Annuitants receive a fixed monthly payment for life, guaranteed by the insurance company. The <strong>insurance company bears the investment risk</strong>.</p>
  <p><strong>Variable annuity → Separate account.</strong> The funds are held in a separate account from other insurance products. The annuity owner is taking on the investment risk, but they are not at risk should the insurance company experience financial difficulties. Remember: <strong>all separate account products are securities</strong>, which require prospectus delivery at time of sale.</p>
</div>

### Topic 3: Characteristics of Variable Annuities

#### Death Benefit

If the annuitant dies during the accumulation period, before the insurance company has begun making payments, the beneficiary will receive a payment from the insurance company, which bears the <span class="key-term">mortality risk</span> of the contract. This is called a <span class="key-term">death benefit</span>. The payment the beneficiary receives will equal the total investments and any additional earnings from the annuity. If the annuity loses money, the beneficiary will receive the total invested money. The beneficiary is responsible for paying income taxes on any earnings.

In some cases, an annuitant may choose to annuitize a contract and receive payments for a specific period (e.g., 20 years). If the annuitant dies during the distribution phase, the named beneficiary will continue to receive the annuity payments. Annuitization payments are not considered a death benefit.

#### Purchasing Annuities

#### Deferred Annuity Surrender Periods

With a deferred annuity, the annuitant may not withdraw funds for up to 10 years. The length of time that the investor must wait before withdrawing money from the annuity without penalty is called the <span class="key-term">surrender period</span>. If the annuitant needs the money before the end of the surrender period, they can surrender the contract by paying a <span class="key-term">surrender fee</span>. The <span class="key-term">surrender value</span> of the account will be the account's value at the time of surrender minus the surrender fee.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Customers who need income in the near future, such as the elderly, should not consider deferred annuities.</p>
</div>

#### Free-Look Period

Variable annuities typically have a <span class="key-term">free-look period</span>, which allows customers to cancel the policy within a specified time frame at no charge. The duration of the free-look may be 10 days or more, depending on state law.

#### Annuity Payout Options (Settlement Options)

If the owner chooses to annuitize the contract, they must select a method of receiving payments. The choice of payout option will determine the amount of the monthly check the annuitant will receive. These options include the following:

- <span class="key-term">Life income</span>: The insurer pays the annuitant for the remainder of their lifetime. When the annuitant dies, benefits stop. This option is likely to be the shortest period, and so it will be the least expensive for the insurance company. In this case, the annuitant will receive the <strong>largest monthly check</strong>.

- <span class="key-term">Life with period certain</span>: The insurer pays the annuitant an income during their lifetime. But if the annuitant dies before the <span class="key-term">period certain</span> expires, payments will continue to be made to a beneficiary.

- <span class="key-term">Joint life with last survivor</span>: This option basically covers two lifetimes. The insurer will continue to pay a benefit until both covered parties have died. A married couple typically chooses this option. The joint life with last survivor option will have the <strong>smallest monthly checks</strong> because payments will continue through the life of the last survivor.

<div class="info-box">
  <div class="info-box__title">Real-World Example</div>
  <p>The annuitant chooses life with a period certain of 20 years. The annuitant will receive a payment for life. However, if they die within the next 20 years, the named beneficiary will receive payments for the remainder of the period. An annuitant who chooses life with period certain will receive medium-sized monthly checks.</p>
</div>

#### Assumed Interest Rate (AIR)

Once the payout option has been selected and the contract has been annuitized, the initial payment must be calculated using the <span class="key-term">assumed interest rate (AIR)</span>. When the contract is annuitized, the insurance company estimates future investment earnings. The AIR is expressed in percentage terms as an annual "projected" rate of growth.

<span class="key-term">AIR is fixed.</span> Once set, the AIR does not change. If the assets continue to perform in line with the AIR, the insurer will send the annuitant a check for the same amount each period. More likely, the earnings on the annuity units will vary, and the annuitant's check for each period will change. Some insurers adjust the amount of the payments annually, while others make changes every month.

Key AIR rules:

**AIR applies to annuity units only.** During the accumulation phase, the value of accumulation units increases if the separate account earns any positive return; AIR does not apply. After annuitization, the annuity unit value increases only if the return exceeds AIR. If the annuity unit's return is below AIR, the annuity unit value falls.

**AIR is not a guarantee.** The AIR shown in a variable annuity prospectus illustrates the payment to be made if the separate account performs at that rate. It is a conservative estimate, but it is not a guaranteed minimum rate of return.

<div class="critical-concept">
  <div class="critical-concept__label">MEMORIZE THIS</div>
  <p><strong>Payment based on actual performance vs. projected AIR:</strong></p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Perform in line with the AIR</strong> → the amount of the next payment is the same</li>
    <li><strong>Outperform the AIR</strong> → the amount of the next payment will increase from the prior payment</li>
    <li><strong>Underperform the AIR</strong> → the amount of the next payment will decrease from the prior payment</li>
  </ul>
</div>

### Topic 4: Variable Annuity Rules and Regulations

#### Federal Regulations Regarding Variable Annuities

Under the <span class="key-term">Securities Act of 1933</span>, variable annuities are considered to be securities and, therefore, need to be registered with the SEC and must always be sold with a prospectus. Under the <span class="key-term">Securities Exchange Act of 1934</span> and the <span class="key-term">Investment Company Act of 1940</span>, these contracts are securities and may only be sold by a registered broker-dealer and their registered representatives. The broker-dealer must be a member of FINRA.

As a separate business entity, variable annuities must keep the invested premiums in a separate account from the insurance company's fixed annuity and other insurance accounts. Securities can only be bought and sold within this separate account.

#### Recommending Deferred Variable Annuities

When recommending a deferred variable annuity to a customer, a registered representative must describe the annuity's specific features. These include:

- Potential surrender period and surrender charge
- Potential tax penalty if customers make withdrawals before reaching the age of 59 1/2
- The various costs and fees (variable annuities have higher fees than regular mutual fund investments, which can be made within an IRA account)
- Subject to market risk

A registered representative must have a reasonable basis to believe that a customer would benefit from their features, such as tax-deferred growth and a death benefit. The registered representative must determine the suitability of a deferred annuity, taking into consideration surrender periods and charges, additional fees, and whether there have been any contract exchanges within the last 3 years.

A registered principal must approve a customer's application for a deferred annuity before it is sent to the insurance company. A customer's suitability must be considered. Approval (or not) must occur within 7 business days of receiving the application.

#### Suitability

When recommending a fixed annuity vs. a variable annuity, a representative must know the customer's risk tolerance. A fixed annuity is suitable for a risk-averse investor who is seeking guaranteed fixed payments for life. A variable annuity is suitable for customers who are willing to accept investment risk in exchange for the potential for higher earnings.

Other suitability factors to consider:

- Annuities with long surrender periods should <strong>not</strong> be sold to elderly customers, who may need to withdraw their money before the surrender period is over
- Annuities may be appropriate for customers who want to be assured of a death benefit
- Customers who need money in the near future should consider only an immediate annuity, not a deferred annuity
- The IRS allows the earnings from an annuity to grow tax-deferred so that annuities may be suitable for individuals in a high tax bracket
- Unlike contributions to IRAs and 401(k)s, contributions to most annuities are not tax-deductible. Generally, a customer should first have reached the maximum amount that can be put into their IRA or 401(k) <strong>before</strong> investing in an annuity
- Because the earnings in an annuity grow tax-deferred, there is no extra tax benefit from holding an annuity inside an IRA or a 401(k)

#### Voting Rights

Owners of variable annuity contracts have the right to vote based on their ownership interest in the separate account. If the separate account is set up to hold mutual fund shares, the owner can vote on both the management of the separate account and the management of the mutual fund it contains.

<span class="key-term">Separate account voting rights</span>: At the separate account level, the owner has the right to vote for the sub-account's board of directors, the investment adviser, and the independent auditor. The owner also has the right to vote on any changes in investment policies.

<span class="key-term">No voting on distributions</span>: Annuity holders do not vote on distributions of dividends and capital gains; the variable annuity's board of trustees or board of managers makes these decisions. The prospectuses for the annuity and the underlying mutual fund describe these voting rights.

#### Taxation

In a <span class="key-term">nonqualified annuity</span>, contributions are made on an after-tax basis. Earnings from nonqualified variable annuities are taxed as ordinary income when they are withdrawn. If the owner chooses to annuitize the contract, each annuity payment will be divided between an earnings portion that is taxed as ordinary income and the remainder (contributions) as a tax-free return of capital.

In a <span class="key-term">qualified annuity</span> (e.g., a 403(b) plan, also known as a tax-sheltered annuity), contributions are made on a pre-tax basis. As a result, all withdrawals are fully taxable as ordinary income.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Withdrawals from any retirement plan or variable insurance product are never subject to capital gains taxes; they are only treated as ordinary income.</p>
</div>

### Topic 5: Variable Life Insurance Contracts

Another insurance product that is also considered a security is <span class="key-term">variable life insurance</span>. As with variable annuities, the premiums paid for insurance coverage are deposited by the insurance company into a separate account. The growth in the separate account's value determines the death benefit that will be paid, so the insurance coverage amount will vary with the performance of the separate account.

#### Variable Life Is a Security

Variable life insurance policies require that a customer be provided with a <span class="key-term">prospectus</span>. The SEC views a variable life policy as a security because the purchaser bears the investment risk and not the insurance company.

#### Permanent Insurance

Variable life insurance, also known as <span class="key-term">permanent insurance</span>, remains in force as long as the premium is paid. Part of the premium covers the cost of insurance, and the balance is invested in a separate account. The premium payment amount is fixed—it does not change. What does vary is the death benefit and the policy's <span class="key-term">cash value</span>. Cash value is the amount that can be borrowed against the policy or withdrawn if the policy is surrendered.

Costs associated with variable life policies are deducted from premiums and may include:

- <span class="key-term">Mortality and expense risk charges</span>: The cost of the death benefit (the life insurance component)
- <span class="key-term">Sales fees</span>: To pay the agent's commission cost
- <span class="key-term">Administrative fees</span>: To cover the insurer's ongoing expenses
- <span class="key-term">State premium fees</span>: Insurance companies are state-regulated, and states often impose a premium tax to pay for the cost of oversight
- <span class="key-term">Investment management fees</span>: The fees charged for running the mutual funds held in the separate account
- <span class="key-term">Rider fees</span>: The cost of optional additional benefits that alter the terms of the policy. The most typical rider is a <span class="key-term">guaranteed minimum death benefit (GMDB)</span>, which promises to grow the separate account at a minimum guaranteed rate (say 4%).

## Section 3: Municipal Fund Securities

In 1996, New Hampshire launched the first state-sponsored 529 college savings plan. The idea was brilliantly simple: let parents invest after-tax dollars in a state-managed account, let the earnings grow tax-free, and make withdrawals tax-free as long as the money goes toward education expenses. It was essentially a Roth IRA for college. Within a decade, every state had one. Today, 529 plans hold over $450 billion in assets—proof that when you give Americans a tax break and tell them it's for their kids, the money shows up.

### Why This Section Matters

Municipal fund securities are a small but reliable portion of the Series 7 exam. The questions focus on 529 plan characteristics (who owns the account, tax treatment, qualified expenses), and the key distinctions between 529 plans, Coverdell ESAs, and ABLE accounts. Know the rules, and these are easy points.

---

### Topic 1: Education Savings Plans

#### 529 College Savings Plans

<span class="key-term">529 college savings plans</span> are designed to encourage savings for college and other educational costs. As with a Roth IRA, contributions are made with after-tax dollars. Earnings grow tax-deferred, and withdrawals for qualified education expenses are completely tax-free.

Any adult can establish a 529 account in any state—you don't have to be a resident of that state. The account is established in the name of a beneficiary, but the <span class="key-term">beneficiary does not own or control the account</span>—the account owner does. The contributor/owner can change the beneficiary at any time.

**Qualified Education Expenses:**

**Higher Education Expenses** — Qualified education expenses for higher education include:
- Post-secondary tuition and fees
- Room and board
- Textbooks and computers
- Special needs equipment

**Elementary and Secondary Education Expenses** — Up to <span class="key-term">$10,000 per year</span> can be withdrawn for elementary and secondary (K-12) educational expenses at a public, private, or religious school.

**Apprenticeship & Credentialing** — Withdrawals can go to pay for certain qualified costs associated with certain apprenticeship programs, registered training programs, and credentialing fees.

**Student Loan Repayment** — Thanks to the SECURE Act, up to <span class="key-term">$10,000 lifetime</span> per beneficiary may be used for student loan repayment.

**Roth IRA Rollovers** — Under the <span class="key-term">SECURE 2.0 Act</span>, if a 529 account has been open for 15 or more years, the qualified beneficiary can roll over up to <span class="key-term">$35,000 lifetime</span> into a Roth IRA. Rollovers are subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits and must be a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer.

<div class="test-tip">
  <p><strong>Test Tip:</strong> Funds withdrawn from a 529 plan for non-qualified expenses are subject to ordinary income tax on the earnings portion PLUS a 10% penalty. Know what counts as "qualified" and what doesn't.</p>
</div>

#### Characteristics of 529 Accounts

- Any adult can establish a 529 account in any state
- The account owner (not the beneficiary) controls the account
- Beneficiary can be changed at any time to another family member
- Earnings grow tax-deferred; withdrawals are tax-free if used for qualified expenses
- No income limit restrictions on contributors
- No age limit for beneficiaries
- Non-qualified withdrawals: earnings taxed as ordinary income plus 10% penalty

#### Alternatives to 529 College Savings Plans

**Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA):** <span class="key-term">Coverdell ESAs</span> are tax-favored savings vehicles for qualified education expenses covering elementary, secondary, and higher education. Key features:

- Annual contribution limit: <span class="key-term">$2,000</span> per beneficiary
- Contributions must be in cash
- Account must be established before beneficiary reaches age <span class="key-term">18</span> (except for special needs beneficiaries)
- Funds must be used by the time the beneficiary reaches age <span class="key-term">30</span>
- Income limits apply—high earners cannot contribute
- Unused funds can be transferred to another family member's Coverdell ESA without tax consequences

**529 Prepaid Tuition Plans:** <span class="key-term">529 prepaid tuition plans</span> allow tuition to be pre-purchased at today's rate for the beneficiary's future college tuition. Typically, only in-state residents can participate. These plans are guaranteed by the state government, and the value reflects current tuition rates—essentially locking in today's price. The risk is that the investment's value will only increase at the rate of tuition inflation. Prepaid tuition plans are considered to be <span class="key-term">municipal securities</span> and are not registered securities.

### Topic 2: Other Municipal Fund Securities

#### Local Government Investment Pools (LGIPs)

<span class="key-term">Local government investment pools (LGIPs)</span> are trusts established by state and local governments that offer municipal entities a place to invest their money. Government entities may use their surplus cash to purchase interests in the LGIP, which invests the assets in a portfolio of securities according to the trust's investment objectives and state laws. Only municipal governments and their agencies may invest in LGIPs. They are <span class="key-term">not open to the public</span>.

By pooling their funds, government participants benefit from economies of scale, diversification, professional portfolio management, and liquidity.

#### Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) Accounts

<span class="key-term">Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) accounts</span> are designed to allow people with disabilities and their families to save for the future on a tax-deferred basis. An ABLE account can hold up to <span class="key-term">$100,000</span> without interfering with the disabled person's ability to qualify for federal Supplemental Security Income (SSI) benefits. If the ABLE account holds more than $100,000, only the amount in excess of $100,000 counts as an asset of the disabled person when determining their eligibility for SSI.

A disabled person is eligible for an ABLE account only if their <span class="key-term">disability began before the age of 46</span>.

ABLE accounts work in a similar way to 529 accounts. Contributions are made with after-tax dollars, but earnings and withdrawals are not taxed if they are used for <span class="key-term">qualified disability expenses</span>, such as medical treatment, education, and transportation. States offer their plans, and contribution limits are the same as a state's 529 college savings plan. Many states allow nonresidents to open an ABLE account in their state.

Unlike 529 accounts, the beneficiary of an ABLE account is also the account owner. A beneficiary can only have <span class="key-term">one ABLE account</span>, but anyone may contribute to that account.
