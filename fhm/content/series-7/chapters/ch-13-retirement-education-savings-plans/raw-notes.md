## SECTION 1: RETIREMENT PLANS

### Introduction
- Most people want to stop working at some point
- A **retirement plan** is a formal way for working people to save and invest money for retirement systematically
- Two goals: (1) Provide enough income in retirement, (2) Defer taxes until after retirement (lower bracket)

### Key Concepts
- Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)
- Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plans

---

### Topic 1: Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)

**Contributing to an IRA**
- Under IRS rules, individuals can set up **individual retirement accounts (IRAs)** to save for retirement on a tax-deferred basis
- Depending on income and availability of workplace plan, may be eligible to **deduct contributions**
- At withdrawal: all deductible pre-tax contributions and earnings are taxed at ordinary income tax rate
- Nondeductible (after-tax) contributions are withdrawn tax-free
- To distinguish from Roth: these are referred to as **traditional IRAs**

**IRA Contributions**
- Must be made only from **earned income** (not passive income)
- **Earned income** = wages; **Unearned income** = dividends, capital gains, interest on securities, Social Security benefits, unemployment benefits, child support, rental income, inherited funds
- All new contributions must be made in **cash only**
- Existing assets from a pre-existing IRA may be transferred to another IRA

**Test-Taking Tip:** There is no such thing as a joint IRA. IRAs are for individuals and must be separate accounts.

**Contribution Limits**
- Traditional IRA contributions are limited to **earned income**
- Persons aged 50 or above may contribute an extra **$1,000** annually as catch-up contribution
- A nonworking spouse can set up a separate IRA and their working spouse can contribute up to the annual maximum with their earned income (with catch-up if qualified)
- Excess contributions are taxed at **6% per year** as long as they remain in the account

**Contributing to More Than One Retirement Plan**
- If individual participates in another qualified plan (e.g., 401k), they can open an IRA and make contributions up to the limit, but contributions may not be tax-deductible
- Deduction depends on the individual's income level

**Suitable Investments for an IRA**
- Only cash or cash equivalents can be deposited
- Once in the account: can buy securities, IRAs can be used to purchase real estate
- **Prohibited:** Collectibles (works of art, stamps), life insurance
- Naked calls are also prohibited trades in an IRA
- Covered calls, writing covered calls, buying protective puts ARE permitted
- Municipal bonds are typically not suitable (already tax-exempt, so tax-deferred nature is wasted)

**Withdrawing Funds from a Traditional IRA**
- Most make pre-tax contributions to traditional IRA
- After-tax contributions can be withdrawn tax-free
- Must show evidence taxes were paid on contributions
- Withdrawals before age **59 1/2**: taxed on pre-tax contributions + earnings, PLUS additional **10% penalty**
- Can avoid 10% penalty if withdrawal is for:
  - Qualified education expenses (tuition, books, supplies)
  - Payment of medical expenses (>7.5% of individual's gross income)
  - Permanent disability
  - First-time home purchase ($10,000 lifetime maximum)
  - Payment of health insurance premiums by unemployed or self-employed person who has received state or federal aid for 3 consecutive months
  - Withdrawals in substantially equal periodic payments (IRS Rule 72(t)) continuously for 5 years
  - A person who has a child (including by adoption): can withdraw up to $5,000 from their 401k or IRA without penalty. Must be used within 1 year of date of birth or adoption, and the $5,000 limit applies separately to each parent.

**First Distribution**
- The first distribution from the plan must be taken no later than **April 1 of the year following the year in which the participant turns 73**
- All other distributions must occur by **December 31** each year
- Amount of withdrawal calculated by IRS
- Failure to take RMD results in a **25% tax penalty**

**Retirement Plan Transfer vs. Rollover**
- **Transfer**: Move IRA directly into another IRA at a different bank or broker-dealer; if individual is leaving a company, retirement plan assets can also be directly transferred into a conduit IRA
- When assets are transferred directly: no tax liability, no limit on number of transfers
- May be referred to as **direct rollovers**
- **Rollover**: Assets distributed directly to the participant; check sent directly; 20% of rollover will be withheld; money must be redeposited within **60 days** of the distribution date, or the entire amount will be taxable and possibly subject to early withdrawal penalties
- Rollovers can only occur **once per year**

**Traditional IRA: Death of IRA Owner**
- What happens if an IRA holder dies? Recipient must deplete the assets over **10 years at less** and pay taxes due, but **no penalty tax** applies if the recipient is under age 59 1/2
- Spouses have more tax-friendly options

**Spousal beneficiary** - spouse has 3 choices based on age and need:
1. A younger spouse may roll over the account into their existing IRA; if spouse is 53 years old, RMDs are not required to begin until 20 years later
2. An older spouse with sufficient income may open an inherited IRA, which is depleted over a 10-year period. If spouse is 80 years old with 2-year life expectancy, this choice "stretches" the payout window by 8 years, reducing annual tax liability
3. Wealthy persons may refuse to accept (disclaim) the IRA

**Non-spousal beneficiary** - has 3 choices:
1. Transfer the IRA into an inherited IRA and deplete the assets within 10 years
2. Cash out the IRA and pay taxes due
3. Wealthy persons may refuse to accept (disclaim) the IRA

### Roth IRAs
- Unlike traditional IRAs, Roth IRA contributions are **never tax-deductible**
- Contributions are made with after-tax dollars
- However, if the account has been held for **5 years or more** AND the individual is at least age **59 1/2**, earnings may be withdrawn **tax-free and penalty-free**
- Rules for Roth IRAs:
  - Contributions are **never** tax-deductible
  - Contributions are limited to **earned income**
  - Contribution limits are the **same** as traditional IRAs
  - Contributions can be withdrawn tax-free **without penalty at any time**
  - Participants with income over certain levels (high-earners) are not eligible to contribute
  - There are **no required minimum distributions**

- Individuals may contribute to **both** a traditional and Roth IRA, but cannot contribute more than the annual maximum combined across both accounts
- Roth IRAs have the **same annual contribution limits** as traditional IRAs

### Comparison Table: Traditional vs. Roth IRA

| Feature | Traditional IRA | Roth IRA |
|---------|----------------|----------|
| Contributions | May be tax-deductible; Must be earned income | Not tax-deductible; Must be earned income |
| Withdrawals | 10% penalty if made before age 59 1/2, subject to exceptions | 10% penalty on earnings if made before age 59 1/2, subject to exceptions |
| Taxation of withdrawals | Both pre-tax contributions and earnings are taxed at ordinary tax rates; If nondeductible contributions (after-tax) were made, they can be withdrawn tax-free, subject to age and holding period requirements | Contributions (principal) and earnings can be withdrawn tax-free subject to age and holding period requirements |
| Contribution limits | $7,500; Additional $1,000 catch-up for individuals age 50 or over | $7,500; Additional $1,000 catch-up for individuals age 50 or over |
| Earnings | Tax-deferred until withdrawn | May grow tax-deferred and are generally tax-free |

### Topic 2: Employer-Sponsored Retirement Plans

**ERISA Requirements**
- **ERISA** (Employee Retirement Income Security Act): establishes minimum standards for retirement plans in private industry
- Not all plans are subject to ERISA (e.g., 457 plans are NOT subject)
- Plans subject to ERISA must meet certain requirements including fiduciary responsibilities and minimum vesting standards
- A person **vested** = the amount of time the employee must work for the company before the employer's contributions become fully the employee's; the employee's own contributions vest **immediately**

**Defined Benefit Plans**
- Employer bears the investment risk
- Employer promises a specific retirement benefit (e.g., percentage of salary × years of service)
- Less common today

**Defined Contribution Plans**
- **Employee** bears the investment risk
- Employer and/or employee contribute specific amounts
- The retirement benefit depends on investment performance
- Types include: 401(k), 403(b), 457

**Withdrawing Funds from Defined Contribution Plans**
- Qualified withdrawals are taxed as **ordinary income**
- Early withdrawals (before 59 1/2) subject to 10% penalty plus taxes
- Hardship withdrawals may be available

**Profit Sharing Plans**
- Employer contributions are discretionary (based on company profits)
- Subject to ERISA

**Qualified Plans for Small Businesses and Self-Employed**

**SEP IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)**
- For small businesses and self-employed
- Employer contributions only (no employee contributions)
- Subject to ERISA

**SIMPLE IRA (Savings Incentive Match Plan for Employees)**
- For businesses with 100 or fewer employees
- Both employer and employee contribute
- Subject to ERISA

**403(b) Plans**
- For employees of tax-exempt organizations (schools, hospitals, nonprofits)
- Also known as **Tax-Sheltered Annuity (TSA)** plans
- Subject to ERISA

**457 Plans**
- For state and local government employees
- **NOT subject to ERISA** (this is a key exam point)

**Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP)**
- Company contributes its own stock to employee accounts
- Subject to ERISA

**Nonqualified Retirement Plans**
- NOT subject to ERISA
- Employer contributions are NOT tax-deductible until the employee receives the benefit
- Deferred compensation plans: employer promises to pay employee at a later date

### Section 1 Quick Quiz Answers
1. **a.** Withdrawals before age 59 1/2 from a traditional IRA are subject to taxes and penalties unless the withdrawal meets an exception
2. **b.** Retirees who do not take their RMDs by the deadline will be subject to a tax equal to 25% of the undistributed RMD
3. **b.** In a defined contribution plan, the investment risk is borne by the employee
4. **b.** Qualified withdrawals from a defined contribution plan are taxed as ordinary income
5. **a.** A 401(k) plan is subject to ERISA (457 is NOT)

## SECTION 2: VARIABLE ANNUITIES

### Introduction
- Annuities are investment vehicles that provide **tax-deferred growth**
- Can also be used to provide income to an individual during retirement

### Key Concepts
- Annuity Basics
- Variable Annuity Phases
- Characteristics of Variable Annuities
- Variable Annuity Rules and Regulations
- Variable Life Insurance Contracts

---

### Topic 1: Annuity Basics

**What is an Annuity?**
- An **annuity** is an investment contract between an individual and an insurance company
- Owner invests money with insurance company (lump sum or periodic payments)
- At retirement age, insurance company distributes payments (monthly, lump sum, or scheduled)
- Size of payments depends on amount deposited and accumulated growth
- Two types: **fixed annuities** and **variable annuities**

**Fixed Annuity: Guaranteed Rate of Return**
- Insurer guarantees a **specific rate of return**
- Insurance company invests customer's **annuity premiums** into the company's **general account**
- Insurance company chooses investments and assumes investment risk
- **NOT considered a security** — regulated by insurance laws only
- **Indexed annuities**: track an index and pay return based on index performance; also considered fixed annuities, not subject to securities laws; offer guaranteed minimum rate of growth but investors often only earn a percentage of equity index return
- At retirement, owner may choose to **annuitize** the contract and receive periodic payments
- Fixed annuity payments subject to **purchasing power risk** (inflation risk) — future payments worth less in real dollar terms

**Variable Annuity: No Guaranteed Rate of Return**
- No guarantee of a specific rate of return
- Customers given a menu of **mutual fund-like investments** to choose from
- If investments perform better than expected → annuity value increases
- If investments perform worse → annuity value decreases
- Hence the name "variable" annuity

**Variable Annuity Regulation**
- Since investor selects investment vehicles, variable annuities are **considered securities**
- Regulated under **securities laws**
- Unlike fixed annuities, must be sold under a **prospectus**
- Individuals who sell any type of annuity must be **state registered** to sell insurance products
- Sellers of variable insurance products must **also** be registered to sell securities (Series 6 or Series 7)

---

### Topic 2: Variable Annuity Phases

**Separate Accounts**
- Funds from variable annuities are kept in accounts that are **separate** from the insurance company's **general account**
- Often referred to as **separate accounts**
- Within a separate account, investor chooses from **subaccounts** that operate similarly to mutual funds
- For variable annuities, the **investor** (not the insurance company) bears the investment risk

**Two Phases:**

**Accumulation Phase**
- Customer contributes money to annuity account and purchases a **variable number of accumulation units**
- As customer contributes more → purchases more accumulation units
- Units grow (or shrink) in value depending on performance of separate subaccount investments chosen
- Contract grows **tax-deferred**, taxes paid on earnings upon distribution
- **Riders**: optional additional benefits (guaranteed minimum growth rate, insurance against market downturn)
- Popular rider: **guaranteed minimum income benefit (GMIB)** — guarantees a minimum level of payments once contract has been annuitized, regardless of market conditions

**Distribution Phase**
- Customers typically wait until retirement age to begin receiving distributions
- May come in lump sum or random withdrawals
- May choose to **annuitize** the contract
- If annuitized, customer becomes an **annuitant** and receives a series of payments over a specified period or for life
- Accumulation units converted into a **fixed number of annuitization units** based on life expectancy of annuitant
- Monthly payments depend on: performance of investments in separate account, age and sex of customer, customer's choice of payment
- Contributions typically made with **after-tax dollars**
- Taxes on income earned deferred until withdrawal
- **10% penalty** on earnings portion of withdrawals before age **59 1/2**
- Once earnings withdrawn, taxed at customer's **ordinary income tax rate**
- Variable annuities are both an **insurance product** and a **security** — subject to regulations of both industries
- On the security side: regulated by SEC and FINRA, as well as each state

**General vs. Separate Accounts**
- **Fixed annuity**: customer's investment deposited into insurance company's **general account**; insurance company manages funds in same pool as other insurance products; annuitants receive fixed monthly payment for life, guaranteed by insurance company; **insurance company bears investment risk**
- **Variable annuity**: funds held in a **separate account** from other insurance products; annuity owner takes on investment risk; not at risk if insurance company has financial difficulties; **all separate account products are securities**, requiring prospectus delivery at time of sale

### Topic 3: Characteristics of Variable Annuities

**Death Benefit**
- If annuitant dies during accumulation period (before insurance company has begun making payments), beneficiary receives a payment, which bears the **mortality risk** of the contract → called a **death benefit**
- Payment = total investments + any additional earnings from the annuity
- If annuity loses money, beneficiary receives total invested money
- Beneficiary responsible for paying income taxes on any earnings
- In some cases, annuitant may annuitize a contract and receive payments for a specific period (e.g., 20 years); if annuitant dies during distribution phase, named beneficiary continues to receive payments
- **Annuitization payments are NOT considered a death benefit**

**Purchasing Annuities**

| Type of Annuity | How Purchased | Payout |
|---|---|---|
| Single premium deferred annuity | Lump sum | Delayed to a later date |
| Periodic payment deferred annuity | Weekly, monthly, or annually on a scheduled basis | Delayed to a later date |
| Immediate annuity | Lump sum | Begins in 30 days |

**Test-Taking Tip:** There is no periodic payment on an immediate annuity.

**Deferred Annuity Surrender Periods**
- With a deferred annuity, annuitant may not withdraw funds for up to **10 years** (length varies by contract)
- The **surrender period** = time the investor must wait before withdrawing without penalty
- If annuitant needs money before end of surrender period, can surrender by paying a **surrender fee**
- **Surrender value** of account = account's value at time of surrender minus the surrender fee

**Test-Taking Tip:** Customers who need income in the near future (such as the elderly) should not consider deferred annuities.

**Free-Look Period**
- Variable annuities typically have a **free-look period**: cancel the policy within a specified time frame at no charge
- Duration of free-look may be 10 days or more, depending on state law

**Receiving Payments and Taxation**
- At a certain point, investor decides to transition from accumulation to payout phase
- Investor can choose to receive payment in one of the following ways:
  - Lump sum
  - Monthly payments (annuitize the contract)
  - Random withdrawal — annuitant chooses to take random sums over random periods

**Annuity Payout Options (Settlement Options)**
- If owner chooses to annuitize, must select a method of receiving payments
- Choice of payout determines the amount of monthly check:
  - **Life income**: insurer pays annuitant for remainder of their lifetime; when annuitant dies, benefits stop; likely to be the **shortest period** and **least expensive** for insurance company; annuitant receives the **largest monthly check**
  - **Life with period certain**: insurer pays annuitant an income during their lifetime; but if annuitant dies before the **period certain** expires, payments continue to be made to a beneficiary
  - **Joint life with last survivor**: covers two lifetimes; insurer continues to pay a benefit until both covered parties have died; married couple typically chooses this; will have the **smallest monthly checks** because payments continue through life of last survivor

**Example:** Annuitant chooses life with a period certain of 20 years. The annuitant will receive a payment for life. However, if they die within the next 20 years, the named beneficiary will receive payments for the remainder of the period. An annuitant who chooses life with period certain will receive medium-sized monthly checks.

**Assumed Interest Rate (AIR)**
- Once the payout option has been selected and the contract has been annuitized, the initial payment must be calculated using the **assumed interest rate (AIR)**
- When the contract is annuitized, the insurance company estimates future investment earnings
- AIR is expressed in percentage terms as an annual "projected" rate of growth
- **AIR is fixed**: Once set, the AIR does not change; if assets continue to perform in line with AIR, insurer will send annuitant a check for the same amount each period
- More likely: earnings on annuity units will vary, and the annuitant's check for each period will change
- Some insurers adjust the amount of payments annually, while others make changes every month

**AIR rules:**
- **AIR applies to annuity units only**: During accumulation phase, value of accumulation units increases if separate account earns any positive return; AIR does not apply
- After annuitization, annuity unit value increases only if the return exceeds AIR; if annuity unit's return is below AIR, the annuity unit value falls
- **AIR is not a guarantee**: The AIR shown in a variable annuity prospectus illustrates the payment to be made if the separate account performs at that rate; it is a conservative estimate, but NOT a guaranteed minimum rate of return

**Payment based on actual performance vs. projected AIR:**
- If annuity unit values fall → amount of annuitant's monthly check will decline
- Conversely, if separate account performance exceeds AIR → annuity units' value will increase → annuitant's payments will grow
- If contract performs in line with AIR over a given period → next payment will be the same as the prior payment

Summary:
- **Perform in line with AIR** → amount of next payment is the same
- **Outperform the AIR** → amount of next payment will increase from the prior payment
- **Underperform the AIR** → amount of next payment will decrease from the prior payment

### Topic 4: Variable Annuity Rules and Regulations

**Federal Regulations Regarding Variable Annuities**
- Under the **Securities Act of 1933**: variable annuities are securities → must be **registered with SEC** and sold with a **prospectus**
- Under the **Securities Exchange Act of 1934** and the **Investment Company Act of 1940**: contracts are securities → may only be sold by a registered **broker-dealer** and their registered representatives
- Broker-dealer must be a member of **FINRA**
- As a **separate business entity**, variable annuities must keep invested premiums in a **separate account** from insurance company's fixed annuity and other insurance accounts
- Securities can only be bought and sold within this separate account

**Recommending Deferred Variable Annuities**
- When recommending a deferred variable annuity, registered representative must describe the annuity's specific features including:
  - Potential surrender period and surrender charge
  - Potential tax penalty if customers make withdrawals before reaching the age of 59 1/2
  - Various costs and fees (variable annuities have **higher fees** than regular mutual fund investments, which can be made within an IRA account)
  - Subject to market risk

**Suitability**
- When recommending fixed annuity vs. variable annuity: must know customer's **risk tolerance**
- Fixed annuity suitable for **risk-averse** investor seeking guaranteed fixed payments for life
- Variable annuity suitable for customers willing to accept investment risk in exchange for potential for higher earnings
- Other suitability factors:
  - Annuities with long surrender periods should **not** be sold to elderly customers who may need their money before surrender period is over
  - Annuities may be appropriate for customers who want to be assured of a death benefit
  - Customers who need money in the near future should consider only an **immediate annuity**, not a deferred annuity
  - The IRS allows earnings from an annuity to grow tax-deferred, so that annuities may be suitable for individuals in **high tax brackets**
  - Unlike contributions to IRAs and 401(k)s, contributions to most annuities are **not tax-deductible**; generally customer should first have reached the maximum amount that can be put into their IRA or 401(k) **before** investing in an annuity
  - Because earnings in an annuity grow tax-deferred, there is **no extra tax benefit** from holding an annuity inside an IRA or a 401(k)

**Voting Rights**
- Owners of variable annuity contracts have the right to vote based on their ownership interest in the **separate account**
- If separate account is set up to hold mutual fund shares: owner can vote on management of the separate account and management of the mutual fund it contains
- **Separate account voting rights**: owner has right to vote for the sub-account's board of directors, investment adviser, and independent auditor; also has right to vote on any changes in investment policies
- **No voting on distributions**: Annuity holders do **not** vote on distributions of dividends and capital gains; the variable annuity's board of trustees or board of managers makes these decisions
- The prospectuses for the annuity and underlying mutual fund describe these voting rights

**Taxation**
- In a **nonqualified annuity**: contributions are made on an **after-tax** basis; earnings from nonqualified variable annuities are taxed as ordinary income when withdrawn
- If owner chooses to annuitize the contract, each annuity payment will be divided between an earnings portion (taxed as ordinary income) and the remainder (contributions) as a **tax-free return of capital**
- In a **qualified annuity** (e.g., 403(b) plan, also known as tax-sheltered annuity): contributions are made on a **pre-tax** basis; as a result, **all withdrawals** are fully taxable as ordinary income

**Test-Taking Tip:** Withdrawals from any retirement plan or variable insurance product are **never** subject to capital gains taxes; they are only treated as ordinary income.

**Three distribution tax events in an annuity:**

| Distribution Event | Tax Effect |
|---|---|
| Annuitization | Monthly blended payment of tax-free principal and taxable earnings |
| Lump sum withdrawal | Single blended payment of tax-free principal and taxable earnings |
| Random withdrawal | Taxable earnings are distributed first, then tax-free principal (last-in, first-out [LIFO] tax treatment) |

### Topic 5: Variable Life Insurance Contracts

- Another insurance product that is also considered a **security** is **variable life insurance**
- As with variable annuities, the premiums paid for insurance coverage are deposited by the insurance company into a **separate account**
- The growth in the separate account's value determines the **death benefit** that will be paid → insurance coverage amount will vary with the performance of the separate account

**Variable Life Is a Security**
- Variable life insurance policies require that a customer be provided with a **prospectus**
- The SEC views a variable life policy as a security because the **purchaser bears the investment risk** and not the insurance company

**Permanent Insurance**
- Variable life insurance, also known as **permanent insurance**, remains in force as long as the premium is paid
- Part of the premium covers the cost of insurance, and the balance is invested in a separate account
- The **premium payment amount is fixed — it does not change**
- What does vary is the **death benefit** and the policy's **cash value**
- **Cash value** = the amount that can be borrowed against the policy or withdrawn if the policy is surrendered

**Costs associated with variable life policies (deducted from premiums):**
- **Mortality and expense risk charges**: cost of the death benefit (life insurance component)
- **Sales fees**: to pay the agent's commission cost
- **Administrative fees**: to cover the insurer's ongoing expenses
- **State premium fees**: insurance companies are state-regulated; states impose premium tax
- **Investment management fees**: fees charged for running the mutual funds held in the separate account
- **Rider fees**: cost of optional additional benefits; most typical rider is a **guaranteed minimum death benefit (GMDB)** which promises to grow the separate account at a minimum guaranteed rate (say 4%)

## SECTION 3: MUNICIPAL FUND SECURITIES

### Introduction
- A **municipal fund security** is an investment product issued by a state or municipality
- Because a state or local government issues it, it is **exempt from the rules of the Investment Company Act of 1940**
- Sale is regulated by the **Municipal Securities Rulemaking Board (MSRB)**
- Income from municipal fund securities may be exempt from federal taxes and often state and local taxes as well
- MSRB defines three kinds: **higher education savings plans (529 plans)**, **local government investment pools (LGIPs)**, and **Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) accounts**

### Key Concepts
- 529 College Savings Plans
- Other Municipal Fund Securities

---

### Topic 1: Education Savings Plans

**529 College Savings Plans**
- 529 college savings plans are designed to encourage savings for college and other educational costs
- As with a Roth IRA, contributions are made with after-tax dollars
- However, unlike individual retirement accounts, they are **not limited to those participating in an employer's 529 plan**; investors are limited to the portfolios offered by a state's 529 plan

**The following are not applicable to 529 plans:**
- **NOT ERISA** (this is a municipal fund security, not an employer plan)
- Contributions are NOT tax-deductible for federal purposes (some states give state tax deductions)

**Qualified Education Expenses:**

**Higher Education Expenses:**
- Qualified education expenses for higher education include:
  - Post-secondary tuition and fees (but not sports and recreation activities unless part of a degree)
  - Room and board
  - Textbooks and computers
  - Special needs equipment

**Elementary and Secondary Education Expenses:**
- Up to **$10,000/year** can be withdrawn for elementary and secondary K-12 educational expenses ("qualified tuition" for K-12)
- Expenses at a public, private, or religious elementary or secondary school qualify

**Apprenticeship & Credentialing:**
- Withdrawals can go to pay for certain qualified costs associated with certain apprenticeship programs, registered training programs, and credentialing fees (e.g., CPA exam prep, legal bar fees)

**Student Loan Repayment:**
- Thanks to the SECURE (Setting Every Community Up for Retirement Enhancement) Act, qualified distributions for K-12 expenses extended
- Up to **$10,000 lifetime limit** per beneficiary for student loan repayment (limit is on a per-beneficiary basis)

**Roth IRA Rollovers**
- Under the **SECURE 2.0 Act**: if 529 account has been open for **15+ years**, qualified beneficiary can roll over up to **$35,000 lifetime** into a Roth IRA
- Rollovers are subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits (i.e., you can roll over up to the annual Roth contribution limit per year)
- Must be a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer; beneficiary must have earned income; amount rolled over from 529 and contributed to the Roth IRA cannot exceed the Roth IRA annual contribution limit

**Characteristics of 529 Accounts**
- Any adult can establish a 529 account in any state (doesn't have to be resident of that state)
- Established in the name of a beneficiary
- The beneficiary does **not** own or control the account — the **account owner** does
- The contributor/owner can change the beneficiary at any time
- **Tax-deferred growth**: earnings are not taxed while in the account
- **Tax-free withdrawals** if used for qualified education expenses
- If funds withdrawn for non-qualified expenses: earnings are taxed as ordinary income **plus a 10% penalty**

**Usage:**
- Can be established by a parent for a child and if child doesn't use it, beneficiary can be changed to another family member
- Balance remaining after 15 years can now be rolled into a Roth IRA (SECURE 2.0)
- No income limit restrictions on contributors
- No age limit for beneficiaries

**Alternatives to 529 College Savings Plans**

**Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)**
- Coverdell education savings accounts (or **Coverdell ESAs**, a.k.a. **ESAS**) are tax-favored savings for qualified education expenses (elementary, secondary, and higher education)
- Annual contribution limit: **$2,000** per beneficiary
- Contributions must be in cash
- Account must be established before beneficiary reaches age **18** (except for special needs beneficiaries)
- If funds remain in the account after the beneficiary reaches age **30**, the money must be withdrawn and taxed accordingly
- **Income limits**: Contributions limited to individuals with modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) up to a certain threshold

**Key differences from 529:**
- 529: no age limit, state-sponsored, higher contribution limits ($300K+ depending on state)
- Coverdell: $2,000/year limit, must open before 18, use by 30

**Age limits:** Coverdell contributions are not permitted for the account after the beneficiary reaches the age of 18. There are no age limits for making contributions to a 529 plan.

**Unused funds can be given to another family member.** When a balance is transferred to a Coverdell ESA, there is no "change of beneficiary" tax consequence so long as the new beneficiary is a member of the previous beneficiary's family.

**Annual funds can also go to a sibling.** $2,000 is the per-beneficiary limit in a single year, so if the beneficiary's sibling, the contribution to the sibling's ESA counts against THAT sibling's limit.

**Not available to high earners.** High earners cannot establish accounts or contribute to Coverdell ESAs. There are no income limits for making contributions to a 529 plan.

**529 Prepaid Tuition Plans**
- 529 prepaid tuition plans: tuition pre-purchased at today's rate for the beneficiary's future college tuition
- Typically, only in-state residents can participate
- States that offer 529 prepaid tuition plans are guaranteed by the state government; value of these plans reflects the current tuition rates (lock in current tuition)
- Higher education institutions also sometimes offer prepaid plans
- The risk is that the investment's value will only increase at the rate of tuition inflation
- Prepaid tuition plans are sponsored by individual states, and the sponsoring state of the plan guarantees the plan value
- Major difference from 529 savings plans: 529 prepaid tuition plans are considered to be **municipal securities** and are not registered securities

### Topic 2: Other Municipal Fund Securities

**Local Government Investment Pools (LGIPs)**
- **Local government investment pools (LGIPs)** are trusts established by state and local governments that offer municipal entities a place to invest their money
- Government entities may use their surplus cash to purchase interests in the LGIP, which invests the assets in a portfolio of securities according to the trust's investment objectives and state laws
- **Only municipal governments and their agencies may invest** in LGIPs — they are **not open to the public**
- Benefits: economies of scale, diversification, professional portfolio management, and liquidity

**Example:** The state of Virginia offers public entities in Virginia the opportunity to participate in a professionally managed, AAA-rated LGIP. Offers competitive return with minimal risk of principal loss, liquidity, and benefits of large-scale institutional investment management.

**Achieving a Better Life Experience (ABLE) Accounts**
- **ABLE accounts** are designed to allow people with disabilities and their families to save for the future on a tax-deferred basis
- Can hold up to **$100,000** without interfering with the disabled person's ability to qualify for federal **Supplemental Security Income (SSI)** benefits
- If ABLE account holds more than $100,000, only the amount in excess of $100,000 counts as an asset when determining SSI eligibility
- A disabled person is eligible for an ABLE account only if their **disability began before the age of 46**
- ABLE accounts work similarly to 529 accounts: contributions made with **after-tax dollars**, earnings and withdrawals not taxed if used for **qualified disability expenses** (medical treatment, education, transportation)
- States offer their plans, contribution limits same as state's 529 plan
- Many states allow nonresidents to open an ABLE account in their state
- Unlike 529 accounts: the **beneficiary** of an ABLE account is **also the account owner**
- A beneficiary can only have **one ABLE account**, but anyone may contribute to that account
