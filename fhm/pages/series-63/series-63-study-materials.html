Welcome to Franklin Hugh's Series 63 prep. I'm working through this exam alongside the SIE and Series 7 materials, and as with the others, I am trying to write the version I wish I had read first.

The Series 63 is the state-law bolt-on. FINRA's exams qualify you to sell securities in general. The Series 63 is each state saying you are allowed to sell them to its residents in particular. Most states require it before you can register as an agent, which is why almost everyone who sits for the Series 7 sits for this one too.

Sixty scored questions, seventy-five minutes, forty-three right to pass. The exam tests the Uniform Securities Act of 1956 as amended by NASAA. That is an oddly specific thing to mention, until you realize it matters: when the 1956 primary text and the modern prep-provider consensus disagree, the primary text wins. There is exactly one of those disagreements on this exam, and it lives in a footnote on Chapter 8.

Two domains carry 45% of the exam between them. Ethics is 25%. Communications is 20%. If you are short on time, start where the points live.

The Eight Domains

NASAA weights the Series 63 across eight subject areas. Each chapter below is a full walkthrough of one domain, built from the primary source text (USA 1956, NASAA model rules, SEC and FINRA rules) rather than a prep-provider paraphrase.

Chapter 1 · 12%

Regulation of Broker-Dealers

Broker-dealer definition, exclusions from the definition, registration mechanics, surety bonds, denial and suspension grounds, FINRA 3110 supervision.

Chapter 2 · 13%

Regulation of Broker-Dealer Agents

Who is an agent, issuer-representative carve-outs, Canadian limited registration, outside accounts under FINRA 3210, payments to unregistered persons.

Chapter 3 · 25% · Start Here

Ethical Practices and Obligations

NASAA Dishonest Practices for BDs and agents, IA unethical practices, Vulnerable Adults Model Act, Regulation Best Interest, suitability, UPIA. The biggest single domain on the exam.

Chapter 4 · 20%

Communication with Customers and Prospects

FINRA 2210 communication tiers, customer account information, options disclosure, margin, Regulation S-P 2024 amendments, SEC Rule 17a-4 retention.

Chapter 5 · 9%

Regulation of Securities and Issuers

Security definition, sale and offer, exempt securities, exempt transactions, burden of proof, the Howey test.

Chapter 6 · 5%

Regulation of Investment Advisers

The ABC test, LATE exclusions, AUM dividing lines, qualified client thresholds, custody rule, recordkeeping, NIST cybersecurity framework.

Chapter 7 · 5%

Regulation of Investment Adviser Representatives

IAR registration, Form U-4 amendments, where IARs register for state vs. federal covered advisers, §203A preemption.

Chapter 8 · 11%

Remedies and Administrative Provisions

Antifraud under §101, Administrator powers, criminal penalties, civil liability and rescission, joint and several liability, judicial review, service of process.

Start with Chapter 3: Ethics

Ethics is 25% of the exam. Combine it with Communications (20%) and you are looking at 45% of the test in two domains. If you only have time for one chapter this week, this is the one that pays.

Study Approach

The reality: the Series 63 has a reputation for being easier than the Series 7, and it is, provided you read carefully. The questions are almost all judgment calls. There is no single calculation on the test that will save you.

The strategy: front-load Ethics and Communications. Together they are 45% of the exam. Then Agents and Broker-Dealers, another 25%. That's 70% of your score locked in before you touch the smaller domains.

The thing most people miss: NASAA writes to the primary statute text, not to any particular prep provider. When the two disagree, know both answers and pick the one closest to the 1956 Uniform Securities Act. Chapter 8 flags the one place this matters.
