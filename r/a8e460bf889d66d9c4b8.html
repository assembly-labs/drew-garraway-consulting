#2

#4

Thank you again — this kind of detail is exactly what makes the difference between building something generic and building something that actually works for Berwyn.

Drew Garraway

Section 2
Your Questions, Our Answers
Each of your 18 questionnaire questions restated, with how we're addressing it in the product.
Question 1 — Vendor Management

Walk me through how a new vendor joins Berwyn — from first inquiry to first market day. What documents do you require?

Your 15-step onboarding process — from initial product tasting to the vendor announcement on social media — confirmed we need a robust vendor management foundation, not just a simple contact list.

What we're building: The admin vendor management system A24-A27 now includes additional fields for genre/category, availability notes, stall preferences, special requirements (power, flat ground, van access), and season commitment dates M6. This captures the data currently in your head and spreadsheets.

What's later: A full vendor onboarding workflow with document uploads, compliance tracking, and automated reminders is planned for Phase 2-3. For MVP, vendor data entry is admin-managed.

Documents required: COI showing 1 million liability coverage, Chester County Health Department approval or exemption, and completed application.
Question 2 — Vendor Rotation

You have 70+ vendors with 25-30 selling weekly. How do you manage that rotation?

The rotation logic you described — core vendors get every week, similar-item vendors alternate, genre gaps get filled, then remaining spots go to vendors wanting more dates — is too complex for MVP automation. But we're laying the data foundation.

What we're building: Every vendor profile now includes a genre field (produce, dairy, pastry, olive oil, etc.) and season dates M6. This lets you sort and filter vendors by type when planning the weekly lineup. The weekly product reset tool A43 lets you quickly activate/deactivate each vendor's products.

What's later: Full vendor scheduling with conflict detection and automated rotation is Phase 3 M3/M4.

Question 3 — Stall Locations

How do you assign stall locations? First-come, seniority, product type, or something else?

Your approach — drawing foot traffic through the market, accommodating vendor needs (glass jars on flat spots, produce near van access, hot food near power), and keeping vendors in the same spot all season — is a Phase 3 feature. But we're storing the data now.

What we're building: Vendor profiles include stall location preference and special requirements fields (power, flat ground, van access) M6. This is the data foundation for the full market layout tool later.

What's later: Interactive market map with drag-and-drop stall assignment is Phase 3.

Question 4 — Vendor Compliance

How do you track vendor compliance (insurance, licenses, certifications)? What happens when something expires?

"This is a huge pain. This was my job and it's since been delegated to Erika which I'm not sure she keeps up with weekly/monthly."

We hear you. This is currently planned for Phase 3, but given how painful it is, we're considering pulling a basic version into Phase 2: expiration date tracking with automated alerts when documents are about to lapse.

What's later: Full vendor compliance portal where vendors can upload documents themselves, with automated expiration alerts and renewal workflows M7/M8. Your ideal — "if vendors could submit their documents through a portal, and we all get notified if there's a lapse" — is exactly what we'll build.

Question 5 — Fee Structure

What's your fee structure? Daily flat fee? Seasonal fee? Percentage of sales? A mix?

Flat fee based on attendance with a prepay discount confirmed what we expected. Vendor fee management isn't in the MVP — you'll continue collecting fees as you do now. Phase 3 will add digital vendor invoicing and payment tracking.

What's later: Vendor fee management, invoicing, and payment tracking M5/M6 in Phase 3. This includes the no-show fee you mentioned wanting to enforce.

Question 6 — Vendor Payments

How and when do vendors pay you? What happens when someone is late?

Cash/check collection on market day (with occasional Square card payments) and the no-show problem are noted. This is a Phase 3 feature, but your feedback about the failed monthly invoicing experiment is important context — any digital payment system needs to be day-of, not monthly.

Key insight for us: The wholesale-resale model (you buy from vendors via POs, resell to customers) may simplify our payment architecture significantly. We may not need Stripe Connect split payments for MVP — a single merchant account could work, with vendor payments handled separately based on PO amounts.

Question 7 — Vendor Communication

How do you communicate with vendors? Email, text, WhatsApp, phone?

Primary communication via email and text, with Erika sending weekly vendor emails. For MVP, the vendor purchase order system O66 replaces the manual "here's what we need from you" part of the weekly email. POs are emailed directly to vendors from the dashboard.

What's later: Full vendor communication portal (Phase 2) where vendors can view POs, confirm attendance, and update availability through a simple interface.

Question 8 — Weather & Cancellations

How do you handle weather cancellations or last-minute changes?

Good news: you rarely cancel (maybe two dates in three seasons) and only need a day's notice. This means we don't need complex cancellation workflows for MVP. The existing email/text approach works fine for the rare weather event.

What's later: If needed, a broadcast notification system for weather alerts could be added to the automated messaging infrastructure O68-O71 in Phase 2.

Question 9 — Customer Texting

Walk me through what messages you send. Order confirmations? Pickup reminders? "What's fresh this week"?

"Tuesday morning I would manually text our shoppers to remind them that the market closes at noon and to get their order in. I usually included something like 'new milk jawn flavor' or some other highlight to catch their attention."

This is your #2 pain point, and we're automating it completely. Three automated messages match your current cadence:

"Market is open" O68 — Email + SMS when the ordering window opens. You'll be able to add a weekly product highlight (like the "new milk jawn flavor").

"Last chance to order" O69 — Email + SMS a few hours before the window closes.

"Pickup reminder" O70 — Email + SMS on pickup morning with each customer's time slot.

All messages will feel like they come from you and Carlo — not a robot. Customers who want to text back will still be able to reach a real person.

Follow-up question: What day and time does the ordering window typically open? When does it close? Is the Tuesday reminder the same week as pickup, or the week before?
Question 10 — Text Management

How do you manage customer texts right now? What's working and what's painful?

"The only pain point was the time consumption of physically texting hundreds of people on Tuesday, and dozens of people on Friday when pickup day is already busy enough."

This is exactly what O68-O71 solve. The automated messaging system handles the bulk sending. Customers opt in to SMS at checkout O71, with easy opt-out in every message. TCPA compliant.

The personal phone setup that works — customers knowing there's a real person on the other end — stays. Automated messages handle the broadcasts; personal texting stays for one-on-one conversations.

Question 11 — Ideal Automation

If you could send automated messages to customers, what would be most valuable?

"'Market is open', 'market closes in 3 hours', and day-of pickup (with timeslot) reminders would be most valuable, as texts and emails. Additionally, order confirmation emails."

Done. All four are in the MVP:

O68 Market is open  •  O69 Market closes soon  •  O70 Pickup day reminder  •  Order confirmation emails were already in v1.0.

Question 12 — Order-to-Pickup Process

Walk me through the full order-to-pickup process.

Your 20-step process was the single most valuable answer in the questionnaire. It revealed several workflows we were completely missing. Here's how we're addressing the key steps:

Steps 1-5 (ordering & monitoring): Customer storefront, order notifications, and the ops dashboard handle this C1-C55 O1-O20.

Step 6 (vendor POs after close): This was missing entirely. Now automated O66-O67. After the ordering window closes, the system generates per-vendor purchase orders with total quantities, emailable to vendors directly.

Step 7 (print customer slips & master list): Customer packing slips O64 now sorted by time slot and pick zone. Master inventory list via existing pick list features O24-O35.

Steps 9-11 (review orders, assembly): New customer badge O72 flags first-timers. "Last-minute grab" flags on items like flowers, ice cream, and delicate pastries tell your runner what NOT to pre-bag M2. Pick zone sorting means your foreman can call out items by zone: cold, dry, produce, fragile O65.

Steps 12-13 (pickup & runner): QR check-in and runner workflow O36-O47 designed for your "customer pulls up, runner grabs bags + last-minute items" model.

Step 15 (comp & waste tracking): Comp item logger O75 with reason codes.

Step 17 (thank-you email): Automated welcome email to first-time customers O73.

Step 18 (collections & weekly reset): Full collections system C56-C57 A40 and weekly product reset workflow A43 to stop rebuilding the site every week.

Question 13 — Print Materials

What print materials do you create? Packing lists? Customer order sheets? Who uses them?

You described six types of print materials. Here's how we're handling each:

Vendor order quantities / PO spreadsheet: Automated PO generator O66 — emailable and printable.

Master inventory list: Existing pick list features O24-O35 cover this.

Customer order slips: Smart packing slips O64 with name, contact, order #, dollar total, items sorted by pick zone, checkboxes, and bag label. Batch-printable by time slot.

Pickups per time slot: Dashboard view of orders grouped by time slot O15.

Bag name tickets: Built into the packing slip as a tear-off label O64. No more pre-writing Post-it notes.

Items owed clipboard: Digital credit tracker O74. When a vendor shorts a delivery, log a credit or specific owed item. It carries forward to the customer's next order automatically.

"We had a running clipboard of items owed to customers (like if a vendor shorted us on a delivery and we couldn't make it right by pickup time, we would add it to their next order)."
Question 14 — Vendor Order Delivery

How do vendors receive their orders? How do they know what to pack?

The PO workflow you described — orders go out after market closes, some vendors need early estimates, some are pass-through, some are wholesale case ordering — shaped our PO generator design O66.

Key design decisions from your answer:

• POs are generated after the ordering window closes (with the option to send preliminary estimates earlier for vendors who need lead time)

• The system tracks which vendors have confirmed their PO O67

• Your insight about preferring "pass-through" vendors to minimize inventory is noted — the system will work for both models

Follow-up question: What does a good PO look like to your vendors? Simple list (item + quantity), or do they expect specific formatting? Do any vendors need a different format (case quantities vs. individual units)?
Question 15 — Time-Consuming Tasks

What's the most time-consuming admin task in the order fulfillment process?

Your ranked list became our priority list:

#1 Website management
Weekly product reset workflow A43 — bulk toggle, duplicate last week, per-vendor quick toggle
#2 Text reminders
Automated messaging O68-O71 — three messages matching your current cadence
#3 Sorting orders by time slot
Packing slips auto-grouped by time slot O64
#4 Picking notation
Pick zone sorting within each order O65 + structured staff notes M3

Every one of your top four pain points has a dedicated feature in the MVP.

Question 16 — Customer Wishlist

Your customers value convenience. What do they ask for that you currently can't provide?

Your customers asked for six things. Here's where each lands:

Mobile app — Phase 4. The web app is fully mobile-optimized from day one — it will feel like an app on their phone.

Ability to favorite items — Phase 2, after customer accounts are built.

Edit time slot after checkout — Phase 2. Requires customer accounts. Added to the roadmap based on your feedback.

Product reviews — Phase 4. Nice-to-have, not operational.

Loyalty program — Phase 4. We need order volume data first to design it properly.

Customer profile ("messed up her last order, give her something nice; birthdays; VIP; usually brings dogs") — We're laying the foundation now. Every customer gets a profile with order history, internal notes, and tags Customer entity. First-time orders get auto-flagged O72. Full internal CRM is Phase 2-3.

"Customer profile: messed up her last order give her something nice next week; birthdays; VIP; top spender; 'usually brings dogs'; stuff like that. It's not necessary but helps us to provide high level of customer service. This stuff was just in my and Carlo's brain all the time."

We want to get that out of your brain and into the system.

Question 17 — #1 Automation Request

If we could automate ONE thing, what would save you the most time?

"Ability to sort customer order slips by time slot and sort products within the order by pick location."

Done. This is O64 Customer Packing Slips + O65 Pick Zone Assignment. P0 priority. Your exact request, built exactly as you described it.

Follow-up question: What are the actual pick zones in your space? We're starting with Cold Storage, Dry Goods, Produce, and Fragile/Delicate. Are there zones we're missing? Would "Freezer" be separate from "Cold"? Is there a dedicated area for meal kits?
Question 18 — Anything Else

Anything else about how you run Berwyn that we should know before we start building?

"The things we want to emphasize on our platform is the level of curation and having a 'main line' level of quality. Our customer's loved to know if something was super limited run, or award winning. They spent a lot of money on 'supplemental' groceries because we marketed it really well to them."

This answer shaped the entire collections system C56-C58 A40-A42. Named curated collections, curation badges (Limited Run, Award Winner, Carlo's Pick, New This Week), seasonal collections, and a homepage designed to feel editorial — not transactional.

The collections system is the business model differentiator. It's what makes Gather feel like your market, not just another online grocery store.

Follow-up question: Can you give us 5-6 example collections you'd want at launch? We want to build the prototype around real content, not placeholders.

Follow-Up
A Few More Questions
Beyond the questions embedded above, a couple things that would help us build better.

1. Weekly timeline: Can you map out the typical weekly cycle with specific days? Something like: Sunday = newsletter + open ordering, Tuesday = reminders, Wednesday = ordering closes, Thursday = POs + deliveries, Friday = assembly + pickup. We want the system's automated triggers to match your real schedule.

2. Assembly team: You described a foreman, cold person, dry goods person, bagger, double-checker, and labeler. How many people total? Is this consistent week to week? This helps us design for the right number of simultaneous users on the ops dashboard during pickup morning.

3. Pass-through vs. wholesale vendors: Do some vendors deliver to your storage, while others bring product to the market that you grab from their stall? Or does everything come through deliveries?

4. Product photos: How's vendor photo quality generally? Do you end up taking your own photos, or do vendors provide good ones? This helps us prioritize photo tools (crop, enhance, background removal) vs. basic upload.
