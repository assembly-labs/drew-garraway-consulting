Welcome

Welcome

Your iPad Isn't a Right. It's a Responsibility.

This course will teach you the skills you need to earn back your iPad — and use it the right way going forward.

Why You're Here

You lost your iPad because of choices you made — using curse words and bad emojis in texts, and not following screen time rules. That's not the end of the world. Everyone makes mistakes. But smart people learn from their mistakes so they don't keep making the same ones again.

How This Works

You'll go through 5 short lessons about how to use your devices the right way — and how to handle it when other people don't. At the end, there's a 17-question test. You need to get at least 14 out of 17 right (that's about 80%) to pass. Read each lesson carefully — the test questions come straight from what you'll learn here.

This isn't a punishment. It's a chance to level up. The stuff you'll learn here is real and it matters — not just now, but for when you're older and using social media, group chats, and texting every single day.

Let's get started.

Lesson 1 of 5

YouTube Isn't Real Life

Understanding the difference between entertainment and reality.

The Truth About Your Favorite YouTubers

Here's something important that most kids don't know yet: almost everything you watch on YouTube is planned, practiced, and edited to get you to keep watching. That's not just an opinion — it's how YouTube works as a business. YouTubers make money when you watch their videos. The more you watch, the more money they earn. So everything they do is meant to keep your eyes glued to the screen.

Research from multiple universities has found that YouTube's recommendation algorithm — the system that picks what video plays next — is designed to maximize watch time, and it tends to push content that triggers strong emotions like anger, fear, or excitement.[1] That's why the videos that pop up next are almost always more extreme than the one you just watched.

Reality Check

MrBeast has a team of about 300 employees who help plan, film, and edit his videos. A single video costs between $2.5 million and $5 million to make, and his annual video budget is around $48 million.[2] The "reactions" you see are often filmed more than once to get the best version. His whole channel is a business — and you are the customer.

Reality Check

The Royalty Family films their "daily life" — but think about it: real families don't have camera crews following them around. They pick what to film, set up the situations ahead of time, and cut out anything boring. Their "surprises" are planned before they even start recording. It looks like real life, but it's really a TV show pretending to be real life.

Reality Check

Minecraft YouTubers like Clown Pierce are skilled players, but their videos are heavily edited. That means hours of boring gameplay get cut down to just 15 exciting minutes. The trash talk and big reactions are part of the show — they act that way because it gets more views, not because that's how people really act in real life.

Why This Matters For You

When you spend hours watching people act like loudmouths and show-offs, your brain starts to think that's how people are supposed to act. It isn't. If you acted at school, at BJJ, or at the dinner table the way YouTubers act in their videos, people wouldn't think you were cool — they'd think you were being rude and annoying.

❌ What YouTube Teaches

  • Being loud = being funny
  • Trash talk is normal
  • Reactions should be extreme
  • Spending all day on screens is fine

✅ What Real Life Requires

  • Knowing when to be quiet matters
  • Respect earns respect
  • Calm people are trusted more
  • Screens are a small part of a full life

Three Questions to Ask Yourself

Before you watch any video, ask:

  1. "Is this real, or is this a performance?" — Almost always, it's a performance.
  2. "Would I act this way in front of my coach or teacher?" — If not, don't absorb it like it's normal.
  3. "How long have I been watching?" — If you've lost track of time, that's the algorithm doing its job on you. Take a break.
Lesson 2 of 5

Why People Are Mean Online

It's not just about how you use your device — it's about understanding how others might misuse theirs, and how that can affect you.

The Big Question

Have you ever noticed that people say things in online comments, game chats, or messages that they would never say to someone's face? A kid who's perfectly nice at school might be a total jerk in a group chat. A random stranger in a game lobby might say the most awful things you've ever heard. Why?

This isn't just something you've noticed — it's a real psychological phenomenon that scientists have studied. In 2004, a psychologist named Dr. John Suler gave it a name: the Online Disinhibition Effect.[3] "Disinhibition" is a big word that basically means losing the thing that normally stops you from doing something you shouldn't do. When people go online, the brakes that normally stop them from being rude or mean get weaker — and here's why.

The 5 Reasons People Act Differently Online

Reason 1

They're Hidden — Anonymity

Anonymity means nobody knows who you are. Online, people hide behind usernames, fake profile pictures, and screen names. When nobody knows who you really are, you feel like you can say anything without it coming back to you. It's like wearing an invisible mask.

Back in 1969, a famous psychologist named Philip Zimbardo ran an experiment where he had people wear hoods that hid their faces. The people wearing hoods acted way more aggressively than the people whose faces were visible.[4] Same people, same room — the only difference was whether they could be recognized. The internet gives everyone that same invisible hood.

Reason 2

They Can't See You — Invisibility

Invisibility means you can't see the other person's face. This is different from anonymity. Even when people know who they're talking to, not being able to see the other person's reaction makes a huge difference.

Research from 2012 found that lack of eye contact was actually the #1 reason people say hurtful things online — even more than anonymity.[5] When you can see someone's face crumble after you say something mean, your brain feels bad and tells you to stop. But in a text or a game chat? You don't see the tears. You don't see the hurt. So your brain doesn't hit the brakes.

Reason 3

There's No One Watching — No Consequences

In real life, there are adults around. At school, there's a teacher. At BJJ, there's a coach. At home, there are parents. If you say something rude, someone hears it and there are consequences — you get in trouble, you get a look, you lose a privilege.

Online? There's no teacher in the game chat. No coach watching the group text. No parent reading over your shoulder (most of the time). When people feel like no one is watching and no one will punish them, they do things they'd never do in front of an adult.

Reason 4

The Screen Is a Wall — People Don't Feel "Real"

Here's something weird about the human brain: when you're looking at words on a screen instead of a real person, your brain starts treating the other person like a character in a game rather than a real human being. Dr. Suler called this "solipsistic introjection" — but in simple terms, it means that when you can't see or hear someone, your brain kind of forgets they're a real person with real feelings.[3]

Think about it: you'd never walk up to someone at school and say "you're trash" to their face. But in a game lobby, people type it constantly — because the screen makes the other person feel less real.

Reason 5

The Crowd Makes It Worse — Mob Mentality

Mob mentality is when people in a group start doing things they would never do alone. One person starts being mean in a comment section, and then others pile on. Each person thinks: "Well, everyone else is doing it, so it's fine." Research shows that groups of people tend to become more extreme in whatever direction they were already leaning.[6] So if someone starts being a little negative, the group pushes it into being a lot negative.

Online, anonymity makes this even worse. One study found that when people are anonymous AND there's an aggressive group around them, they use significantly more aggressive language than either factor alone.[7]

How This Affects You

You might be thinking: "Okay, so people are mean online. So what?" Here's the "so what":

The Numbers Are Real

A 2022 survey by the Pew Research Center found that 46% of U.S. teens ages 13–17 have experienced cyberbullying — that's almost half.[8] That includes being called offensive names online (32%), having false rumors spread about them (22%), or being physically threatened (10%).

It Gets Inside Your Head

A major scientific review that combined the results of over 130 studies found that being bullied online is linked to depression, anxiety, lower self-esteem, and feeling worse about your life overall.[9] Mean comments from a stranger you'll never meet can stick in your head for days, even when you know they shouldn't matter. That's not weakness — that's how human brains work.

What To Do About It

Now that you know why people are mean online, you can protect yourself:

  1. Don't feed the trolls. A "troll" is someone who says mean things online just to get a reaction. The more you respond, the more they enjoy it. The best move is to ignore them completely. They want your attention — don't give it to them.
  2. Don't take it personally. That person in the game chat calling you names? They don't know you. They'd say the same thing to literally anyone. It's not about you — it's about them feeling powerful behind a screen.
  3. Talk to someone you trust. If someone says something online that really bothers you or scares you, tell a parent, a coach, or a teacher. That's not being a snitch — that's being smart. Even adults deal with this stuff and ask for help.
  4. Block and report. Every app and game has a way to block people who are being harmful and report them. Use it. You don't owe anyone your attention, especially when they're being terrible.
  5. Remember the "why." Now that you know about the Online Disinhibition Effect, you understand why people act this way. They're hiding behind a screen, they can't see your face, and there's no adult watching. Understanding why takes away a lot of its power over you.
Knowing why people are mean online is a superpower. Most kids — and even most adults — don't understand this. Now you do. Use it to protect yourself and to make sure you never become the person hiding behind a screen to make someone else feel bad.
Lesson 3 of 5

Words Have Weight — Even in Texts

Rules for texting anyone — parents, friends, coaches, or anyone else.

Texts Are Not Invisible

When you say something out loud, the words disappear into the air. But when you type something in a text message, it stays forever. People can take screenshots (a picture of your message). Messages get forwarded to other people. Parents check phones. Teachers can read messages. Nothing you type is ever truly private.

This is the #1 rule: Never type anything you wouldn't say out loud in front of your parents, your teacher, or your coach.

The Middle Finger Emoji

Using the ðŸ–• emoji in a text to your parents — or anyone — is the same as doing it to their face. It's not funny. It's not edgy. It's disrespectful. Emojis carry the same meaning as the real action. Research has shown that negative emojis can shift how an entire message is interpreted — one bad emoji can make an otherwise normal message feel hostile.[10] Just because it's on a screen doesn't make it less real.

The Texting Rules

These apply to texting anyone — parents, friends, teammates, classmates, or people you don't know well:

  1. No curse words. Not even short versions of them (like WTF or letters that stand for bad words). If you wouldn't say it in class, don't type it.
  2. No bad emojis. The middle finger, and any emoji used to be mean or disrespectful, is off limits.
  3. Re-read before you send. Read your message back to yourself. Would you be okay if your teacher read it? If not, delete it and try again.
  4. Reply to your parents. When your parents text you, answer them. Ignoring a parent's text is the same as ignoring them when they talk to you face-to-face.
  5. Don't text when you're mad. If you're upset, put the phone down. Wait until you've calmed down. Angry texts always make things worse.
  6. Sarcasm doesn't work in texts. More on this in Lesson 4, but sarcasm almost always gets misunderstood in texts because the other person can't hear your voice or see your face.

Key Idea

Being respectful doesn't mean you can't disagree or ask for what you want. It means how you say it matters just as much as what you say. You can stand up for yourself without being rude. That's a skill that will help you your whole life — with friends, at school, at work, everywhere.

Lesson 4 of 5

Tone, Sarcasm & How Messages Get Misread

Why what you meant and what they understood are often two different things.

The Comedy Problem

Think about Jim Carrey. He's one of the funniest people alive. But why is he funny? It's not just the words he says — it's his face, his body, his voice. He spent hours as a kid practicing facial expressions in the mirror, and he can move his face in ways that are almost cartoonish. In movies like Ace Ventura, The Mask, and Liar Liar, his comedy works because you can see every exaggerated expression and hear every weird voice.[11]

Or think about Jack Black. He charges around a room like an angry bear, then suddenly does a tiny delicate move with his fingers, then leaps off like a ballerina. His comedy in School of Rock and Nacho Libre is all energy, facial expressions, and physical movement.[12]

Now imagine reading a transcript of their jokes — just the words, printed on paper, with no face and no voice. Would it still be funny? Not even close. Most of the humor would completely disappear. That's exactly what happens when you try to be funny, sarcastic, or playful in a text message.

The Science Behind It

A study at Cornell University found that people who send emails are confident the reader will understand their intended tone about 80% of the time — but the reader actually gets it right only about 50% of the time. That's a coin flip.[13] The reason? When you type a message, you "hear" your own tone in your head as you write it. But the person reading it doesn't hear anything — they just see flat words on a screen.

Another study found that emails are consistently read as more negative than intended.[14] So even when you think you're being friendly or funny, the person reading your text probably thinks you're being colder or meaner than you really are.

Is this person being nice? Or making fun of you? In person, you'd know instantly from their face and voice. In a text — you have no idea.

Quick Tone Traps

❌ These Sound Rude in Text

  • "K" — sounds annoyed
  • "Whatever" — sounds dismissive
  • "Fine." — sounds angry (that period!)
  • Sarcasm — sounds like an insult

✅ Say This Instead

  • "Ok sounds good!"
  • "I'm fine with either option"
  • "That works for me"
  • Just say what you actually mean
The Golden Rule of Texting:
Be more clear and more kind than you think you need to be. Jim Carrey can make "alrighty then" hilarious because you can see his face. You can't do that in a text. A few extra words prevent a lot of problems.
Lesson 5 of 5

What's Coming: Social Media & Why It Matters Now

You're not on social media yet — but the habits you build today will protect you when you are.

What Is Social Media?

Social media includes apps like Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, X (which used to be called Twitter), and others. Most of these apps say you have to be at least 13 years old to make an account — that rule comes from a federal law called COPPA (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act) passed in 1998.[15] You're not old enough yet — and that's actually a good thing. Here's why:

The Research Is Clear

In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General — the top doctor in the country — released an official warning that we "cannot conclude social media is sufficiently safe for children and adolescents."[16] The report found that teens who spend more than 3 hours per day on social media face double the risk of depression and anxiety. In 2024, the Surgeon General called for tobacco-style warning labels on social media platforms.

Research by psychologists Jonathan Haidt and Jean Twenge found that social media use has a consistent negative link to mental health, especially among girls.[17]

Designed to Be Addictive

Social media apps are built by teams of really smart people whose job is to keep you scrolling. The guy who invented infinite scrolling — Aza Raskin — has publicly said he regrets it. He described social media design as "taking behavioral cocaine and just sprinkling it all over your interface."[18] These apps use the same psychological tricks as slot machines: unpredictable rewards that keep you pulling the lever, hoping the next scroll will show you something great.[19]

Why Your Texting Habits Matter Right Now

Right now, you're mostly texting your parents. But in a few years, you'll be in group chats with tons of friends, maybe on social media, and talking to people you don't know that well. The habits you build right now are the same habits you'll carry into all of that.

If you learn now how to be respectful, think before you send, and understand that your words really do affect people — you'll be way ahead of most teenagers who have to learn the hard way.

Things Teens Wish They Knew

Every year, teenagers get in serious trouble for things they posted or texted. A Pew Research survey found that 57% of teens decided not to post something because they were worried it would look bad, and 27% avoided posting because it could hurt their chances at school or a job.[20] Messages that were "just jokes" get screenshotted and sent to parents, teachers, and principals. What you put out there online follows you around — sometimes forever.

The Big Ideas to Remember

Idea 1

The Screenshot Rule

Before you send any message, picture the person you respect the most — your coach, your grandparent, whoever it is — reading that message. If you'd feel embarrassed, don't send it. Remember: anything you send can be screenshotted and shared with anyone.

Idea 2

You Are Not Your Feed

YouTube, TikTok, and Instagram only show you the best moments of other people's lives — like a highlight reel in sports. Research shows that people who compare themselves to these highlight reels end up with lower self-esteem.[21] Facebook's own internal research (leaked in 2021) found that 32% of teen girls said that when they felt bad about their bodies, Instagram made them feel worse.[22] Nobody posts their boring Tuesday. Nobody posts their failures. You're comparing your regular life to their greatest hits.

Idea 3

Real Confidence vs. Fake Confidence

Real confidence comes from being good at things, being kind to people, and knowing who you are. You build it by working hard at BJJ, being a good teammate in soccer and flag football, and treating people with respect. Fake confidence is acting tough online, putting other people down, or copying YouTubers who act like jerks just to get views. Real confidence lasts because you earned it. Fake confidence falls apart the second someone calls you out.

The Bottom Line

Your iPad, your phone, your screens — they're tools. Think of a hammer: it can build a house or break a window. It all depends on the person using it. You're learning to be the kind of person who builds things, not breaks them. That starts with how you talk to people, what you choose to watch, and how you spend your time.
