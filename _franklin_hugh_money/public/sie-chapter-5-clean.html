Municipal Debt

Cities need money. Investors want tax-free income. Municipal bonds make both parties happy—usually.

What You Need to Know
  • Municipal bonds pay tax-exempt interest (federal)
  • GO bonds: backed by taxes, generally safer
  • Revenue bonds: backed by project income, more risk
  • Official statement replaces prospectus
  • Legal opinion should be unqualified
  • Trade OTC, settle T+1, can't be shorted

Why Municipal Bonds Exist

+

The federal government wanted states and cities to build infrastructure without asking Washington for money. The solution? Make municipal bond interest exempt from federal taxes.

This tax break has existed since 1913—the same year income tax began. It's a century-old subsidy delivered through the tax code rather than direct spending.

In 2013, Detroit filed for bankruptcy with $18 billion in debt. Some bondholders received just 13 cents on the dollar. Municipal bonds aren't risk-free.

Who issues municipal debt?

  • States, counties, cities, towns
  • School districts
  • Water and sewer authorities
  • Transportation authorities
  • Special purpose districts

How Municipal Bonds Work

+

Municipal bonds skip federal registration. Instead of a prospectus, they use an official statement—think of it as a simplified disclosure document.

The Legal Opinion

Every municipal bond needs a legal opinion from bond counsel A law firm that verifies the bond is legally valid and tax-exempt . The opinion confirms:

  • Legality: The bonds are legally binding
  • Validity: The issuer has authority to issue
  • Tax status: Interest qualifies for tax exemption
Test tip: Issuers want an "unqualified" legal opinion—meaning unconditional approval. "Qualified" means there are concerns.

The Bond Contract

Contains a bond resolution authorizing the sale and pledging revenue sources for repayment.

General Obligation Bonds

+

GO bonds are backed by the issuer's "full faith, credit, and taxing power"—meaning they can raise taxes to pay bondholders.

Most GO bonds are backed by ad valorem taxes Property taxes based on assessed value (Latin: "according to value") with no limit. The city can raise property taxes as high as needed to pay bondholders.

Variations

  • Limited tax bonds: Cap on tax rate increases
  • Statutory debt limits: Maximum amount of GO debt allowed
  • Capital appreciation bonds: Zero-coupon munis that count less against debt limits
GO bondholders can legally force tax increases in case of default. This makes GO bonds generally the safest type of municipal debt.

Revenue Bonds

+

Revenue bonds are backed by income from specific projects—toll roads, hospitals, stadiums, utilities. No tax increases available if revenues fall short.

Feasibility Studies

Before issuing revenue bonds, consultants analyze whether the project will generate enough income. The study examines:

  • Projected revenues and expenses
  • Construction costs
  • Competition analysis
  • Economic projections

Revenue bonds typically include a trust indenture for investor protection, since there's no taxing power backup.

Revenue bonds carry more risk than GO bonds and typically offer higher yields to compensate.

Special Types & Short-Term Debt

+

Special Municipal Bonds

  • Moral obligation: Promise to pay, not legally binding
  • Double-barreled: Revenue + tax backing
  • Certificates of participation: Lease payments, avoids debt limits
  • Industrial development: For private companies, often taxable

Short-Term Notes

Municipalities issue anticipation notes while waiting for money:

  • TANs: Tax Anticipation Notes
  • RANs: Revenue Anticipation Notes
  • BANs: Bond Anticipation Notes
  • TRANs: Tax and Revenue Anticipation Notes

Trading & Tax Treatment

+

How Munis Trade

  • Over-the-counter (OTC) market
  • Settlement: T+1 (next business day)
  • Mostly buy-and-hold investors
  • Cannot be sold short (too illiquid)

Tax Benefits

Municipal bond interest is exempt from federal tax. If you buy bonds from your own state, often exempt from state and local taxes too—achieving "triple tax-exempt" status.

Tax-equivalent yield example: A 4% muni equals a 6.35% taxable bond for someone in the 37% tax bracket. The higher your tax rate, the more valuable munis become.

Key Points for the Exam

  • Municipal interest is federally tax-exempt
  • GO bonds: taxes, safer / Revenue bonds: projects, riskier
  • Official statement, not prospectus
  • Legal opinion: unqualified = good
  • MSRB regulates dealers, not issuers
  • Settlement T+1, trade OTC
  • Know your notes: TAN, RAN, BAN, TRAN
  • Triple tax-exempt: federal, state, local
